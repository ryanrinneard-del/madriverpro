Practice should be fun. So should the round.

A library of games and drills you can play with friends or by yourself — on the course, on the practice green, in the bunker, on the range. Find one. Try it this week. Beat your score next week.

⛳ On the Course — Solo

Games you can play during a stroke-play round, by yourself or against the course. Most of these change how you score so a bad hole doesn't ruin everything — you keep playing aggressive, smart golf.

Stableford

1+ players Stroke Play Course play

The classic points-based scoring system. Instead of counting strokes, you earn points based on your score relative to par. Bad holes can't kill your round — you just get zero points and move on.

How to score
  • Eagle or better — 4 points
  • Birdie — 3 points
  • Par — 2 points
  • Bogey — 1 point
  • Double or worse — 0 points (pick up!)
Why play it: teaches you to attack, not protect. A 36-point round = a "good day."

Modified Stableford

1+ players Stroke Play Aggressive scoring

The PGA Tour version (used at the Barracuda Championship). Eagles count more, doubles count negative. Pushes you to swing for it on par-5s and play smart on tough holes.

How to score
  • Eagle — +5 points
  • Birdie — +2 points
  • Par — 0 points
  • Bogey — −1 point
  • Double or worse — −3 points

Quota

1+ players Handicap-based Course play

Each player gets a points target based on handicap. Use the same Stableford scoring above — did you hit your quota? Beat it = good round. Miss = practice harder.

Sample quotas
  • 10 handicap → quota of 30
  • 20 handicap → quota of 22
  • 30 handicap → quota of 14

Bingo Bango Bongo

2-4 players Hole-by-hole All-around

Three points up for grabs on every hole. Even higher-handicap players can compete with low handicaps because the points are about position and order, not just score.

The 3 points
  1. Bingo — first ball on the green
  2. Bango — closest to the pin once everyone is on
  3. Bongo — first ball in the cup
Etiquette: always play the player furthest from the pin first — that's how you actually get a chance at Bingo.

Worst Ball

1 player Solo practice round Mental toughness

Hit two balls on every shot. Always play the worse one. A brutal solo training round — expect to shoot 10-20 strokes more than usual. Forces clean execution because every mistake stays.

When to use: a quiet weekday afternoon with no group behind. Builds nerves and consistency fast.

Maximum Score

1+ players USGA-recognized Pace of play

Cap your score on each hole at, say, double bogey. Once you hit the cap, pick up and move on. Keeps the round moving and your head up. The USGA's "Maximum Score" format is exactly this.

🥇 Match Play — 1 v 1

Match Play is hole-by-hole, not stroke-by-stroke. Every hole resets — a triple-bogey on hole 1 only loses you that hole, not the round. Match Play is how you'll play in club championships, tournaments like the Ryder Cup, and most fun bets among friends.

Standard Match Play

2 players Head-to-head Clutch play

Win the hole, lose the hole, halve the hole. Score is reported as "X up" or "all square." If you're 3 up with 2 holes to go, the match is over — "3 & 2".

Key terms
  • Up — how many holes ahead you are
  • All square — tied
  • Dormie — up by exactly the holes remaining
  • "3 & 2" — won 3 up with 2 to play

Skins

2-4 players Hole-by-hole prize Aggressive play

Each hole is worth a "skin." Outright low score wins the skin. Tie? It carries to the next hole. A run of ties can pile up and one big shot wins them all back.

Strategy: when the carryover is big, aggressive play pays. When it's just one skin, smart pars often win.

Nassau

2 players Three matches in one Mental resilience

Three separate matches in one round: front 9, back 9, total 18. Lose the front and you can still win the back and the overall. Most common bet structure on the planet.

Pressing
  • If you fall behind, you can "press" — start a NEW match for the remaining holes at the same stake
  • Multiple presses can stack on the same nine — careful or you'll lose more than you bargained for

Wolf (1 v 1)

2 players Stack-the-deck Decision making

One player is the Wolf each hole, alternating. The Wolf hits first, then watches the other player tee off. If the Wolf likes the opponent's drive, they pair up; if not, the Wolf goes "Lone Wolf" for double points.

🤝 Team Games — On the Course

The fun stuff. Pair up with a friend, a parent, a partner. Some of these let everyone hit every shot; others split it up so the team is genuinely a team. All of them turn the round into something bigger than your own score.

Scramble

2-4 players Best ball, all play Team / fun

The most popular charity-event format. Everyone tees off, pick the best one, everyone plays from there. Repeat until in the cup. Friendly to mixed-skill groups; everyone contributes.

Common rule
  • Each player must contribute at least 3 (or 4) drives over the round — keeps it fair
  • Mark the chosen ball spot — play within one club length, no closer to the hole

Shamble

2-4 players Scramble + own ball Team / individual mix

Scramble off the tee, then everyone plays their own ball from there to the hole. Lowest score on the team counts. Rewards a good drive AND clean approach play.

Best Ball (a.k.a. Four-Ball)

2 teams of 2 Stroke play Team scoring

Each player plays their own ball the whole round. The lower of each team's two scores on each hole is the team's score. No one gives up — either partner can save it.

Foursomes (Alternate Shot)

2 teams of 2 One ball per team Communication

One ball, two players, alternating shots. Player A tees off, Player B hits the second shot, A hits the third, etc. Players also alternate tee shots: A on odd holes, B on even.

Toughest of all formats — a missed shot is on your partner. Communicate everything beforehand.

Greensomes / Chapman

2 teams of 2 Tee both, then alternate Team strategy

Both players tee off. Pick the better drive. Then alternate shots from there to the hole. Less brutal than pure foursomes — you always have a tee shot to fall back on.

Texas Scramble

3-4 players Scramble variation Team strategy

Like a scramble, but a player whose drive is chosen can't have their drive used again until everyone else's has been used. Forces teams to use everybody's strengths, not just the long hitter.

6-6-6 (Three Stooges)

2 teams of 2 Mixed formats All formats in one

An 18-hole round split into three formats: 6 holes Best Ball, 6 holes Scramble, 6 holes Alternate Shot. Tests the whole team. The most fun way to discover what your partnership is good at.

Pinehurst (Modified Chapman)

2 teams of 2 Cross-shot Team strategy

Both players tee off. Each plays their partner's ball as the second shot. Then choose the better second-shot ball. From there, alternate shots to the hole. Sounds wild, plays great.

🏆 Team Match Play

The Ryder Cup formats — and the Solheim Cup, the Walker Cup, the Curtis Cup. These are the games that decide international team golf. They're also what your high-school team will play in matches.

Four-Ball Match Play

2 teams of 2 Match play, lowest of pair Strategy

Each player plays their own ball. The team's score on each hole is their lower of the two. Compare team scores hole by hole — standard match play after that. The fastest, most aggressive team format.

Foursomes Match Play

2 teams of 2 Alternate shot, match Composure under pressure

Same as Foursomes (alternate shot, one ball per team) but scored as match play hole by hole. The hardest team format in golf. One bad shot from your partner means you have to save it.

Skins (Group)

3-4 players Hole-by-hole prize Aggressive play

Each hole is one skin. You must outright win the hole — tie any other player and the skin carries. With 4 players, ties are common, so big carryovers happen often. Drama on every hole.

Wolf (Group)

4 players Rotating partner Decision making

The Wolf rotates each hole. After each player tees off, the Wolf can pick that player as a partner. If the Wolf passes everyone, they go "Lone Wolf" against all three for double points.

Pro move: "Blind Wolf" — you can declare you're going Lone Wolf BEFORE anyone tees off, for triple points. High risk, high reward.

Vegas

2 teams of 2 Combined two-digit score Team play

Combine your team's two scores into a two-digit number: lower score is the tens place, higher is the ones. Team A makes 4 and 5 = 45. Team B makes 4 and 6 = 46. Lower number wins. One bad hole can blow up (a 7 + 5 = 57, not 75). High variance, high fun.

🎯 Putting Games

Putting is the easiest skill to practice with intent because the feedback is instant: it goes in or it doesn't. These games make repetition feel like a contest. Run any of them on the practice green for 20 minutes and your score will move.

The 21 Game (Around the World)

1-2 players Race to 21 Pressure putting

Place tees in a circle around a hole at 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, and 8 feet. Make a putt = +1 point. Miss = lose all your points. First to 21 wins. Putting under pressure with something on the line.

Around the Clock

1 player 12-putt circuit Short-putt consistency

Place 12 tees in a circle around the hole at 3 feet. Make all 12 in a row. Miss = start over. The classic short-putt confidence builder. Most amateurs need 3-5 attempts the first time.

Variation: after you make 12 at 3 feet, move to 4 feet. Then 5. Then 6. Build a short-putt streak that travels.

Lag Ladder (3-6-9-12-15)

1 player Progressive distance Lag control

Place tees at 3, 6, 9, 12, and 15 feet from a hole. Make all five in order. Miss any — start over at 3 feet. Pressure builds with every distance because one miss costs you progress.

The Snake

2-4 players Worst-putter trophy Mental toughness

Whoever three-putts most recently holds the snake. The snake is a small toy or a marker passed around the green. Whoever has the snake at the end of practice (or after 18 holes) buys the snacks. Brutal social pressure. Effective.

Worst Ball Putting

1 player Two-ball drill Lag-and-tap

Hit two putts on every roll. Always play the worse one. Long putt that finishes 4 feet long? Now play the next putt from there. Forces clean lag putting.

Long-Putt Lag Game (30/40/50)

1 player Lag distance Speed control

Three tees at 30, 40, 50 feet from a hole. Three balls each. Goal: finish every putt within a 3-foot circle of the hole. Cuts three-putts more than any other drill.

Closest to the Tee

2-4 players Lag contest Distance touch

Drop a tee on the green 30+ feet from where you're putting. Lag putt to the tee, not a hole. Closest to the tee wins the point. No "in or out" reward — pure distance touch.

Putt-Out Race

2 players Speed round Confidence

Two players, same hole. First to make 5 putts in a row from 5 feet wins. Reset to zero if you miss. Recreates the rhythm of a tournament putting practice round.

🪁 Chipping Games

Chipping practice without a target is just hitting balls. These games give you a target, a score, and a reason to commit to every shot. Spend 20 minutes on one of them and your up-and-down rate will jump.

Up & Down Tournament

1-4 players Score-based Save percentage

10 chips from around the green — mix of lies, distances, lie quality. After each chip, putt out. Score: 2 points for an up-and-down, 1 for two-putt save, 0 for three-putt or worse. 20 = perfect score.

Tic-Tac-Toe Chipping

1+ players Landing zone Trajectory control

Place 9 tees on the green in a 3x3 grid pattern at different distances. Hit a chip that lands in each square. Forces you to vary trajectory and roll-out for different distances.

Four-Club Chipping

1 player Same shot, different clubs Versatility

From one spot off the green, hit the SAME chip with four different clubs — PW, 9-iron, 8-iron, 7-iron. Watch how each one releases. Builds a feel for "which club for which job."

Try it: 30 feet of green to work with, the higher-lofted club lands closer and stops; the lower-lofted releases and runs to the hole.

Random Lies

1+ players Real-world prep Course transfer

Drop balls into 10 random spots around a chipping green — rough, fringe, sand, hardpan, downhill lie, sidehill lie. Play each shot once. No do-overs. Mirrors what the course actually gives you.

Closest to the Hole

2-4 players One-shot contest Shot selection

Pick a chipping spot. Each player hits one chip. Closest to the hole wins the round. Best of 9 rounds (or first to 5). Forces commitment because you only get one swing.

Chip-In Challenge

1+ players Make-a-chip Aim and commitment

Pick a spot. Hit chips until you hole one. Track how many tries it took. Try to beat the count next session. Beats "just chip some balls" because there's a clear goal.

⚡ Short Game Games (50 Yards In)

Pitches and partial wedges — the shots that decide tournaments. Most amateurs guess at these. Tour players have specific yardages and specific shots. These games help you build your own.

Three-Yardage Test

1 player Distance calibration Wedge yardage

Pick three yardages with your most-used wedge: full, three-quarter, half. Hit 5 balls at each. Score: a point for any ball within 3 yards of the target line and the right depth. 15 = perfect.

50-100 Ladder

1 player Progressive distance Wedge ladder

Hit one shot at 50, 60, 70, 80, 90, 100 yards — in order. Each shot must land closer to its target than the previous one missed. Reset on a wild miss. Builds pure distance ladder.

Trouble Shot Series

1+ players Skill rotation Versatility

Hit 5 shots from each: fairway, light rough, deep rough, fairway bunker, hardpan. Score: par or better = 1 point per shot. Total 25 = world-class. 15 = solid recovery skills.

Bunker Confidence Drill

1+ players Sand only Bunker

Drop 10 balls in the practice bunker. Hit until 5 land within 6 feet of the pin. Track how many balls it takes. Best score wins. Removes the fear of bunker shots through repetition.

Phil Mickelson's "Around the World"

1 player 8-station rotation Variety

Set up 8 stations around the green, each different: short pitch, lob, bunker, flop, bump-and-run, downhill chip, uphill chip, uneven lie. Hit one shot from each, putt out. Total score, lowest wins.

🎳 Iron Play Games

Hitting greens in regulation is the single biggest predictor of low scores. These games make iron practice feel like a contest, not a chore. They also reveal which clubs in your bag actually do what you think they do.

Closest to Target

2-4 players Iron contest Shot accuracy

Pick a target on the range — a flag, a yardage marker, a tree. Each player hits one ball. Closest to the target wins. Rotate clubs every round. Best of 7 wins.

The Ladder

1+ players Progressive distance Distance control

Pick three targets at increasing distances. Hit a ball at the closest target. Then the middle. Then the farthest. Each ball must travel further than the last but still hit close to its target. Trains carry distance precision.

Bigger or Smaller

2 players Call-your-shot Shot shaping

Player A hits a shot. Player B has to predict the next shot's flight — bigger draw, smaller fade, higher, lower. Player A executes. If B was right, B gets a point. Switch. First to 10.

The 9-Shot Challenge

1 player Trajectory matrix Shot variety

One club. Hit 9 different shots: low draw, low straight, low fade, mid draw, mid straight, mid fade, high draw, high straight, high fade. Score: a point per shape you hit cleanly. Pros score 9. Most amateurs: 3-4.

Why pros do this: Tiger has talked about it. Real shot variety beats repeating one swing forever.

Iron Quota

1 player Hit-the-zone Carry consistency

Pick a target zone on the range — say, between two flags 10 yards apart at 150 yards. Hit 10 balls with your 7-iron. Goal: 7 of 10 in the zone. Calibrates your stock yardage.

🚀 Driver & Long Game

The driver is the most fun club to hit and the easiest to abuse. Every golfer thinks they need to bomb it. Most need to keep it in play. These games train both — speed when it matters, accuracy always.

Fairway Gates

1+ players Accuracy Fairway-finding

Imagine a 30-yard-wide fairway between two range markers. Hit 10 drivers. Score: 1 point per ball that finds the gate. 7+ = tour-level fairway accuracy. 5 = solid. Below 5 = work to do.

Carry Distance Contest

2-4 players Distance only Speed

Longest carry wins. 5 swings each. Drop the lowest. Best of remaining 4 carries counted. Roll-out doesn't count — carry only. Best with a launch monitor or a flat range with markers.

Two-Ball Driving

1+ players Hit-vs-roll Sequencing

Hit two drivers per "hole." The first ball must be a hard swing — bomb it. The second must be a soft 3/4 swing for placement. Score: 1 point per swing that finishes in the imagined fairway.

Risk-Reward Driving

1+ players Strategic Course management

Imagine the hardest hole at your home course. Pick the safe target line and the aggressive one. Hit 5 drivers. Score: aggressive line + in fairway = 3 points. Safe line + in fairway = 2 points. Out of bounds = −2 points.

9-Tee Driver

1 player Multi-club rotation Off-the-tee versatility

Set up 9 imagined "tee shots" with different demands: tight fairway, wide fairway, short par 4, par 5 to bomb, dogleg left, dogleg right, etc. Pick the right club for each — driver, 3-wood, hybrid, even an iron. Score 1 point per shot in play.

🎲 Mixed Practice & Range Games

Practice doesn't have to be boring. These games turn the range or the practice course into actual rounds — with stakes, scores, and something to beat next time. Use them when motivation is low or you want to make practice feel like play.

King of the Hill

2-4 players Elimination All-around pressure

Pick a target. Each player hits one shot. Worst shot is eliminated. Repeat until one player remains. Reset, repeat. Build pressure into every swing because the worst one's out.

Range "Course"

1 player Imagined round Course transfer

Play 9 imagined holes on the range. Pick a tee shot target, hit it. Pick an approach target based on where you "landed," hit that. Then a chip and putt if you missed the green. Track the score.

This is the single highest-leverage practice for transferring range form to the course. Most amateurs never do it.

3-Shot Cycle

1 player Driver / iron / wedge Rhythm

Hit one driver, one mid-iron, one wedge. Repeat 10 times. Don't hit two of the same club back-to-back. Forces switching focus and prevents the boredom of grooving one swing.

One Ball Round

1 player Solo course Mental discipline

Play 9 holes with only one ball. No mulligans. No second tries. If you lose it, hole's over. Worst-case golf training. Every shot matters because there's no recovery.

The Caddie Game

2 players Decision practice Course strategy

One player is the player. The other is the caddie. The caddie picks every club — including off the tee. Both players have to live with the choices. Switch roles after 9 holes.

Pin-Pointing

1+ players Imagine pins Target focus

Pick a single small target on the range — a flag, a divot, a yardage stake. Hit 5 balls at it. Mark them. Pick a different target. Hit 5 more. Cycle through 6 targets per session. Better target acquisition transfers directly to the course.
