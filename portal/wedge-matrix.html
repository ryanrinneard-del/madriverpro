TOUR PRO KNOW-HOW

Why the pros launch wedges under 30°

The most copyable shot in professional golf

Above 30° of launch angle, a wedge does something the wind loves: it climbs slowly and stays airborne. The longer the ball is in the air, the more a 10 km/h breeze gets to push it around. Watch any tour event in wind — every wedge looks shorter, flatter, and more penetrating than what the weekend player tries to hit.

A wedge that launches at 35°+ balloons. A 10 km/h headwind that steals 6 yards from a flat flight steals 15 or more from a high one — and the ball drops vertically onto the green without the spin or speed to check up. Tailwind's just as ugly: ball lands hot, skips past the hole, runs out 20 feet.

The tour move — call it the knocked-down wedge, the dead-hand flight, the Phil Mickelson punch — lives under 30° of launch. Here's how they build it.

Setup

  • Ball position — centered or one ball back of center. Never forward.
  • Hands forward — shaft leans 5–8° toward the target at address.
  • Stance — slightly open, weight 55% on the lead foot.
  • Grip down — an inch or so for more control, less yardage, better feel.

Motion

  • Backswing — three-quarters max, often half. You're buying control, not power.
  • Tempo — slower, with softer grip pressure. This shot is timing, not effort.
  • Chest covers the ball — sternum stays over (or slightly ahead of) the ball through impact.
  • Shaft leans forward at impact — de-lofts the face, compresses the ball, launches it down.
  • Abbreviated finish — hands stop at chest height. Don't wrap around.

What you get

  • Lower apex — less time exposed to wind.
  • Higher spin-to-launch ratio — the ball still stops quickly even though it's flying flat.
  • Predictable carry — what you practice in still air is what you get in wind.
  • Cuts through headwinds instead of getting shoved sideways and short.
  • Usable in cold weather — cold air is denser and amplifies every flaw in a high shot.

When to deploy it

  • Any wind over 10 km/h — head or tail. Tailwind shots balloon too.
  • Firm, fast greens where a stock wedge would skip and release.
  • Cold rounds below 10°C / 50°F.
  • Tucked back pins where you need the ball to check up short.
PRACTICE CUE

Pick a flag. Aim a foot under it. If your ball reaches the flag, you're already too high — quit the ballooning wedge. Drill the sensation: chest over it, ball knocked down.

Every yard of carry you trade for control in wind, you get back in predictability. That's the trade tour players make on roughly 80% of their wedge shots from 100 yards in. It's the most copyable shot in professional golf — and the fastest way to start scoring like a pro from inside a wedge.
