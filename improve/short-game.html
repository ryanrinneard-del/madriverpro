Putt Like a Pro

Reset your expectations with real numbers, then practice the right things at your handicap level.

Most amateurs drastically overestimate how often tour pros make putts beyond 10 feet. TV coverage shows the makes, never the misses. Here's the actual data from PGA Tour seasons — what they make, where they start three-putting, and what that means for how you should practice.

The 3-putt cliff

Tour pros 3-putt almost never from inside 25 feet. The rate stays under 5% until about 30 feet. From 40 feet the rate climbs to 10%; from 50 feet, 17%; and from 60 feet, 23%. By 90+ feet, even tour pros 3-putt about 41% of the time. The lesson: distance control matters far more than line on long putts.

RESET YOUR EXPECTATIONS

If a 25-handicap "expects" to make a 15-footer because Justin Thomas just did on TV, they're going to be miserable. Tour pros make 1 in 4 from there.

Chip Like a Pro — Low-Point Management

Every great chipper controls one thing: where the club bottoms out. That's it.

A chip is just a small swing where the bottom of the arc has to happen past the ball, every time. Hit the ground before the ball: chunk. Hit the ball without ever reaching the ground: skull. Tour pros bottom out 2-3 inches past the ball, every chip, automatically. Here's how they do it.

The setup that locks low point forward

  • Narrow stance — feet about 8 inches apart, weight 60-70% on lead foot
  • Ball position — middle of stance for a standard chip; back of stance for a low runner; forward for a higher lob
  • Hands ahead — grip pressure 4 out of 10, hands slightly forward of the ball
  • Shaft lean toward target — about 5° of forward lean
  • Choke down — an inch or two for control

The one move that matters

Keep the chest moving toward the target through impact. Not stop, not hang back — moving. If your sternum stops or shifts back, the club bottoms out behind the ball. If your sternum continues toward the target, the club bottoms past the ball. That's the entire technique distilled.

The line drill. Draw a 6-inch line on the ground in chalk or with grass paint. Place a ball on the line. Hit ten chips. Where does your divot start? It should start on or after the line — every time. If most divots start before the line, your chest is hanging back.

Club selection by lie and distance

The simplest system: pick the club that lets you land it on the green and roll it the rest of the way like a putt.

The biggest mental shift

Stop trying to "lift" the ball. The loft on the club is enough. Your only job is to strike down on the ball with the chest moving forward. The face does the lifting. The moment you try to help it up, you scoop, you decelerate, and you skull or chunk it. Trust the loft.

Pitch Like a Pro

A pitch is a chip with bigger ambitions — same low-point rules, more body, and bounce becomes your best friend.

A pitch is the in-between shot — too far for a chip (which is mostly arms and chest), too close for a full wedge. 30 to 70 yards. The shot most amateurs are worst at because they're either chunking it short or rocketing it 90 yards over the green. Here's the framework tour players use.

Three swing lengths, three carry numbers

Don't try to hit a pitch by feel — calibrate three lengths with each wedge and memorize the carry. The Wedge Matrix tool in your portal is built for this. Most tour pros use:

  • Half swing (lead arm to 9 o'clock) — "the easiest distance to repeat"
  • Three-quarter swing (lead arm to 10 o'clock)
  • Full swing (lead arm to 11 o'clock)

With three swing lengths × four wedges, you have twelve calibrated carry numbers covering every yardage from 30 to 110. Now there's no guessing — just match the matrix.

Setup adjustments from a chip

  • Stance widens slightly — about hip width
  • Weight goes 55-60% on lead foot (less than a chip; you need rotation)
  • Ball position centered for a standard pitch
  • Body rotates — the swing is now driven by torso turn, not just arms

Use the bounce

The "bounce" of a wedge is the angle between the leading edge and the trailing edge of the sole. It's there to keep the club from digging. Tour players present bounce to the ground at impact — not the leading edge. Practical move: keep the shaft close to vertical (less forward lean than a chip), let the back edge of the sole graze the turf first, and the club glides through. Players who lean the shaft way forward dig the leading edge in, hit it heavy, and blame the lie.

PRACTICE CUE

Pick a yardage. Hit 5 balls to it. Don't move on until 4 of 5 land within a 5-yard window. Distance control is the whole game on pitches.

The "land number" system

Don't pick a target — pick a landing spot. A 50-yard pitch with a back pin needs to land 35 yards in (if it'll release 15) or 47 yards in (if it'll check). Walk off the land number, aim there, and let the spin/release do the rest. Pros think in landing spots, not flag distances.

How to Make More Putts Inside 10 Feet

The single biggest scoring lever in your game. Three drills that will move the needle in two weeks.

Forget the 30-footers. The PGA Tour's strokes-gained data is unambiguous: the most improvable putting distance for amateurs is 4-10 feet. Tour pros make 56% from 8 ft; the average 15-handicap makes about 30%. Closing that gap is worth 1-2 strokes per round on its own.

What pros do differently inside 10 feet

  • Stable lower body. Hips and legs don't move. Period. Watch a pro from behind on a 5-footer — the only thing moving is shoulders and arms.
  • Eyes directly over the ball. Drop a ball from your nose; it should land on top of the ball you're about to putt. This guarantees an accurate read of the target line.
  • Putter face controls everything. A 1° open face at 6 feet misses the cup. The hands must keep the face square through impact — no flippy release, no manipulation.
  • Confident speed. Pros putt to roll the ball ~12-18 inches past the cup. Not 6 inches (too soft, bumps off line). Not 4 feet past (too much come-back). The Pelz/Stockton "die-it-in" school is largely outdated; the modern game proves a firmer roll holds line.

The three drills that move the needle

Drill 1: Gate drill at 4 ft

Two tees in the ground, just wider than your putter head, 4 feet from the cup. Putt 10 balls through the gate into the cup. You'll feel any face manipulation immediately. Goal: 8 of 10 makes before you leave.

Drill 2: Around-the-world consecutive

Five balls in a circle around the cup, all at 5 feet. Make all five in a row. If you miss, restart. Builds the pressure of "must-make" putting into your nervous system.

Drill 3: Speed-line drill

One ball at 8 feet. Pick a target 12 inches past the cup. Putt with the goal of stopping at that target if you miss. Repeat 10 times. Develops the firmer roll that holds line.

Two weeks, 10 minutes a day. Run all three drills. Track makes. Most players see make percentage from 5-8 ft jump from ~50% to 70%+ in under a month. That's the difference between a 92 and an 88.

AimPoint Express Explained

The green-reading system used by Adam Scott, Justin Rose, and most of the world's best. It takes 30 seconds to learn the basics.

AimPoint Express was developed by Mark Sweeney, a putting coach and data analyst, and it's now the most widely-used green-reading system on the PGA, LPGA, and DP World Tours. The premise is radically simple: your feet feel slope better than your eyes see it, and you can convert that feel into an exact aim point with your fingers. Three steps.

Step 1 — Feel the slope

Walk to a point about halfway between your ball and the hole. Straddle your line — one foot on each side of the imaginary path. Stand shoulder-width apart with your weight evenly distributed. Now, without looking at the ball or the cup, feel which foot is heavier. The heavier foot is on the high side; gravity is pulling you toward the low side. That tells you which way the putt breaks.

Then rate the slope severity on a scale of 1 to 5:

  • 1 — barely perceptible tilt
  • 2 — noticeable but mild
  • 3 — clearly tilted
  • 4 — pronounced slope
  • 5 — severe (hard to stand on naturally)

Step 2 — Hold up your fingers

Walk back behind your ball. Hold your hand up at arm's length, palm facing you, with the number of fingers that match your slope rating (1-5). Hold the hand so the inside edge of your index finger lines up with the cup. The fingers fan out toward the high side of the slope. The outside edge of the last finger is your aim point.

For a 3% slope putt, you're aiming three "finger widths" away from the cup, on the high side. For a 1% slope, just one finger width. The system calibrates aim to slope automatically.

Step 3 — Commit and roll

Now stop reading. Your aim point is the spot you've identified. Set up to send the ball straight at that aim point with confident speed (12-18 inches past the cup if you miss). Gravity does the rest — the ball curves down the slope and back to the hole.

WHY IT WORKS

Eyes get fooled by visual illusions on greens (light, color, surrounding terrain). Feet don't lie — slope is a physical fact. AimPoint converts physics into a repeatable aim.

Common mistakes

  • Looking at the ball or cup while feeling slope. Eyes override feet. Close your eyes if you have to.
  • Holding fingers too close to your face. Arm should be extended fully — the geometry depends on it.
  • Underestimating slope. Most amateurs feel a 3 and call it a 2. Trust your feet; if you feel it, it's there.
  • Bailing out on speed. A perfect read fails if the ball doesn't have the speed to hold the line. Firm roll, every time.
Practice protocol. Spend one putting practice session doing AimPoint reads on every putt. Don't worry about make percentage — focus on whether your AIM was right (did the ball start on the line you picked?). After two sessions of this, the system becomes intuitive.
