The Swing Fundamentals.

How I teach the golf swing — built on the shoulders of John Jacobs, Ben Hogan, Michael Hebron, Jim McLean, and Plummer & Bennett.
Ryan's framework · influences credited throughout
Ryan's Framework

This module is Ryan Rinneard's original teaching framework, synthesized over years of coaching adult golfers. It draws on the published work of multiple golf instructors — they are credited by name throughout and listed in the attribution section at the end. Every source referenced is in print; links to buy their books are provided.

This is not an authorized publication of any named teacher or system. "Stack & Tilt" is a trademark of its owners; the Jim McLean Golf Schools and the Michael Hebron Golf Academy are separate entities from Ryan's coaching practice. Concepts below are described in Ryan's own words with the original work of each teacher cited as the inspiration.

From the coach

I've been teaching golf for a long time, and I've learned from everyone. John Jacobs taught me to read the ball before I touch the player. Ben Hogan taught me the setup is 90 percent of the shot. Michael Hebron taught me that knowing and doing are two different neurological processes and that most practice doesn't actually produce learning. Jim McLean gave me the eight-position checkpoints I still use on every video review. Andy Plummer and Mike Bennett — through Stack & Tilt — made me rethink what “weight shift” actually means, and changed how I teach impact.

This module is the synthesis. Not a book summary — those are in the Teaching Library under their own listings, marked clearly as independent interpretations. This is the way I actually teach. The framework, the sequence, the four fundamentals I come back to in every first lesson with a new student. Eight chapters. Starts with what has to be true at impact. Ends with how I put it all together in the first hour with someone I've never taught before.

Read it straight through. If something rings true, take it to the range this week. If something reads wrong, tell me — I update this framework as my teaching evolves.

— Ryan

Chapter One

What every swing must do at impact

Three non-negotiables. Everything else is style.
›

Before we talk about how to swing, we have to agree on what a swing has to accomplish at the one moment that matters — impact. If you look at the greatest ball-strikers in history, their swings look radically different on the way back and on the way down. Hogan looked nothing like Moe Norman. Rory McIlroy looks nothing like Tiger Woods. Dustin Johnson and Jim Furyk share a planet and very little else in a swing sense.

But at impact, they are remarkably similar. That's not coincidence. At impact, three things are true of every single one of them. Miss any of the three and the ball doesn't go where you want. Produce all three and the ball does — no matter what the swing looked like on the way.

The three non-negotiables at impact.

1. The clubface is close to square to the path. If the face is radically open or closed to the direction the clubhead is travelling, the ball curves a lot. Close enough and it flies straight or with a playable curve.

2. The lead wrist is flat or slightly bowed. Not cupped up under the club. A flat lead wrist is what delivers shaft lean, de-lofts the club for compression, and keeps the face square.

3. The clubhead's low point is at or past the ball. The ball gets struck before the ground is. The club compresses the ball against the turf. This is what produces a penetrating shot with repeatable distance.

Why these three, specifically

Each one controls a different variable of where the ball goes:

  • Face-to-path controls the curve of the shot.
  • Flat lead wrist controls the face angle itself and therefore the starting direction.
  • Low point past the ball controls strike quality — whether the shot is struck solid or fat/thin.

You could list other variables (attack angle, clubhead speed, dynamic loft), but those are either downstream of the three above or are variables of how much rather than whether the shot works. The three above are the essentials.

Where this framing comes from
The concept that impact, not the swing, defines the shot goes back to John Jacobs — who said "the ball tells you everything." The flat-left-wrist imperative was codified by Homer Kelley in The Golfing Machine (1969) as one of his "three imperatives." The emphasis on impact as the destination, rather than any particular swing pattern, is shared across modern teachers including Jim McLean and the TrackMan launch-monitor era.

What this means for how we teach

Everything in this module — the four fundamentals, the 8-position check, the motor-learning principles, the first-lesson sequence — is a set of tools for producing the three impact conditions reliably. That's the only goal.

Different students need different tools to get there. Some are natural rotators who need to work on weight shift; others are natural sliders who need to work on rotation. Some need to be shown video; others need to feel a drill. The path to the three impact conditions is individual. The destination is universal.

“Styles are infinite. Impact is universal. Teach toward impact, not toward a style.”
Common misunderstanding

"If I swing like my favourite tour player, I'll hit it like them."

You won't. Copying a tour player's backswing copies their style, not their impact. Your body is different, your flexibility is different, your sequencing is different. Copy the impact — the flat wrist, the forward low point, the square face — and let your body find its own way there.

Before we can build toward impact, we have to be able to diagnose what the ball is telling us — which is John Jacobs's enduring contribution.

Chapter Two

Reading the ball

John Jacobs's insight. Diagnose before you prescribe.
›

John Jacobs was a British teacher who, more than anyone else, changed how the modern generation of coaches looks at a golf swing. His core insight is captured in four words: "the ball tells you everything." Before Jacobs, golf instruction was largely about style — what the swing looked like. After Jacobs, good instruction became about diagnosis: watch the ball, reverse-engineer what happened at impact, then work backward to the cause.

The key idea. Every ball flight is the signature of a specific impact condition. A pull-slice means the face was open to the path and the path was inside-out. A straight shot left means the face was square to the path but the path went left. Before you change anything in a student's swing, you read the ball to know what happened at impact. Then — and only then — you work backward to find the swing flaw producing that impact.

The ball-flight laws, in plain English

Modern research (especially from the TrackMan launch monitor) has refined Jacobs's original framing, but the core is intact:

  • Starting direction is mostly determined by where the clubface points at impact. Roughly 80 percent face, 20 percent path. If the ball starts left of your target, the face was left of your target at impact.
  • Curve is determined by the gap between face and path. Face open to path = the ball curves right (slice). Face closed to path = ball curves left (hook). Face matched to path = ball goes straight.
  • Strike quality (solid vs. fat vs. thin) is determined by where the low point of the swing is relative to the ball.
Where this comes from
John Jacobs published Practical Golf in 1972, the book that formalized ball-flight-based instruction. He trained Butch Harmon, David Leadbetter, and a generation of the world's top teachers. Every modern instructor, knowingly or not, works in Jacobs's tradition. The specific percentages (80 face / 20 path for starting direction) come from TrackMan's D-Plane research, which refined Jacobs's original approximation that starting direction was 100 percent face.

The nine ball flights

Every shot you can hit falls into one of nine categories, defined by start direction (left / straight / right) and curve (left / straight / right). For a right-handed player:

  • Pull-hook: starts left, curves more left. Face closed to target and path, path is out-to-in.
  • Pull: starts left, goes straight. Face matched to an out-to-in path.
  • Pull-slice: starts left, curves right. Face pointing left at impact but open to an even-more-left path.
  • Hook: starts at target, curves left. Face closed to a neutral path.
  • Straight: starts at target, goes straight. Face matched to a neutral path.
  • Slice: starts at target, curves right. Face open to a neutral path.
  • Push-hook: starts right, curves left. Face closed to an even-more-right path.
  • Push: starts right, goes straight. Face matched to an in-to-out path.
  • Push-slice: starts right, curves more right. Face open to an in-to-out path.

Each pattern is a different impact signature. Each has a different fix. “You hit it left” is not a diagnosis — it's three different problems with three different fixes.

“I don't tell a student what to change until I've watched twenty shots. For the first twenty, I'm just reading the ball. The ball is the diagnosis.”

What this means for self-taught players

If you're working on your swing without a coach, the ball-flight laws are your most important tool. Before you try to fix anything, hit ten balls and write down the pattern. Does every miss curve the same direction? Does the miss start left or right? Is the contact consistently fat, thin, or solid? That pattern is your diagnostic data. You can't fix what you haven't identified.

Common misunderstanding

"I hit it left, so I must be swinging over the top."

Not necessarily. Hitting it left could mean over-the-top (pull), or a closed face (hook), or both (pull-hook). Each has a different fix. Over-the-top needs path work. Closed face needs grip or clubface-control work. Applying the wrong fix makes the problem worse.

Once you know what the ball is telling you, the single biggest lever you have is where the low point of your swing lives.

Chapter Three

The ball-first principle

Where the low point of your swing actually lives — and how to move it.
›

Solid iron contact, in one sentence: the clubhead's low point is past the ball. You hit the ball first, then the ground. You take a divot that starts at or in front of the ball. The club compresses the ball against the turf before the turf is disturbed.

That's it. If you do that, your iron shots fly the way irons are supposed to fly — penetrating trajectory, repeatable distance, clean strike. If you don't do that, you're either hitting the ground first (fat) or picking the ball thin in compensation. Everything in this chapter is about moving the low point to where it needs to be.

The key idea. The clubhead's low point lives roughly beneath the center of your swing — which is approximately your sternum. If your sternum is forward of the ball at impact, the low point is forward of the ball. If your sternum is behind the ball at impact, the low point is behind the ball. The whole of iron-striking boils down to keeping the sternum forward of the ball through the swing.

Why the "shift to the trail side" instruction fails for most amateurs

Traditional golf instruction teaches a two-part weight shift: shift your weight to the trail side during the backswing, shift it back to the lead side during the downswing. This is taught everywhere — magazines, YouTube, junior clinics, most club pros.

For skilled players with strong rotation, it works. For the majority of amateur players, it doesn't — because the "shift back" portion of the sequence fails. They shift back to the trail side beautifully, then never fully shift forward again. Their sternum is still behind the ball at impact. Their low point is behind the ball. The result: fat shots, thin shots, and the compensations that follow (chicken-wing release, scooping at impact, loss of distance).

The corrective framing
Andy Plummer and Mike Bennett, in their 2009 book The Stack & Tilt Swing, formalized an alternative: don't shift back at all. Start with weight forward and keep moving forward through the swing. The observation itself isn't new — Hogan's impact position shows it, Moe Norman's entire swing shows it — but Plummer and Bennett named it, codified it, and built a teaching system around it. Their book is the original source for what follows.

How this feels, in practice

At setup with an iron, roughly 55–60 percent of your lower-body weight is on your lead foot. As you take the club back, that weight doesn't shift to your trail side — it stays forward. At the top of the backswing, you can still feel weight in your lead heel. From the top, weight continues moving forward through impact. At the finish, almost all your weight is on your lead foot and your chest is facing the target.

The key feel: during the backswing, you should feel like you're leaving the ball behind you — as if the ball is slightly behind your center of mass at the top. That's the opposite of the traditional feel (where the ball is “in front of your center” at the top). It will feel wrong at first. Stay with it.

“The weight doesn't go back and then forward. It goes forward, and then more forward.”
Common misunderstanding

"Weight forward means leaning forward over the ball."

No. Weight forward means toward the target. Your weight should still be balanced between the balls of your feet and your heels. Lean over the ball and you fall off balance. Shift weight toward the target and your spine angle stays intact.

Test yourself

Set up with an iron. Have someone film you face-on. Take a full backswing and pause at the top. Did your head move backward? Did your lead foot heel lift off the ground? Did your weight visibly shift onto your trail side? If the answer to any is yes, you're in the traditional pattern — and if you're an amateur struggling with consistent contact, this is almost certainly the first thing to fix. Work on staying centered over the ball at the top. Every other fundamental becomes achievable once this one is in place.

Keeping the sternum forward requires four coordinated moves. Those are the fundamentals.

Chapter Four

The four fundamentals

Weight forward. Shoulder down. Arms straight. Hips tuck. In that order.
›

There are hundreds of things a golf swing can do well or badly. After many years of teaching adult club golfers, I've narrowed it to four fundamentals that produce the highest rate of improvement for the least amount of instruction. They're all in service of the ball-first principle from the last chapter.

The four fundamentals.

1. Weight forward. More weight on your lead side at setup and continuing forward through the swing. Produces ball-first contact.

2. Shoulder down. The lead shoulder moves downward in the backswing, not around or inward. Keeps the head still and the club on plane.

3. Arms straight. Arms stay extended through impact. Preserves the radius of the swing and lets the club square up consistently.

4. Hips tuck. Through impact and into the finish, the pelvis moves forward and up — not rotationally around a fixed center. Delivers the club and produces a held finish.

Weight forward — the foundation

Covered in detail in Chapter 3. Every other fundamental depends on this one. Without weight forward, the shoulder can't go down without producing a reverse pivot. Without weight forward, the arms must bend to compensate for a sternum behind the ball. Without weight forward, the hips can't tuck — they can only rotate around a mis-positioned center.

This is why weight forward is always first. Not because it's the most important move — they're all important — but because every other move becomes possible only when this one is in place.

Primary influence
Plummer and Bennett's Stack & Tilt codified weight forward as a teaching principle. Their book is the reference. Also borrowed: TPI's physical-screen work, which shows that players with limited pelvic mobility physically cannot shift weight to the trail side and back without losing posture — meaning weight-forward is not just optional for them, it's the only pattern their body can execute reliably.

Shoulder down — the backswing move

The lead shoulder moves downward in the backswing, not around the body. This is counterintuitive for most amateurs, who've been taught to "turn your shoulders" (which usually produces a flat, baseball-style rotation). The shoulder-down pattern keeps the head still during the backswing and tilts the shoulder plane toward the ball, which in turn produces a more in-to-out club path on the way down.

The geometric reason it works: if your shoulders rotate on a flat plane, one shoulder has to pass through the space your head occupies. Your body's only options are to move the head out of the way (sway) or pivot backward (reverse pivot). Both break your center. But if your lead shoulder goes down, neither shoulder passes through the head's space — the body can turn a full 90 degrees while the head stays put.

Primary influence
Plummer and Bennett's work, again. Also connects to TPI's research on spine-angle maintenance in the backswing.

Arms straight — the radius preserver

Arms stay extended through impact and into the finish. Not locked — extended. The radius from your lead shoulder to the clubhead stays constant. The moment your lead arm folds at the elbow through impact (the "chicken wing"), the radius shortens, the clubhead comes up off plane, and contact becomes random.

The chicken wing is almost always a rescue move — a subconscious compensation for a sternum that's out of position. Fix the weight and the chicken wing usually fixes itself. That's why arms-straight is the fourth fundamental, not the first.

Primary influence
Ben Hogan's Five Lessons introduces the "pane of glass" concept — a visual of the swing plane tilted so the arms stay extended on it. It's his way of describing radius preservation. The specific term "chicken wing" is modern shorthand used by many instructors for the opposite.

Hips tuck — the delivery and the finish

Through impact and into the finish, the pelvis moves laterally (toward the target) and upward (standing up into the post leg), rather than rotationally around a fixed center. The motion is a tuck — the pelvis moves forward and up, the lead leg straightens, and the body finishes vertical. The test: can you hold your finish position for three seconds without stumbling? Yes means the hips tucked correctly. No means they didn't.

Primary influence
The hip-tuck pattern is most clearly articulated in Plummer and Bennett's Stack & Tilt. The biomechanical reason it works — vertical ground-reaction force from the post leg contributing to clubhead speed — is grounded in TPI's research and in modern launch-monitor data.

The order matters

These four fundamentals are listed in teaching order, not importance order. They all matter. But you install them in the order above, because each one depends on the previous. Try to fix the chicken wing without fixing weight forward and you'll chase your tail for years. Fix weight forward first and the arms often straighten out without direct intervention.

Common misunderstanding

"I'll work on all four at once so I improve faster."

You won't. The human nervous system can only install one motor pattern at a time. Work on weight forward for a month. When it's reflexive, add shoulder down. Two at a time is already pushing the brain's learning capacity — we'll cover why in Chapter 6.

To know whether the fundamentals are actually installed, you need checkpoints — which is where Jim McLean's work matters most.

Chapter Five

The 8-position check

Using McLean's framework as a diagnostic tool. Checkpoints, not prescriptions.
›

Jim McLean's great contribution to modern golf instruction was not a swing method — it was a diagnostic framework. He broke the swing into eight positions and catalogued what tour players tend to do at each. He called his book The 8-Step Swing (1994), but the title is slightly misleading: he didn't teach eight steps. He taught eight checkpoints for diagnosing where any swing is working or breaking down.

The key idea. Pause the swing at each of eight positions and you can see exactly where a swing is going wrong. The positions themselves are not prescriptions — they're checkpoints. McLean's framework is the vocabulary modern coaches use to talk about where in the swing a problem actually lives.

The eight positions

  1. Address. How you stand to the ball. Grip, stance, posture, ball position.
  2. Takeaway. The first few feet of the club moving back from address.
  3. Halfway back. The club parallel to the ground on the way up.
  4. Top. The end of the backswing. Where the club is pointing, where the body is.
  5. Transition. The moment the swing changes direction — the first move down.
  6. Halfway down. The club parallel to the ground on the way down.
  7. Impact. The moment of contact.
  8. Finish. The end of the follow-through.
Where this comes from
Jim McLean's The 8-Step Swing (1994). McLean is one of the most-published instructors in modern golf and a long-time Golf Digest Top-Teacher. His eight-position framework is used, implicitly or explicitly, in most video-based instruction today. The book is still in print; an independent summary is available in the Teaching Library as a separate primer.

Corridors of success

McLean's key insight is that each position has a range of acceptable values, not a single correct one. He called these "corridors of success." Tour players don't all look identical at Position 4 (the top of the backswing) — Furyk's top is radically different from Fowler's — but all of them fall within a corridor, a range of positions that produce playable shots. Outside the corridor, compensations become necessary; inside the corridor, the swing can largely self-regulate.

The three positions that matter most for most amateurs

You don't have to master all eight positions to play good golf. In my experience teaching club golfers, three positions do most of the work:

  • Position 4 — the top. Is your head still? Did your lead heel stay down? Are your shoulders fully turned? This is where most “weight shift” flaws show up on video.
  • Position 7 — impact. Is your lead wrist flat? Is your sternum forward of the ball? Is your weight on your lead foot? This is the one that actually determines the shot.
  • Position 8 — finish. Can you hold it for three seconds without stumbling? This is your retrospective test: a balanced finish proves the preceding seven positions held together.

If you film yourself and those three positions look right, the other five will usually be close. If they don't, you've found your project for the month.

“The 8 positions aren't a checklist you execute during a swing. They're a map you use after the swing — when you freeze the video and figure out where the thing actually broke down.”
Common misunderstanding

"I'm going to hit positions 1 through 8 during my swing."

That's not how the brain works during a golf swing. The swing is too fast for conscious position-checking. McLean's framework is a diagnostic tool — for review after the swing — not a prescriptive one during the swing. During the swing, you focus on ONE feel (covered in Chapter 6 on motor learning). The 8 positions are for reviewing, not for executing.

Reviewing your positions tells you what to fix. Installing the fix — actually getting the brain to learn a new pattern — is a different discipline. That's Michael Hebron's territory.

Chapter Six

How you actually install a swing

Michael Hebron on motor learning. Why most practice doesn't produce change.
›

Here's an uncomfortable truth about golf instruction: most of it doesn't work. Not because the instruction itself is wrong — most modern golf instruction is solid. It's because what we call "practice" mostly violates how the human nervous system actually learns motor skills. Michael Hebron's great contribution to golf coaching was translating motor-learning research into teaching practice.

The key idea. Learning a motor skill is a neurological process with rules. Most amateur practice breaks those rules. Understanding the rules — massed vs. distributed, block vs. random, feel vs. real — turns a range session from a workout into a learning session. Same hour on the range. Very different outcome.

Massed vs. distributed practice

Massed practice is what most amateurs do: hit 100 balls in 30 minutes, once a week. Minimal rest between reps. Distributed practice is the opposite: 20 balls at a time, four times a week, spaced out.

The research is unambiguous: for short-term performance, massed practice looks better — you get warmed up, you find a groove, your last 20 balls of a 100-ball session are often your best. But for long-term retention, distributed wins by a wide margin. The skill sticks. A week later, you still have it. After a massed session, you mostly don't.

Most amateurs practice massed and wonder why their range-session improvements don't hold up on the course. The practice was visible; the learning wasn't.

Block vs. random practice

Block practice: hit 20 straight 7-irons, then 20 straight 8-irons, then 20 straight 9-irons. Random practice: alternate clubs every shot — 7-iron, pitching wedge, driver, 6-iron, lob wedge, back to 7-iron, and so on.

Same asymmetry as before: block practice produces better immediate results (you groove each club), random produces better transfer to the course. On the course, you never hit the same club twice in a row. Random practice trains the brain to adapt. Block practice trains the brain to execute a single task it won't face again under those conditions.

Most amateurs block-practice their whole lives and can't understand why the ball-striking that felt great on the range evaporates on the first tee. It's not a pressure problem. It's a practice problem.

Where this comes from
Michael Hebron — a Long Island teacher and PGA of America Teacher of the Year — translated decades of motor-learning research (much of it originating with sports scientists like Richard Schmidt and Gabriele Wulf) into golf teaching. His books See and Feel the Inside Move the Outside and The Art of Teaching Golf are written for coaches but are accessible to committed amateurs. A dedicated Hebron primer may appear in the Teaching Library later.

Feel vs. real

The single most important concept in Hebron's work, and in most good motor-learning research: what a swing feels like and what it actually looks like are often very different.

A student trying to feel "weight forward" for the first time will often just stand more centrally — because their old pattern was so weight-back that the new feel of "forward" is actually just "not-as-back." The feel says “way forward.” The video says “neutral.” The gap between feel and real is where most lessons don't stick.

The fix: film every session. Use video as the reality check against the feel. When you can reconcile the feel with the video — when you can feel “way forward” and the video confirms you're actually forward — the pattern is installed.

What this means for your practice

If you want your practice to produce lasting improvement rather than warm-up euphoria, use these rules:

  1. Spread your reps across the week. 15-20 balls four times a week beats 80 balls once a week.
  2. Change clubs every shot. Mirror what happens on the course.
  3. Film everything. Use the video to check whether what you feel and what you see are converging.
  4. Work on ONE thing per session. Not a checklist. One feel, one fundamental. The brain can only install one pattern at a time.
  5. Practice the uncomfortable pattern. If the old pattern feels good and the new pattern feels weird, keep practicing the new one until it feels normal. That's learning.
“Practice is what you do. Learning is what sticks. They're not the same thing. You can practice for twenty years without learning anything. Most amateur golfers do.”
Common misunderstanding

"I hit the ball well at the range last week, so I know the swing change is installed."

Range performance at the end of a massed practice session is not evidence of learning. Evidence of learning is: can you take the same swing to the course next weekend and still have it? If yes, it's installed. If no — and for most amateurs, the answer is no — you've been practicing, not learning.

All of the above sits on a foundation Ben Hogan laid in 1957.

Chapter Seven

Hogan's modern fundamentals

Why a 1957 book is still the most-cited text in golf instruction.
›

Ben Hogan's Five Lessons: The Modern Fundamentals of Golf came out in 1957. Almost seventy years later, it remains the single most-cited book in modern golf instruction. Every good coach I've ever met has a dog-eared copy somewhere. That's not historical accident. Hogan identified what the body had to do in the setup and in the first two moves of the swing — and the physics of those moves haven't changed since the book came out.

The key idea. Hogan broke the modern swing into five lessons: the grip, the stance and posture, the first part of the swing (through waist-high back), the second part (from waist-high back to the top), and the actual impact/finish. Every modern instructor, whether they cite Hogan or not, is teaching some version of his five-part breakdown. Understanding Hogan's frame is understanding the foundation the entire modern teaching tradition sits on.

The grip

Hogan's opening lesson. He considered the grip the most important piece of the swing, because the hands are the only connection between the body and the club. Two basics carry forward to today: the hands work as a unit (not independently) and the grip pressure is light enough to keep the wrists free but firm enough to prevent the club from twisting in the hand at impact.

Modern biomechanics confirmed what Hogan felt. A grip that's too tight produces tension all the way up the arms, which slows clubhead speed and disrupts timing. A grip that's too loose produces clubface rotation at impact that no compensation can fix.

Where this comes from
Ben Hogan's Five Lessons: The Modern Fundamentals of Golf, co-written with Herbert Warren Wind, published 1957. The book was originally serialized in Sports Illustrated as a five-part series. It is still in print; the current edition is widely available, and any amateur serious about the fundamentals should own a copy.

Stance, posture, and balance

Hogan's second lesson was about how you stand to the ball. Stance width, ball position, knee flex, spine tilt, weight distribution. Every element is in service of balance — the theme that runs through all five lessons. Without balance at setup, nothing downstream of it can work reliably.

Modern teaching has refined Hogan's specific angles (his spine-tilt recommendations are slightly steeper than most modern instruction) but the principle is unchanged: a setup that doesn't produce balance can't produce a reliable swing.

The waggle and the first move back

Hogan's third lesson covers the waggle (a pre-swing rehearsal motion) and the first part of the backswing. The waggle is under-taught today. Hogan considered it essential — a neurological bridge between the static address position and the dynamic swing. It's also a rhythm primer. Most tour players waggle; most amateurs don't.

The first move back, in Hogan's framing, is a one-piece motion: the shoulders, arms, and club move together. Not the hands alone, not the body alone. He wrote about it in passive voice — "the club is taken back" — which is deliberate. The hands don't initiate; the body initiates, and the hands follow.

The first move down

Hogan's fifth lesson addresses transition and impact. The first move down, he wrote, is initiated by the lower body, not the arms and not the shoulders. Hips fire first, torso follows, arms follow, club follows. Before the term "kinematic sequence" existed, Hogan was teaching it. Every launch monitor today confirms what he felt: the downswing must be initiated from the ground up, or clubhead speed leaks out and impact becomes inconsistent.

“Hogan wrote about the swing before the measurement tools existed to verify what he felt. When the tools finally arrived — launch monitors, motion capture, force plates — they confirmed almost everything he'd said. He was right. He just couldn't prove it at the time.”

Why Hogan still holds

Three reasons. First, the physics he described are the physics of an object being swung around a fixed point — and those physics haven't changed. Second, his writing is unusually precise for a pre-biomechanics-era teacher. He describes what you should feel, not just what you should do, and those feels map remarkably well to what modern research shows is actually happening. Third, Hogan himself was one of the greatest ball strikers in history. He had earned the right to describe it.

Common misunderstanding

"Hogan's book is old and the instruction is outdated."

Parts of it (the specific degree-measurements, the swing-plane description) have been refined by modern data. But the structural framework — grip, stance, first move back, first move down — is still the frame every modern teacher works from. Read the book with the understanding that the big ideas hold; the small numbers have been improved.

Seven chapters in, we've got the theory. The final chapter is how I put it all together in the first hour with a new student.

Chapter Eight

The first-lesson sequence

How I put all of it together in the first hour with a new student.
›

I've taught a lot of first lessons. What I've learned from running them is that the first hour is not about teaching all of the above. It's about teaching one thing, deeply, in a sequence that produces a noticeable improvement the student can take to the course tomorrow. The framework below is the sequence I use. It works for new students of every age, every handicap, every background.

The first-lesson sequence.

1. Read the ball. Twenty shots of diagnosis before I touch a thing.

2. Find the low point. Where is the clubhead bottoming out relative to the ball? That's usually project number one.

3. Identify the fundamental most broken. Of the four — weight forward, shoulder down, arms straight, hips tuck — which is producing the ball-flight issue?

4. Install one feel. One. Not a checklist. Use a drill to get the feel into the body.

5. Confirm with video. Before the student leaves, film a swing. Verify that what felt different actually IS different.

Step 1: Read the ball

For the first 20 shots of any first lesson, I say almost nothing. I watch the ball. What does the pattern look like? Does every miss go left? Right? Fat? Thin? Is there a shape to the dispersion or is it random? Without this data, no prescription is useful.

Students sometimes get anxious during the first twenty minutes — they want me to start teaching. I tell them: if I start teaching before I've finished diagnosing, I'll teach the wrong thing, and we'll both waste the hour. Reading the ball is working. Be patient.

Step 2: Find the low point

After the initial diagnostic, I look at where the clubhead is bottoming out. Fat shots = low point before the ball. Thin shots = low point before the ball with a compensation. Chunked iron shots = low point way before the ball. For most struggling amateurs, the low point is project number one, because fixing it fixes at least three different ball-flight problems simultaneously.

A simple test: I put a tee in the ground a couple inches in front of the ball. If you take the ball and the tee out in the same divot, your low point is past the ball. If you take the ball but not the tee, your low point is at or behind the ball.

Step 3: Identify the fundamental most broken

Given the ball-flight pattern and the low-point data, which of the four fundamentals is producing the issue? Usually one, occasionally two. Rarely all four. In my experience:

  • Weight forward is broken in about 70 percent of new adult students I see.
  • Shoulder down is broken in about 40 percent — often as a downstream consequence of weight-back.
  • Arms straight is broken in about 30 percent, almost always as a rescue move for a sternum-back backswing.
  • Hips tuck is broken in about 50 percent — visible in the finish, which is why finish-holding is a useful diagnostic.

The numbers don't sum to 100 because students often have more than one. But usually one is dominant, and that's where the lesson focuses.

Step 4: Install one feel

Pick the dominant broken fundamental. Find a drill or a feel that installs the correction. Give the student ONE cue to take away. Not five. Not three. One.

This is where the motor-learning principles from Chapter 6 matter most. The brain can install one pattern at a time. Overloading the student with a checklist produces zero learning — they can't hold five things in working memory during a swing. They can hold one.

My named drills — The Wrench for the hip tuck, The Bowler for transition, 90-90-90 as a position check — each target one feel. In a first lesson I'll pick the one drill that matches the broken fundamental and run it for most of the hour.

Step 5: Confirm with video

Before the student leaves, we film a swing on their phone. We look at it together. What felt different? What actually looks different on the video? The goal is that the feel and the visual are now aligned — the student feels weight forward AND the video confirms they're actually weight forward. When those two things match, the pattern is installed and the student can go home and practice it productively.

If they don't match — if the feel is way forward but the video still shows weight back — that's the assignment for the next two weeks. Keep practicing until the feel and the video agree. Then we progress.

“A great first lesson produces a concrete improvement the student can own. Not a list of five things to work on. One feel, one cue, one drill. Everything else is ego and noise.”

Between lessons

I send every student home with a practice plan built on the motor-learning principles from Chapter 6: distributed (several short sessions, not one long one), random (change clubs every shot), and filmed (use your phone, check the feel against the video). Fifteen to twenty balls, four times a week, working on one thing. That pattern produces more improvement in a month than a hundred-ball massed session on Saturday morning will produce in a year.

If you're your own coach

Take the five-step sequence and run it on yourself. Record a session with 20 shots. Read the ball — what's the pattern? Look at the divots — where's the low point? Identify which of the four fundamentals is most likely producing the pattern. Pick ONE fundamental to work on. Install one feel. Film yourself at the next session and check whether what you felt changed actually changed. That's a solid self-coaching loop. It's slower than working with a coach but it's real learning.

What to do with this.

The framework above is only useful when you try it. In order from highest-leverage to lowest:

  1. This week: Film yourself face-on with a 7-iron. Hit twenty shots. Read the pattern — what's the ball flight telling you? That's your project for the month.
  2. Next session: Check your low point. Put a tee in the ground in front of the ball; are you taking the tee with the divot? If not, weight forward is project one.
  3. Next month: Pick ONE fundamental — the most broken one — and work on it exclusively. Ignore the other three. Use the motor-learning rules: distributed, random, filmed.
  4. Next lesson: Bring your video and your data. Book a session with me, or with any good coach, and skip the diagnostic phase — show up with your own diagnosis.
  5. Eventually: Once one fundamental is installed, add the next. One at a time. Over a season, all four become part of you.

Sources & further reading

This module is Ryan Rinneard's framework. The ideas that shaped it come from a small list of teachers whose work is foundational to modern golf instruction. None of the books below can be replaced by this module; every one of them rewards reading in full.

  • John Jacobs — Practical Golf (1972). The book that made ball-flight diagnosis the starting point of modern instruction.
  • Ben Hogan — Five Lessons: The Modern Fundamentals of Golf (1957). With Herbert Warren Wind. The foundational text of modern golf instruction.
  • Michael Hebron — See and Feel the Inside Move the Outside, The Art of Teaching Golf. Motor-learning research translated into golf coaching.
  • Jim McLean — The 8-Step Swing (1994). The eight-position diagnostic framework used by most modern coaches, explicitly or implicitly.
  • Andy Plummer & Mike Bennett — The Stack & Tilt Swing (2009). Weight forward, codified. The four fundamentals in this module draw significantly on their work.

Independent summaries of several of these books are available in the Teaching Library as separate primers, clearly marked as independent interpretations. Each includes a direct link to buy the original. This module is not a substitute for any of them.
