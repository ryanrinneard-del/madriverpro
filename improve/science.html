Gear Effect & Off-Center Strikes

Why your driver fades when you strike the heel and draws when you strike the toe — even with a square face.

Here's a counter-intuitive truth: a perfectly square clubface can produce a slice — if you strike the ball on the heel of the driver. Same face, opposite contact, opposite curve. The reason is gear effect, and once you understand it, you'll look at your driver face wear pattern very differently.

What gear effect is, in plain English

Imagine two gears meshing together. When one rotates clockwise, the other rotates counter-clockwise. That's exactly what happens between the clubhead and the ball during an off-center strike on a driver (or any club with a curved face — drivers, fairway woods, hybrids).

A heel strike rotates the clubhead clockwise (face opens through impact) because the ball is on the wrong side of the center of gravity. The ball, like a meshed gear, spins counter-clockwise — left-to-right sidespin. Banana slice. Even with a square face at impact.

A toe strike does the opposite: clubhead rotates closed, ball spins right-to-left, you get a draw or hook. Many "natural draws" amateurs love are actually slight toe strikes hiding inconsistent face control.

Why it matters for your driver setup

Modern drivers (since ~2010) are deliberately designed with a vertical roll on the face that uses gear effect to your benefit. Strike high on the face: lower spin, longer ball. Strike low: higher spin, shorter ball. This is why pros consistently tee the ball high — they're trying to strike high on the face.

YOUR FACE TELLS THE STORY

Spray your driver face with foot powder or use impact tape. Hit 10 balls. The wear pattern shows you exactly where you're striking — and explains your typical miss before any swing analysis.

Practical fixes by strike pattern

  • Heel strikes — Stand a quarter-inch farther from the ball. Tee it slightly forward. Feel the toe of the club exit toward the target.
  • Toe strikes — Stand a touch closer. Make sure your weight isn't shifting onto your toes during the swing.
  • Low strikes — Tee the ball higher. Make sure your hips aren't sliding forward (early extension) — that drops the strike point.
  • Inconsistent — Width of stance and ball position are usually the culprits. Get checked.
Irons are different. Gear effect is mostly a driver/fairway/hybrid phenomenon because of the bulge and roll on the face. Irons have flatter faces — strike issues there mostly affect distance and direction proportionally, without the gear-effect curve flip.

How the Wind Affects Your Golf Shots

From driver to wedge, here's what wind actually does to a golf ball — and the rules pros use to play it.

Wind is the most under-respected variable in amateur golf. The average player notes the flag, says "looks like a club more," and grabs a 7-iron instead of an 8. Tour pros know the math is more interesting — and unforgiving — than that.

The fundamental rule

Wind affects a ball proportional to how long it's in the air. A 200-yard 6-iron is in the air twice as long as a 100-yard wedge — the wind has twice as long to push it. A high, towering shot stays airborne longer than a penetrating one and gets affected more. This is why pros knock down their irons in wind: less air time, less wind influence.

Specific effects by shot type

Driver

A 10 km/h headwind costs roughly 8-12 yards of carry on a 270-yard drive. Tailwind helps about half as much (4-6 yards). Crosswind creates curve — a 15 km/h crosswind can move a normally-straight drive 8-10 yards offline.

Mid-irons (7-iron range)

About 5-8 yards of carry difference per 10 km/h of wind, depending on launch angle. Knockdown shots (lower launch) reduce wind impact noticeably.

Wedges

Roughly 0.6 yards short per 1 km/h of headwind, 0.3 yards farther per 1 km/h of tailwind — assuming a sub-30° launch. High wedges balloon dramatically; their wind effect can double. (See the Wedge Matrix tool in your portal — it has a built-in wind calculator.)

The pro rules of thumb

  • "Swing easy when it's breezy." A harder swing creates more spin, which makes the ball balloon higher, which gets killed by wind. Take more club, swing smoother.
  • "Knock it down when you need to." Ball back, hands forward, three-quarter swing — kills launch and spin, cuts through wind.
  • "Tailwind is harder than headwind." Sounds wrong, but a tailwind kills your spin AND adds yards — combined effect makes distance control much harder. Take less club than the wind suggests, land short, let it run.
  • "Crosswind aims through, not into." Aim the starting line of your ball at the spot the wind is going to push it from — let the wind move it back to the target. Don't fight it.

Cold air = denser air

Cold air is roughly 4% denser than warm air at typical golf temperatures. A ball traveling through denser air loses speed faster, climbs less, and is affected by wind more. Add a half-club for every 10°C below 20°C / 50°F. A 150-yard 8-iron in summer is a 150-yard 7-iron at 5°C in October.

Altitude works the other way. A ball at 5,000 feet of elevation flies about 5-7% farther than at sea level (less air resistance). If you go play in Calgary or Denver, take a club less than usual and watch the ball fly forever.

The Kinematic Sequence Explained

The single most important concept in golf biomechanics — and the reason most amateurs lose 20 mph of clubhead speed.

The kinematic sequence is the order in which body segments accelerate and decelerate during the downswing. The Titleist Performance Institute (TPI) calls it "probably the number one most important piece of information" in their assessment of a new golfer. Every tour pro — every single one — has the same sequence. Most amateurs have the opposite. Closing that gap is how you find the speed and consistency you've been missing.

The proper sequence (from the ground up)

  1. Pelvis (lower body) fires first. Accelerates and reaches its peak speed, then decelerates rapidly.
  2. Trunk (torso) fires next. Accelerates to a higher peak speed than the pelvis, then decelerates.
  3. Lead arm follows. Accelerates higher still, then decelerates.
  4. Club last. Reaches its maximum speed at impact, transferring all the accumulated energy into the ball.

Each segment peaks faster and later than the segment before it. This is sometimes called "kinematic stacking" — energy gets passed up the chain like a whip. When one segment decelerates, it transfers angular momentum to the next. The result: maximum clubhead speed with minimum effort.

THE WHIP ANALOGY

Crack a whip and the handle moves slowly while the tip breaks the sound barrier. That's the kinematic sequence. The hands and club are the tip — they go fast because everything before them transferred energy properly.

What amateurs do (the "top-down" sequence)

Most amateur golfers reverse the sequence. Their shoulders or hands fire first — sometimes called "hitting from the top" or "casting." When that happens:

  • The hips lag behind, never building any momentum to transfer
  • The torso has to spin hard to catch up, often opening way too early
  • The club gets stuck behind the body or thrown out wide ("over the top")
  • Clubhead speed at impact is dramatically lower than the body's speed could provide

This is the root cause of the over-the-top slice, the cast/early release that kills compression, and the "all arms" feeling that wears out your shoulders by the back nine.

How to feel the right sequence

The hardest part of fixing this is that it doesn't feel like firing the lower body. It feels like being patient at the top, then letting your legs and pressure shift to the lead foot before your arms do anything.

Drill: the step-through drill

Take a 7-iron. Set up. Take the club to the top of your backswing slowly. Now, before starting the downswing, take a small step toward the target with your lead foot. Then complete the swing. The step initiates the lower-body fire and forces the sequence to start from the ground up. After a dozen reps, try it without the step — the feel should remain.

Drill: the pause-at-the-top drill

Swing to the top, pause for a full second, then start the downswing by rotating your lead hip toward the target. The pause kills the urge to fire the upper body first. Slow, deliberate, ground-up.

Get measured. A 3D motion capture session (TPI-certified facility, K-Vest, or similar) shows you your sequence in numbers. You'll see exactly where the chain breaks. Worth doing once, even just for the eye-opener.

How TPI Screens Help Your Game

A 30-minute physical assessment that tells you which swing flaws are actually a body problem — and what to do about it.

The Titleist Performance Institute is the global standard for golf-specific physical assessment. A TPI screen — performed by a certified TPI Level 2 or 3 professional — is a series of physical tests that identify movement limitations in your body that are forcing your swing to compensate. The premise: you can't put your body in positions it physically cannot reach.

What gets tested

The standard TPI Level 1 screen has 16 tests covering:

  • Mobility — hip rotation, thoracic spine rotation, shoulder external rotation, ankle dorsiflexion
  • Stability — single-leg balance, deep squat, pelvic tilt control
  • Strength & Power — overhead deep squat, bridge with leg extension
  • Posture — standing posture, neutral pelvic position
  • Coordination — pelvic disassociation (can you move your hips while keeping your shoulders still?)

What the results tell you

TPI's massive research database has linked specific physical limitations to specific swing characteristics. A few examples:

  • Limited hip internal rotation → early extension (standing up out of posture)
  • Limited thoracic spine mobility → loss of posture, over-the-top swing
  • Poor pelvic disassociation → reverse kinematic sequence (upper body fires first)
  • Tight ankles → balance issues, weight stuck on heels
  • Weak glutes → can't transfer pressure to lead foot, hangs back

You can chase these symptoms in lessons forever, but if the underlying body limitation isn't addressed, the swing flaw keeps coming back. TPI gives you the diagnosis.

What you do with the results

Each limitation gets a corrective exercise program — usually 5 to 10 minutes a day of mobility work and stability drills targeting the specific issue. Most golfers see meaningful changes in 4-6 weeks of consistent work. The exercises aren't punishing; they're targeted.

For Junior Elite athletes, TPI screening is mandatory at intake — it shapes the individual training plan for the whole year. For adult clients, it's optional but highly recommended, especially if you have nagging swing flaws that resist instruction.

THE TPI PHILOSOPHY

"There is no one way to swing a club, but there is one efficient way for every player to swing a club." A TPI screen helps find your efficient way.

Booking a screen

Find a TPI-certified pro at mytpi.com (the "Find an Expert" tool). A Level 1 screen takes 30-45 minutes and typically costs $75-150. It's the best money in golf. Bring the report to your next lesson — your coach can use it immediately to reshape what you work on.
