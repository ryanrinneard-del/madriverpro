Practice Like Rory McIlroy

Rory's range sessions are studied because they're efficient, deliberate, and target-driven. You can copy the structure tomorrow.

Rory McIlroy's practice is famous for one reason: every ball has a purpose. He doesn't beat balls. He doesn't chase results. He runs structured blocks with specific goals, then moves on. Here's the actual structure he uses, broken down so you can adapt it to a 60-minute amateur session.

The Rory framework

In an off-week, McIlroy runs about 4–5 hours of deliberate practice — split into a morning technical block on the range and an afternoon short-game / on-course block. The key word is deliberate: every session has identified goals before he hits the first ball.

Block 1 — Tempo & warm-up (10-15 min, ~25 balls)

Rory starts with short wedges — 30 to 40 yard pitches. Not full swings, not shaping shots. Just rhythm and contact. He's "feeling the bottom of the arc" and grooving tempo before any speed gets added. Amateur translation: 15 minutes of half-swing wedges to a 30-yard target. No outcome scoring; just feel.

Block 2 — Progressive bag work (20-25 min, ~40 balls)

Through the bag in order: short irons → mid-irons → long irons / hybrids → fairway woods → driver. About 5 balls per club, each one to a specific target. He's not just hitting 8-irons; he's hitting 8-irons "to the 145 flag, low fade." Every ball is a shot.

Amateur translation: 5 balls per club, every ball to a different target (vary distance and shape). Don't hit two balls in a row to the same target — it dulls focus.

Block 3 — Skill / shot-shaping (15-20 min, ~25 balls)

Now Rory works on a specific skill. One day it might be "draws only with my 7-iron." Another day, "low knockdown 9-irons." Another, "stinger 3-iron off the deck." He picks one shape, one club, and grinds it for 25 balls.

Amateur translation: Pick one skill you're working on with your coach (e.g., a slight draw, a knock-down, a bunker shot, a 50-yard pitch). Spend a focused block on it. Don't dilute by jumping topics.

Block 4 — Putting (15-20 min, deliberate)

Rory builds confidence first — short putts (3-4 ft) until he makes a chunk in a row, then progresses to 8-15 ft for line and speed, then 30-50 ft lag putts to a 3-ft circle. He finishes with a competitive drill: a specific number of consecutive short makes before he can leave.

Amateur translation: 5 min short putts (must make 10 in a row from 3 ft to leave). 5 min mid-range from 8-15 ft, focused on line. 5 min lag from 30-50 ft to a 3-ft target circle.

THE NUMBERS

~90 balls in 75-90 minutes, every one with intent. Compare that to the average amateur: 80 balls in 30 minutes, no targets, mostly driver.

What you copy from Rory (even if you only have 60 min)

  1. Have a goal before you start. One sentence: "today I'm working on contact with my 7-iron." If you don't know your goal, take 2 minutes to figure it out before you hit a ball.
  2. Block your work. Tempo first. Skills second. Putting last. No switching back.
  3. Every ball gets a target. Pick a flag. Pick a yard line. Pick a marker. No "just hitting."
  4. Vary clubs and shapes. Don't hit 30 driver swings in a row — your focus dies after 5.
  5. End on a make. Last drill of every session has a "must complete" success criterion. Trains your brain to perform under pressure.
The single biggest amateur mistake. Hitting 60 driver balls in 20 minutes "just to get loose." It's the worst possible practice. Reverses the kinematic sequence, ingrains bad tempo, exhausts your back, and teaches you nothing. Always start slow with short clubs.

How to Hit it Farther — The Science of Speed

Dr. Sasho McKenzie's research, the Stack System, and the muscle groups that move the needle. Plus a non-negotiable safety note.

Distance is the most-talked-about variable in modern golf. The PGA Tour averages over 300 yards off the tee; club-fitting and ball technology have evolved around speed; even tour pros now train explicitly for it. The good news: speed is trainable at any age. Here's the science, the protocol, and the muscles to build.

What clubhead speed actually is

Clubhead speed is the velocity of the club at impact, measured in mph. PGA Tour average is roughly 115 mph; long drive competitors push 145+. The average male amateur sits around 90 mph; female amateurs around 75 mph. Every additional 1 mph of clubhead speed is worth roughly 2.5-3 yards of carry distance with a driver.

Dr. Sasho McKenzie and overspeed training

Dr. Sasho McKenzie is the world's leading researcher on golf swing biomechanics and speed development. A professor at St. Francis Xavier University, an engineering consultant for PING, and a biomechanics advisor for FootJoy, he has published more than 20 peer-reviewed studies on swing efficiency and speed generation. His core insight: clubhead speed is largely a trained neuromuscular skill, not just a function of strength.

The training method McKenzie's research validated is called overspeed training. The principle: alternate between swinging a lighter-than-normal club (which lets your nervous system fire faster than it ever has) and a heavier-than-normal club (which builds the strength to support that speed). The contrast forces your body to recruit fast-twitch fibers more effectively. Speed gains come from teaching the brain and muscles to coordinate faster, not from getting bigger.

The Stack System

Co-founded by McKenzie and PGA professional/PING engineer Marty Jertson, The Stack System is the most rigorous and evidence-based overspeed training tool on the market. It uses a single shaft with stackable weights (you literally "stack" weights to dial in resistance) and a guided protocol via their app.

A typical Stack protocol:

  • 3 sessions per week, 20-30 minutes each
  • Each session alternates lighter and heavier configurations across multiple sets
  • App tracks every swing speed and progresses you based on actual data
  • 6-8 week protocols are typical, with a baseline retest at the end

Reported real-world results: most committed users see 5-8 mph gains in clubhead speed within 6-8 weeks, translating to roughly 15-25 yards of additional driving distance. Matt Fitzpatrick credited the Stack System publicly after his 2022 U.S. Open win at Brookline. (See thestacksystem.com for protocols and ordering.)

The muscle groups that move the needle

Speed is generated from the ground up. The four muscle groups that matter most:

1. Glutes (especially gluteus maximus)

Your glutes are your primary hip extensors and rotators. They drive the lower-body fire at the start of the downswing. Weak glutes = no ground reaction force = no speed. Train with: Romanian deadlifts, hip thrusts, single-leg bridges, jump squats.

2. Core (especially obliques and deep stabilizers)

The torso transfers energy from hips to arms via the core. A weak core leaks energy. Train with: Pallof presses, medicine ball rotational throws, cable chops, deadbug variations, plank rotations.

3. Lats and posterior shoulder

The lats are the largest muscle in the upper body and the primary mover that pulls the arms down through the swing. Posterior shoulder strength controls deceleration (which protects your shoulder joints). Train with: pull-ups, lat pulldowns, face pulls, band pull-aparts.

4. Forearms and grip

Grip strength controls clubhead speed at the very last moment of the swing. Tour pros have grip strength typically 30-50% above amateur baseline. Train with: farmer's carries, dead hangs, plate pinches, wrist roller drills.

SAFETY DISCLAIMER — READ THIS. Speed training is high-intensity and high-velocity. The same forces that build speed can injure you if your body isn't prepared. Before starting any overspeed protocol or strength program:

• Get a clearance from your doctor if you have any history of back, hip, shoulder, or cardiovascular issues, or if you're over 50 and new to resistance training.
• Get a TPI screen (see Golf Science · Article 4) so you know which mobility limitations you have before you start swinging at maximum speeds — you'll injure the tightest joint first.
• Work with a qualified golf-fitness professional or physical therapist for the first 2-4 weeks of any new strength program. Form matters more than load.
• Never train through sharp pain. Soreness is fine; pain is information.
• Warm up properly. A 10-minute dynamic warm-up before any speed work is not optional.

Speed training works. It's also where most amateur golf injuries happen. Be smart.

The order of operations

  1. Get assessed. TPI screen + medical clearance.
  2. Build a strength base. 8-12 weeks of foundational work targeting the 4 muscle groups above. Establish good movement patterns.
  3. Add overspeed. Stack System or equivalent, 2-3 sessions per week. 6-8 week protocol.
  4. Re-test. Baseline your clubhead speed before and after. Real numbers, not feel.
  5. Maintain. Speed gains require maintenance — 1-2 short overspeed sessions per week keeps the gains long-term.
THE BIG PICTURE

You can add 5-8 mph to your clubhead speed in two months if you commit. That's 15-25 yards. There's no instruction tip, no club, no ball, no swing thought that compares. Speed is the most leveraged single variable in modern golf.
