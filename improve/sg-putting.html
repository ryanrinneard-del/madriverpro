Section 01

What the data actually says about your stroke.

Face vs. path is roughly 95/5. The stroke is a slight arc, not a straight line. Both points are settled by measurement, not opinion.

For two decades there was a public argument in golf about whether the putter should travel straight back and straight through, or on an arc. Both camps were missing the point. Modern biomechanics measurement — SAM PuttLab and similar systems — has shown two things repeatedly:

  • The stroke is a slight arc. Not a big arc, not a straight line — a gentle arc that follows naturally from the angle of the putter shaft and the rotation of the shoulders. Trying to force the putter dead-straight requires hand manipulation, which destroys face control.
  • The face dictates direction — about 95% of it. Where the face points at impact is where the ball starts. Path, the direction the putter is moving through impact, accounts for the remaining 5% (and influences how much the ball curves, not where it starts).

That second number is the one that should change how you think about every putt. If you miss left, your face was almost certainly closed at impact — not open, not "blocked path," not your alignment. The face was closed. If you miss right, the face was open. The path matters, but not the way the old teaching said it did.

SAM PuttLab measurement
What gets measured every stroke

When I run a player through a SAM PuttLab session, the system captures — at thousands of frames per second — the following per stroke:

Face angle at address, takeaway, and impact. Path direction (where the putter is travelling at impact). Tempo ratio (back vs through swing time). Rotation pattern (does the face open during takeaway and close through impact — or does it stay shut?). Loft delivery (is the putter adding or losing loft at impact?). Stroke length symmetry. Eye position relative to the ball.

After ten strokes the report is unambiguous. You see exactly what your stroke is doing — and exactly which two things to fix.

Here's why this matters for you, even without coming to see me on SAM: when you miss a putt, stop guessing about whether it was your read, your speed, or your stroke. The face is almost certainly the cause. Look at where the ball started rolling relative to your intended start line. If it started left, your face was closed. Right, open. That's the diagnosis. The fix is in Sections 2 through 4.

Section 02

Get fit before you practice.

The wrong putter undoes any amount of practice. Length, lie, head style — all measurable, all worth it.

I see students every season who have practiced putting hard and gotten worse. The reason isn't always their stroke. Often it's the putter. Most amateurs are using a putter they bought because it looked nice or because it was on sale, and the geometry doesn't match their stroke pattern or their body.

The three measurements that matter

Length. Most putters off the rack are 34 or 35 inches. Most amateurs need shorter than that. If your eyes don't sit directly over (or just inside) the ball at address, the putter is the wrong length. A putter that's too long forces your eyes inside the line, which causes a pull pattern. A putter too short bunches you over the ball and locks your shoulders.

Lie angle. When the putter sits naturally on the ground in your stance, the toe should be very slightly raised — neither flat on the ground nor heel-up. A lie angle that's wrong will cause heel/toe contact issues that mimic stroke problems but can't be fixed by stroke work.

Head style: mallet vs. blade. This is where SAM data changes the conversation. A player whose stroke shows strong rotation (the face opens significantly going back and closes through impact) needs a putter that allows that rotation — usually a toe-balanced blade. A player with minimal rotation (face stays close to square through the stroke) needs a face-balanced mallet that resists rotation. Putting a strong-arc player on a face-balanced mallet, or a square-stroke player on a toe-balanced blade, is putting them on the wrong tool. Practice doesn't fix mismatched equipment.

How I figure out which one you need: I put you on SAM and watch your natural rotation pattern across ten strokes. The software shows the face angle through the stroke as a curve. If the curve has big openings and closings, you need a blade. If it's nearly flat, you need a mallet. Five-minute conversation that saves you from a year of wrong-putter practice.
Self-check
Eyes-over-the-ball test

Stand to the ball with your putter as you normally would. Hold a second ball at the bridge of your nose and let it drop. If it lands on or just inside the ball at your feet, your putter length and posture are working. If it lands well inside, the putter is too long for your stance. If it lands outside, you're bent over too far and probably need to stand taller.

This is the simplest fitting check that works without me. If it fails, come see me — we'll measure it properly.

Section 03

Find where your putting actually breaks.

Every player has a distance where their make rate falls off a cliff. Mine is 5–6 feet. What's yours?

Putting practice is most useful when it's targeted at the distance band where you're actually leaking strokes. Most amateurs spread their practice evenly — some short, some medium, some long — and never get systematically better at any of it. Find your weak distance and pour practice into it.

My own weak distance is 5–6 feet. Long enough that I have to commit to a line and a speed, short enough that there's no excuse for missing — which means short enough that the pressure shows up. I know this because I've measured it. You should know yours.

How to find your weak distance

Spend one round paying attention to where your missed putts came from. Don't track makes; track misses. Three feet, six feet, ten feet, fifteen, thirty. After 18 holes you'll have a pattern. Most amateurs find one of three:

  • 3–5 foot misses. Almost always a face issue under pressure. Decel, flinch, or open face from a too-soft grip.
  • 6–12 foot misses. Mostly read, sometimes face. The distance where commitment to a line matters most.
  • 20–40 foot lag misses. Distance control. Speed, not direction. Most three-putts originate here.

If you don't know which one is yours, you're guessing in practice. Once you know it, the drills in Section 4 are targeted — you spend 70% of your practice time on the band where strokes are leaking, not on the band that already feels good.

The diagnostic version on SAM: in a clinic, I'll put you on the system at three different distances and read the data. SAM tells me whether your face control falls apart at 6 feet, whether your tempo gets shaky at 20, whether your eye position changes when you're nervous about a 3-footer. The "where's your weak distance" question becomes a measurement, not a guess.

Drill 01
The 3 · 6 · 9 · 12 · 15 — flat putt, miss-and-restart

On a flat section of the practice green, set up five tees in a line: 3, 6, 9, 12, and 15 feet from a hole. Make all five putts in order. Miss any — back to 3 feet. That's the rule. No second chances at any distance, no skipping ahead.

First time most players try this, it takes 20 minutes. Third time, it takes 6. The pressure builds as you progress — making the 12-footer knowing one miss sends you back to the start is exactly the kind of pressure that exposes your weak spots. The drill scales: when 3-15 is too easy, run 6-12-18 or 4-8-12. The flat-putt-with-restart structure is mine; the progressive-distance idea is borrowed from the way tour practice greens are set up.

Drill 02
The 10 · 15 · 20 ladder — mid-range commitment

Set up three tees at 10, 15, and 20 feet on the practice green. Three balls per distance. The goal isn't to make them — it's to finish each putt within an 18-inch circle of the hole. Make the circle visible by placing tees around it for the first sessions until your eye trains.

What this builds: speed control at the distances where most of your second putts originate. The 10-15-20 band is where good lag putting becomes routine three-putt prevention.

Drill 03
The 30 · 40 · 50 ladder — long-range distance

Three tees, much further out: 30, 40, 50 feet. Three balls per distance. The target now is finishing within a 3-foot circle of the hole. From 50 feet, this is realistic excellence; lag putts that finish within 3 feet rarely become three-putts.

This is the drill I'd run for any player whose miss pattern in Section 3 was 20–40 foot lag misses. If three-putts are your leak, this drill is the patch.

Practice math: 70% of your putting practice should be on your weak distance from Section 3. The other 30% maintains the bands that already work. If you don't know your weak distance yet, run the diagnostic in a round before you redesign your practice.
Section 05

When to come to a clinic.

Some things you can fix from a web page. Some things you really can't.

Everything above is real and works on its own. But there's a ceiling on what you can diagnose without measurement, and there's a ceiling on what you can adjust without a coach watching. If you've read this page, run the drills, and your putting hasn't shifted in a month — come see me on SAM.

What a SAM session reveals in 30 minutes:

  • Whether your face is closed at impact (and by how many degrees) on your typical miss
  • Whether your putter geometry is fighting your stroke pattern
  • Whether your eye line over the ball is actually where it needs to be
  • Whether your tempo is consistent — or whether your back-swing varies in ways you can't feel
  • Where on the green (distance, slope, pressure) your numbers fall apart

That same 30 minutes, without SAM, would be us on the practice green guessing at the same questions. With SAM, the answers are on the screen. One session usually shows me two things to fix that I can put on a weekly practice plan you can actually run.
