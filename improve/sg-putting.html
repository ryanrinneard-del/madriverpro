Module 03 · Skill 01

Putting.

Three things determine whether a putt goes in: the line you start it on, the speed you put on it, and the read you committed to. Get good at those three. Everything else is decoration.
Ryan's framework
From the coach

Putting is the simplest skill in golf and the one most amateurs overcomplicate. There are three pillars — start the ball on a straight line, control your speed, read the green — and almost everything you've ever read about putting fits under one of those three. Get good at the three. Skip the rest.

This page lays out each pillar, what the truth is, and how to do each one like a pro. Then the numbers — what tour players actually make from each distance versus a 10, 20, and 30 handicap, so you can see exactly where your real opportunity is. Then the drills I run with my own students. Then a short note at the bottom on putter fitting and stroke analysis if you want to get measured.

— Ryan

Section 01

Start the ball on a straight line.

The face dictates roughly 95% of where the ball starts. Path is the smaller lever. Settle this in your mind and you simplify everything that follows.

The straight-back-straight-through theory has been put to bed. Good strokes travel on a slight arc — the natural arc that results from the angle of the putter shaft and the rotation of the shoulders. Trying to force the putter dead-straight requires hand manipulation, and hands are what produce the face changes that miss putts.

If you miss left, your face was almost certainly closed at impact. If you miss right, it was open. Path mattered, but only a little. Start every diagnostic conversation about a missed putt with: where was the face?

How to do it like a pro
  1. Aim the face, not your body. Pick the start line of the putt (on a breaker that's not the hole — it's a point left or right of the hole). Set the face to that line first, then build your stance around the face. Most amateurs build the stance and hope the face came along. It rarely did.
  2. Use a tight intermediate target. Spot an old ball mark, a darker patch of grass, or a slightly different shade of green about 6–12 inches in front of your ball, on your start line. Roll the ball over that spot. The brain locks onto a near target far better than a 20-foot-away one.
  3. Eyes over (or just inside) the ball. Drop a ball from the bridge of your nose — it should land on or just inside the ball you're putting. Eyes inside cause pulls, eyes outside cause pushes.
  4. Keep the head perfectly still through impact. Don't watch the ball leave the face. Let your hearing tell you when it was struck. Head movement = shoulders move = face moves = miss.
  5. Accelerate through impact. The forward stroke should be slightly faster than the backstroke. Deceleration is the single biggest cause of face wobble on short putts.
Section 02

Control your speed.

Speed is more important than line. A good-line putt with bad speed misses. A slightly-mis-read putt with great speed gets close. Gravity helps you when the speed is right.

Speed is the variable that makes every other variable forgivable. A putt rolling at the right pace will fall in from the side door. A putt rolling too fast skips over the lip; a putt rolling too slow takes too much break and misses low. Most three-putts are speed problems, not line problems.

How to do it like a pro
  1. Aim for 12–18 inches past on a perfect read. This is the speed at which break is most predictable and the hole accepts the most putts (front edge, sides, even back lip). Putts that die at the hole wobble off-line; putts that race 4 feet past take the break out and remove the side doors.
  2. Build three strokes you trust. Short, medium, long — each tied to a feel and a length of stroke. Short is your 6-footer. Medium is your 20-footer. Long is your 40-footer. Calibrate them on the practice green and pull from those three under pressure, adjusted for slope.
  3. Uphill firmer, downhill dying. Uphill putts hold their line better with extra pace. Downhill putts need to die into the hole or they roll past. Most amateurs leave uphill putts short and run downhill putts off the green.
  4. Read speed before you read line. Walk to the hole. Look back at the ball. Decide: is this fast (downhill, downgrain, smooth) or slow (uphill, into-grain, recently aerated)? Speed read first, line read second.
  5. Practice lag more than line. The single biggest scoring leak for amateurs is three-putts. Three-putts are speed errors. Spend twice as much time on 30-foot lag putts as you do on 6-foot makes.
The math: a missed 6-footer costs you 1 stroke (2 putts instead of 1). A three-putt also costs you 1 stroke (3 putts instead of 2). Stroke-for-stroke they're equivalent — but cutting your three-putts in half is easier than making 20% more of your 6-footers. Speed control improves quickly with focused lag practice; short-putt make rate moves slowly. Lag practice is the single highest-leverage thing most amateurs aren't doing.
Section 03

Read the green.

Most amateurs under-read break by about half. The good news: there's a procedure that works, and it's repeatable.

Green reading is a skill that gets trained, not a gift. Players who read greens well aren't more talented — they're more systematic. They have a procedure and they run it the same way every time. Your job is to build that procedure.

How to do it like a pro
  1. Read from low to high. Walk to the low side of the putt and look up the slope toward the hole. Slope reads cleanly from below; the high side lies to your eye. If the putt is right-to-left, you read it from the right side of the hole. Left-to-right, from the left.
  2. Pick an apex, not a line. The apex is the highest point the ball will travel on its way to the hole. Aim at the apex; let gravity pull the ball down to the cup from there. Aiming at "a curve" is too many decisions; aiming at "a point" is one decision.
  3. Read the last three feet first. The last three feet are where the ball is slowest and most affected by slope. Whatever the green is doing right around the hole — that's the most important read. Build the rest of the line backwards from there.
  4. Play more break than your eye says. If your gut says 6 inches, play 9. Amateurs systematically under-read. Missing on the high side gives you two ways to make the putt (front lip + side); missing low gives you one way (front lip only) and usually rolls further past.
  5. Trust your first read. Don't re-read. The brain's first instinct on a putt is usually right; the second-guess is the eye looking for confirmation it doesn't have. Once you've picked an apex and a speed, commit. Trust beats accuracy under pressure.
  6. Pace off long putts. Walk from your ball to the hole on long putts — not just to feel the slope, but to count distance. Most amateurs misjudge 30 feet vs 45 feet by 5+ feet. Pacing fixes that and the speed read follows.

What the numbers tell you

Two things jump off these tables. First, the gap between tour and 10-handicap is biggest in the 5–10 foot range (75% vs 55% from 5 feet, 40% vs 22% from 10). That's where pressure shows up — the distance is short enough to feel like you should make it, long enough that good stroke mechanics actually matter. If you're a 10-handicap looking to get to 5, this is where the strokes are.

Second, the three-putt rate from 30+ feet is where mid- and high-handicaps bleed. A 20-handicap three-putts a third of their long lag putts. A tour player three-putts those at 6–12%. If you're shooting 90, the difference between 88 and 84 isn't making more 6-footers — it's not three-putting from 35 feet.

How to use this: after your next round, write down where each putt started from and whether it went in (and how many you took). Compare to the table above. The distance where your number is furthest below your tier is your weakness — that's where 70% of practice should go for the next month.
Section 05

The drills.

Three structured drills covering short, mid, and long range. Pressure built in, feedback immediate.

Drill 01 · Short range
The 3 · 6 · 9 · 12 · 15 — flat putt, miss-and-restart

On a flat section of the practice green, set up five tees in a line: 3, 6, 9, 12, and 15 feet from a hole. Make all five putts in order. Miss any — back to 3 feet. That's the rule. No second chances at any distance, no skipping ahead.

First time most players try this, it takes 20 minutes. Third time, it takes 6. The pressure builds as you progress — making the 12-footer knowing one miss sends you back to the start is exactly the kind of pressure that exposes your weak spots.

Drill 02 · Mid range
The 10 · 15 · 20 ladder — lag commitment

Set up three tees at 10, 15, and 20 feet on the practice green. Three balls per distance. The goal isn't to make them — it's to finish each putt within an 18-inch circle of the hole. Place tees around the hole to mark the circle for the first sessions until your eye trains.

What this builds: speed control at the distances where most of your second putts originate. The 10–20 band is where good lag putting becomes routine three-putt prevention.

Drill 03 · Long range
The 30 · 40 · 50 ladder — long-range distance

Three tees, much further out: 30, 40, 50 feet. Three balls per distance. The target now is finishing within a 3-foot circle of the hole. From 50 feet, this is realistic excellence; lag putts that finish within 3 feet rarely become three-putts.

This is the drill for any player whose biggest leak in Section 4 was three-putt rate from long range. If three-putts are your problem, this drill is the patch.

Practice math: 70% of your putting practice should be on the distance band where your numbers are furthest below your tier. The other 30% maintains the bands that already work. Don't practice strengths.

Tools that help.

A note on putter fitting, stroke analysis, and one training aid worth owning.

Putter fitting + stroke analysis

I'm certified on SAM PuttLab. It's the biomechanics measurement system used widely on tour and at the academy level. Two things SAM does well that nothing else does as cleanly:

Putter fitting

The right putter for you depends on your stroke pattern. A stroke that rotates the face strongly through the swing needs a toe-balanced blade that allows the rotation. A stroke with minimal face rotation needs a face-balanced mallet that resists rotation. Putting the wrong head style on the wrong stroke is one of the most common reasons amateurs practice and don't improve. SAM measures your rotation pattern, lie, and length in 10 strokes — and you walk away with a spec sheet for the putter you should actually be using.

Stroke analysis

Once a putter fits, SAM tells you exactly what the stroke is doing — face angle at impact, path direction, tempo consistency, loft delivery. If you've been working on your putting and it isn't moving, a 30-minute session usually reveals two specific things to fix and a practice plan to fix them. Get measured, then practice with intent.
