The Stack & Tilt Swing.

Four fundamentals. Ball-first contact. No pivot backwards.
A primer on the Plummer & Bennett system
From the coach

When Andy Plummer and Mike Bennett published The Stack & Tilt Swing in 2009, the golf world split cleanly down the middle. Half the instruction community called it a revelation — the first swing system that actually matched what they were seeing in tour players' impact frames. The other half called it heresy. “You can't stay forward at the top of the swing,” they said. “That's reverse pivot.”

Plummer and Bennett went to bat with video. They'd studied dozens of the best ball-strikers of all time and found that the “classic” motion — shift your weight to the trail side at the top, then shift it back through impact — wasn't what those players actually did. The best ball-strikers stayed more on their lead side at the top than convention taught. They tilted their spine toward the target, not away from it. And the results were ball-first contact, a consistent low point, and a draw-biased path that the game's best had been producing for a century.

This primer is for the recreational player who's heard the name, is curious whether it's for them, and wants the four fundamentals translated into plain English before picking up the 340-page book. Eight chapters: what the system actually is, the four fundamentals one at a time, how to learn them in order, the drills that ingrain each one, and where Stack & Tilt fits in the modern instruction landscape.

Read straight through or skip to the fundamental you know you're violating. Then go to the range and actually try it — the book becomes useful the moment you put it down and move.

— Ryan

Chapter One

What Stack & Tilt actually is

The four-fundamental system, the history, and the myth it dismantles.
›

Stack & Tilt gets more misrepresented than any modern swing system. The nickname itself is misleading — most people hear it and assume it means don't move at all. Stand still. Reverse pivot. Break the rules of the swing. That's not what Plummer and Bennett argued, and understanding what they actually said is the first step to using the system.

The key idea. Stack & Tilt identifies four fundamentals that the best ball-strikers in history already do: weight forward, shoulder down, arms straight, tuck the hips. The “stack” is staying centered over the ball. The “tilt” is the spine angle tilting toward the target through impact. Together they produce the one thing every golfer actually needs: ball-first contact.

What Plummer and Bennett actually argued

The claim that launched the system was straightforward. Conventional golf instruction teaches a two-part motion: shift your weight onto the trail side during the backswing, then shift it back onto the lead side through impact. This is taught as gospel. It's in every magazine, every YouTube lesson, every junior clinic.

Plummer and Bennett went to the video and said: the best players in the world don't actually do this. Ben Hogan didn't do it. Moe Norman didn't do it. In their research, the ball-strikers with the most consistent low points and the most forgiving patterns stayed more centered over the ball than the traditional model taught. Their weight didn't shift back and then forward — it stayed forward the whole way and kept moving forward through impact.

That single insight, applied consistently, spawned the four fundamentals. Each fundamental is a way of keeping you stacked over the ball (not pivoting backwards) and tilted into the target (not leaning back through impact).

“Stack & Tilt isn't a method. It's a set of universal observations about what actually makes a golf ball go where you want. Whether you call it S&T or not, the four fundamentals are what distinguishes good ball-strikers from poor ones.”

The four fundamentals, in one sentence each

  1. Weight Forward. More weight on your lead side at setup, and it keeps moving forward through the swing.
  2. Shoulder Down. The lead shoulder moves down in the backswing, not around or inward.
  3. Arms Straight. The arms stay extended through impact and into the finish; the radius is preserved.
  4. Tuck the Hips. The hips move laterally and upward through impact, finishing in a vertical, stacked position.
A note on the name
The label “Stack & Tilt” gets attached to a lot of things that aren't Stack & Tilt. A reverse pivot is not Stack & Tilt. Standing still and swinging with your arms only is not Stack & Tilt. Leaning forward so hard your weight falls off your toes is not Stack & Tilt. Plummer and Bennett describe a dynamic motion where the body rotates, tilts, and moves — just not in the directions conventional instruction teaches. If what you're doing looks frozen, you're not doing Stack & Tilt. You're just standing there.

Who teaches and has used it

Andy Plummer and Mike Bennett are the founders. They've worked with Aaron Baddeley, Mike Weir (briefly), Charlie Wi, Grant Waite, and a list of tour players that expands year to year. Plenty of tour players use the ideas without labeling themselves as “Stack & Tilt players” — because at the tour level, everyone is already doing some version of the four fundamentals, whether they know it or not.

Common misunderstanding

“Stack & Tilt = no weight shift. You just stand there.”

There is motion. Weight moves forward more, not less — it just never moves backward. Hips turn. Shoulders turn. Spine tilts. What's different is the direction: every motion is toward the target, not away from it.

Practical takeaway

Film yourself face-on. Freeze at the top of your backswing. If your head has moved backward or your lead heel has come off the ground, you're doing the conventional reverse-pivot — and that's the single biggest source of inconsistent contact for most recreational players. Fix this first. Every other fundamental becomes possible once this one is in place.

Which is why the first fundamental — and the one we start with — is Weight Forward.
2
Chapter Two

Weight Forward

The fundamental that makes every other fundamental possible.
›

If you fix nothing else from this book, fix this one. Weight Forward is the single most counter-intuitive idea in Stack & Tilt — and the most important. Get this right and the other three fundamentals become achievable. Get it wrong and nothing else matters.

The key idea. At setup, more than half your lower-body weight is forward on your lead side. During the swing, that weight doesn't shift backwards. It stays forward and keeps moving forward all the way through impact and into the finish. The result: the club bottoms out after the ball (ball first), the shot launches low-forward on the clubface, and the path becomes naturally in-to-out.

What it looks like, mechanically

At address with an iron, you set up with roughly 55–60% of your weight on your lead foot. (For a driver it's closer to even because you're hitting on the upswing, but the forward bias never disappears completely.) As you take the club back, your weight does not shift to the trail side. If anything, it moves more forward. In the downswing, it keeps moving forward. At the finish, nearly all your weight is on your lead foot and your chest is facing the target.

This is the exact opposite of what most golfers are taught. The conventional instruction is “shift your weight to your trail side at the top of the backswing,” which produces reverse-pivot-looking motions in amateurs and a host of inconsistent-contact symptoms: fat shots, thin shots, heel strikes, pull-hooks, and loss of distance.

“The weight doesn't go back and then forward. It goes forward, and then more forward. That's the whole trick.”

Why it works

Physics. If the center of your swing (your sternum, roughly) stays forward of the ball throughout the motion, the clubhead's low point will be forward of the ball too. The low point of the swing is where the clubhead is nearest the ground — the bottom of the arc. If the low point is after the ball, you make ball-first contact. If the low point is before the ball, you hit the ground first (fat) or, compensating, you pick up with your hands and hit the top of the ball (thin).

Weight forward keeps the low point forward. That's the whole mechanism. Everything else — the shoulder plane, the arm extension, the hip tuck — serves to preserve the forward low point once you've set it at address.

Common misunderstanding

“Weight forward = falling forward onto my toes.”

No. Weight forward means toward the target — lead foot vs. trail foot. It does not mean toward the ball. Your weight should still be balanced between the balls of your feet and your heels. If you feel like you're falling over the ball, you're leaning, not weighting forward.

Another common misunderstanding

“Isn't this just a reverse pivot?” A reverse pivot is when your upper body leans toward the target at the top of the backswing while your head stays centered — producing an over-the-top downswing. Stack & Tilt is the opposite: the head stays slightly target-ward of where it started (or stays centered), while the shoulders turn on a steep angle away from the target. They look similar in a single frame; they are not the same motion.

Test yourself

Stand in address with an iron. Have a friend or tripod film you face-on. Take a full backswing and pause at the top. Look at the video. Did your head move backwards? Did your lead foot heel lift? Did your weight visibly shift to your trail side? If yes to any of those, you're reverse-pivoting rather than staying forward. This is the fix — don't try to hit a ball until the video shows your head at the top in the same horizontal position it started, or slightly forward.

With weight forward anchored at setup, the next move — the Shoulder Down pattern — becomes not just possible but inevitable.
3
Chapter Three

Shoulder Down

The backswing move that keeps the head still and the club on plane.
›

Most golfers are taught to “turn your shoulders” in the backswing. That instruction isn't wrong, but it's under-specified. The shoulders can turn in two very different ways: flat (baseball-style, around the body) or steep (a tilted rotation where the lead shoulder moves down). One of these works. The other produces every slice, pull, and over-the-top you've ever hit.

The key idea. The lead shoulder moves downward in the backswing — not inward, not around, not flat. Combined with weight forward, this keeps the head still and lets the club track on an in-to-out path from the ground up, which is what produces a draw bias and solid contact.

What it looks like

Imagine a line drawn vertically from the top of your head to the ground at address. As you turn back, your lead shoulder passes under your chin and moves toward the ground — not around toward your trail side. At the top of the backswing, your lead shoulder is noticeably lower than your trail shoulder. Your spine is tilted toward the target, not away from it.

In conventional instruction, the turn is often taught as “flat and level — like a baseball swing.” That produces a shoulder plane that's close to horizontal, which in turn produces a club path that comes from outside-in (over the top) or gets stuck behind the body. In Stack & Tilt, the turn is steep because the shoulders are rotating on a plane that already tilts toward the ball. The geometry does the work.

“The lead shoulder going down is what keeps your head still. If your lead shoulder moves around your body instead of under your chin, your head has to go with it — and now you're swaying, reverse pivoting, or both.”

Why the head stays still

This is the key biomechanical point, and it's worth slowing down to understand. Your head sits between your shoulders. If your shoulders turn around your body on a flat plane, one shoulder has to pass through the space your head occupies. The body's only two options are: move your head out of the way (sway) or pivot backward to make room (reverse pivot). Either way, you've lost your center.

But if your shoulders rotate on a tilted plane — with the lead shoulder going down rather than around — neither shoulder passes through the head's space. The shoulders can turn a full 90 degrees while your head stays in the exact position where you started. That's the move.

What it feels like for a player coming from conventional instruction

If you've been taught to turn flat your whole life, Shoulder Down will feel radically steep at first. It will feel like you're tilting the world over. This is normal and the feeling passes — because what's actually happening is much less extreme than it feels. Film yourself and you'll see a turn that looks natural to the eye, even though it felt wildly tilted in the body.

Common misunderstanding

“Shoulder down means dipping my head and chest straight down toward the ball.”

No. Your head stays in its original position. Only the lead shoulder moves down — the chest rotates around the spine without the whole body collapsing. If your head is visibly lower at the top of the backswing than at address, you've overcooked it.

Feel it without a club

Stand in golf posture, arms crossed over your chest. Slowly rotate so your lead shoulder tracks toward a spot on the ground about 12 inches in front of your lead foot. You should feel a distinct tilt in your spine toward the target. Now return to start. Do this 20 times before you ever pick up a club. The feel should become familiar before you try to execute it at swing speed.

With weight forward and shoulder down working together, we can now look at what the arms are doing — specifically, what they need to not do.

Chapter Four

Arms Straight

Preserving the radius of your swing through impact.
›

A golf swing is a circle — or as close to one as a human body can make. The radius of that circle is defined by the length of your outstretched arms plus the length of the club. If you preserve that radius through impact, your contact is consistent. If the radius changes — if your arms bend near impact — contact becomes random.

The key idea. Your arms stay straight through impact and into the finish. “Straight” doesn't mean tense or locked — it means extended, unbent, radius preserved. The moment your arms flex near impact, three things happen at once: the clubhead path moves off line, the low point moves inconsistently, and the clubface goes random. None of those are recoverable.

What it looks like

From the top of the backswing through impact, your lead arm stays extended. The trail arm folds during the backswing — that's normal — but as the downswing starts, the trail arm begins extending too, and by impact both arms are fully straight. The “V” made by your two forearms at address is preserved at impact. Through the follow-through, both arms stay extended until the natural rotation of the body folds the trail arm again in the finish.

The classic amateur problem is the “chicken wing”: as the lead arm approaches impact, the elbow bends and tucks toward the body. This happens because the arms aren't keeping up with the body's rotation. The radius shortens. The clubhead comes up off the plane. The face opens. Contact becomes thin, weak, or sliced.

“Think of your arms as a fishing rod you're casting. The rod stays straight through the cast. It's the wrists that hinge and release. If the rod bends, the cast goes haywire — same thing happens to a golf club.”

Why arms bend at impact

There's almost always a cause further back in the swing. The most common: weight shifted backward in the backswing (violating Fundamental 1), which forces the player to slide forward through the downswing to recover, which in turn forces the arms to fold to keep the clubhead from hitting the ground first. The chicken wing is a rescue move.

In other words, if your arms are bending at impact, the fix isn't usually to consciously straighten your arms — it's to fix the weight position that's forcing the arms to bend in self-defense. Weight forward from the start, arms straight becomes natural. Weight back, arms straight becomes impossible.

The connection to clubface path

Arms straight also determines the clubhead's path through the ball. A fully extended swing traces a wide, flowing arc. A bent-arm swing traces a narrower, choppier arc. The wider arc gives you more time for the clubface to square up through impact; the narrower arc forces the face to square more quickly and often produces timing-dependent contact (fine on good days, terrible on bad ones).

Common misunderstanding

“Arms straight means arms stiff and locked.”

Not the same thing. Straight means extended, unbent, free of flex. Stiff means tense, muscularly locked, unable to move. You want extended and relaxed — think of a martial-arts punch: the arm is fully extended at the moment of contact, but there's no tension anywhere. Tension kills clubhead speed.

Check your impact

Film yourself from face-on with a mid-iron. Freeze at impact. Draw an imaginary line down your lead arm from shoulder to wrist. Is it straight? Or is the elbow clearly folded? If it's folded, that's your project for the next month — and the project is almost always weight forward at address, not conscious arm extension.

Three down, one to go. The final fundamental is what the lower body is doing while all of this happens — the hip tuck.
5
Chapter Five

Tuck the Hips

The lower-body pattern that delivers the club and holds the finish.
›

The first three fundamentals are largely about the upper body and the arms. This one is about the lower body — specifically, what the hips do from transition through the finish. Get this right and the swing finishes “stacked” (hence the name): hips vertical, chest facing the target, lead side fully posted up, balanced finish that holds.

The key idea. Through impact and into the finish, the hips move laterally (toward the target) and upward (standing up into the post), not rotationally around a fixed center. The motion is a tuck — the pelvis moves forward and up, the lead leg straightens, and the body finishes vertical. If you can't pose the finish — if you stumble, fall back, or end up off-balance — your hips didn't tuck correctly.

What it looks like

At impact, your lead hip has already shifted toward the target. Through follow-through, the lead leg extends — the knee straightens as the hips move up and forward. By the finish, your lead leg is a straight post, your pelvis is vertical (not tilted backward), your chest faces the target, and your weight is almost entirely on your lead foot. You can literally lift your trail foot off the ground.

Compare to a conventional finish: many amateurs finish with their hips open but not moved forward — they've rotated around a fixed center, weight ends up on the trail side, lead foot still square or even opened. The hip tuck is different. The hips travel, not just rotate.

“If you can hold your finish position for three seconds without stumbling, your hips tucked correctly. If you can't, they didn't. That's the whole test.”

Why it matters for the strike

The hip tuck provides something subtle but essential: vertical clearance. As the body moves forward and up, it creates room for the arms and club to pass through the impact zone without the body getting in the way. Without the tuck, the body is too close to the ball at impact — the arms have to compensate by folding (chicken wing), and the clubhead often ends up hitting the ground before the ball. With the tuck, the arms stay extended, the clubhead sweeps through cleanly, and contact is consistent.

How to isolate the feel

The hip tuck can be hard to isolate at full swing speed. Ryan uses half-swings to strip away everything else: make a swing where your lead arm finishes parallel to the ground on the backswing, and your trail arm finishes parallel to the ground on the finish. That abbreviated motion takes the long levers out of it and leaves just the hip move. When you can feel the tuck on a half-swing, you can scale it up.

Common misunderstanding

“Tuck the hips means thrust my pelvis forward toward the ball.”

No — toward the target, not toward the ball. The direction is lateral (down the line), not forward (toward where the ball sits). Thrusting toward the ball is early extension — the opposite of the tuck, and one of the most common swing faults in amateur golf.

The finish test

After every full swing at the range, hold your finish for three seconds. Ask: are you balanced? Is your chest facing the target? Could you lift your trail foot? If yes to all three, your hips tucked. If any part of your body is recovering or shifting to stay upright, something in the tuck failed — usually because the weight started back rather than forward.

Four fundamentals in hand. Now: in what order do you learn them?

Chapter Six

Putting them together

The first-lesson sequence. Why you start with weight forward.
›

Knowing the four fundamentals is not the same as being able to execute them. In a first lesson with a new student, we don't work on all four at once — we work on them in a specific order. Each one unlocks the next. Try to work on them out of order and you'll chase your tail for months.

The sequence.

1. Weight Forward — always first. Without this, none of the others are achievable.

2. Shoulder Down — once the weight is forward, the shoulder plane can tilt correctly. Trying to get the shoulder to go down while the weight shifts back is like trying to thread a needle on a trampoline.

3. Tuck the Hips — third, not fourth, because the lower-body pattern is what gives the arms room to extend. Fix the lower body before fixing the arms.

4. Arms Straight — last. If the first three are in place, this one mostly happens by itself. If it doesn't, you've got a genuine arm problem (rare) rather than a compensation problem (common).

Why Weight Forward is always first

Because every other fundamental is a downstream effect of weight position. Weight back → body away from ball at top → downswing has to recover lost ground → hips slide instead of tuck → arms bend to compensate for slide → contact random. Weight forward → body centered over ball at top → downswing is a release not a rescue → hips tuck naturally → arms stay extended → contact consistent.

Trying to fix anything else first is like trying to fix a house built on a cracked foundation by redoing the wallpaper. The foundation is weight forward. Fix that and the rest becomes tractable.

Why Arms Straight is last

Because the chicken wing is almost always a symptom, not a cause. The arms bend because the body was out of position and the player is unconsciously compensating. If you try to consciously straighten the arms without fixing the underlying position, you'll produce a new set of compensations — usually worse contact, because now the arms are tense instead of just bent.

Fix the weight, fix the shoulder, fix the hip tuck, and the arms mostly straighten themselves. On the rare occasion they don't, that's when we work on them directly — but it's a small minority of cases.

“You don't teach the golf swing by explaining all four fundamentals in the first lesson. You teach the first one, run a thousand reps, confirm it's set, then move on. The body learns one thing at a time.”

How long this takes

Depends entirely on the player, their history, and how ingrained their old pattern is. Someone new to golf can install all four fundamentals in a few months of committed practice. A 20-handicap who's been taught the conventional model for 15 years might take a full season to unwind the old pattern and install the new one. Neither timeline is bad — the change is worth the time.

What will not work: taking two lessons, hitting a few balls each week, and expecting the pattern to change. Pattern changes require volume. Expect to hit several thousand reps thinking about weight forward alone before it's reflexive. That's just how the nervous system works. Plan for it.

If you're going to work on this without a coach

Pick Fundamental 1. Weight forward. Set up an iPhone on a tripod face-on. Film every ball you hit for the next two range sessions. Freeze the top of each backswing. If your head moves backward at all, that's your project. Don't move to Fundamental 2 until 9 of 10 swings show a centered head at the top. Then you've earned the right to work on shoulder down.

Which brings us to the drills Ryan uses to ingrain each fundamental — by effect, not mechanics.

Chapter Seven

The drills

Three named drills that ingrain the feels. The Wrench. The Bowler. 90-90-90.
›

Named drills matter because they create shorthand. When a student hears “run through The Bowler before your next full swing,” they know exactly what's coming — the feel, the pattern, the rhythm. The drill name carries all of that in two words.

The drills below are the three Ryan uses most often with Stack & Tilt work. This section describes what each drill creates, not the mechanics — those get taught in person, because the mechanics matter less than the feel, and text descriptions of golf mechanics are notoriously misleading.

The rule of drill descriptions. Describing a drill by its mechanics tempts you to copy the mechanics without understanding the feel. Describing a drill by its effect tells you what success feels like, so when you try it in person with a coach, you know whether you've hit the target.

The Wrench Drill

What it creates: the hip-tuck pattern. The feeling of the pelvis moving laterally and upward through impact, not rotating around a fixed center. After running The Wrench for a few minutes, your finish should feel vertical and held — you can pose the finish without thinking about it.

When to use it: when your finish is falling backward or to the side, when you can't hold your follow-through, when the ball is going left of target on otherwise good swings, or when a video shows early extension (hips thrusting toward the ball at impact).

On the drill mechanics
Ryan teaches the specific mechanics of The Wrench in person. This primer doesn't reproduce them for the same reason the primer doesn't reproduce Homer Kelley's plane geometry: the precision matters, and getting it from text instead of from a coach is a recipe for learning it wrong.

The Bowler

What it creates: the lower-body sequencing of the transition — the feel of the lead hip initiating the downswing before the arms and club arrive. Syncs the body to the club. After running The Bowler, the downswing should feel effortless: the body moves first, the club follows.

When to use it: when the downswing feels rushed or arm-driven, when the club gets stuck behind the body (blocks and pushes), or when the transition feels jerky rather than flowing.

90-90-90

What it creates: a position check. A reference for three key angles (lead arm, trail arm, spine / hip geometry) at specific points in the swing. It's less a feel drill than a calibration drill — a way to confirm the fundamentals are actually being built into the swing rather than being thought about in the head while the body does something else.

When to use it: periodically throughout a range session to confirm positions are holding. If 90-90-90 feels right, the fundamentals are intact. If it feels forced or unreachable, something in the preceding fundamentals is off.

“Drills are only useful if you know what success feels like. The drill names are shorthand between coach and student — if you haven't run them with me in person, running them off a written description is going to teach you something that isn't quite the drill. Come see me and we'll actually do them.”
Common misunderstanding

“I'll just find a YouTube video of these drills and run them at home.”

YouTube is full of videos of drills with these names that aren't actually these drills — or that are, but done with an intention different from the one Ryan teaches. A drill without the coaching context around it is just a physical motion. Bring the drill names into a lesson and we'll teach you what success feels like.

Chapter Eight

Where this fits

S&T, TPI, TrackMan, Vision54 — how these systems overlap and contradict.
›

Stack & Tilt is one of several modern systems that have shaped how golf gets taught in the 21st century. The others — TPI, TrackMan-driven instruction, Vision54, Kelley's component catalog — each make distinct claims about what matters. Understanding where they agree, where they disagree, and how a working coach weaves them together is the final piece of the puzzle.

The working coach's approach. No single system has the complete answer. Stack & Tilt gives you the body-first fundamentals. TPI gives you the physical screen that reveals why your body does what it does. TrackMan gives you the launch-monitor data that tells you whether the swing is actually working. Vision54 gives you the mental game that determines whether you can play your swing under pressure. You need all four — and a coach who knows when to lean on which.

Where S&T and TPI agree

TPI's “Body-Swing Connection” framework identifies common swing faults (early extension, loss of posture, sway, slide, reverse spine angle) and traces them back to physical limitations (pelvic mobility, thoracic rotation, hamstring length). Many of the fundamentals S&T prescribes — weight forward, stacked finish, arms straight — fix the exact faults TPI's physical screen predicts. Stack & Tilt and TPI don't contradict each other. They explain the same phenomenon from two different angles: one from the fundamentals of motion, one from the body's physical capacity to produce it.

Where TrackMan fits

TrackMan measures what actually happened. Clubhead speed, ball speed, smash factor, launch angle, spin rate, carry distance, attack angle, club path, face angle. When S&T says “weight forward produces ball-first contact and an in-to-out path,” TrackMan is what confirms or denies it. The numbers don't lie. A Stack & Tilt swing done correctly produces a measurable attack angle, a measurable club path, and a measurable spin signature. If the numbers don't match, the swing doesn't actually match the model, regardless of how it feels.

Where Vision54 fits

Pia Nilsson and Lynn Marriott's Vision54 works at the layer above the mechanics: pre-shot routine, Think Box / Play Box, emotional state, how to stay present under pressure. The best swing in the world is useless if you can't execute it on the first tee of a tournament. Vision54 is the mental and behavioral wrapping around the mechanical work S&T and TPI identify.

Where Kelley fits

Homer Kelley's The Golfing Machine (covered in Volume 02 of this series) catalogs the full universe of legal swing components — a more general framework than any single system. S&T can be understood as a specific, opinionated selection from Kelley's catalog: weight forward (Kelley's “stationary axis with forward pressure”), shoulder down (a particular plane choice), arms straight (Kelley's “flat left wrist” and “clubhead lag pressure point” at impact), hip tuck (a specific pivot pattern). S&T didn't invent any of these components — it assembled them into a coherent package.

“No one system has the complete answer. S&T gives you the body-first fundamentals. TPI explains why your body does what it does. TrackMan tells you whether it worked. Vision54 tells you whether you can do it under pressure. The job of a working coach is to weave them together for the player in front of them.”
Common misunderstanding

“I should pick one system and commit to it.”

The best coaches don't. They pull from all of them depending on what the player in front of them needs right now. A rangy tall 20-year-old with hypermobile hips needs different work than a 55-year-old with limited thoracic rotation. A scratch player who chokes on the first tee needs Vision54 more than another technical adjustment. Systems are tools; the player is the project.

Final takeaway

The four fundamentals in this book are a strong foundation. They describe how the human body delivers a golf club most reliably. But they are not the whole story. Pair them with physical awareness (TPI), data (TrackMan), and mental game (Vision54), and you've got the complete modern picture.

Now go hit some golf balls.

That's the primer. Eight chapters. Four fundamentals. One coach's interpretation of the system that changed how a generation of teachers looks at the golf swing.

What to do with this.

The fundamentals here are only useful when you try them. In order of highest-leverage to lowest:

  1. This week: Film yourself face-on hitting a 7-iron. Freeze the top of the backswing. Check where your head is. If it moved backward, your project for the month is Weight Forward.
  2. Next range session: Hit 20 balls thinking ONLY about keeping weight forward — head doesn't move back, lead foot stays heavy. Ignore everything else. It should feel wrong at first.
  3. Next week: Add Shoulder Down once the weight is locked in. Practice the no-club turn drill from Chapter 3 ten times before you hit a ball.
  4. Next month: Tuck the hips on half-swings until the finish holds without stumbling.
  5. Eventually: Come in for a lesson and we'll run through the named drills together and calibrate against TrackMan.

This is a primer — a plain-English translation of the Stack & Tilt swing system first codified by Andy Plummer and Mike Bennett in their 2009 book The Stack & Tilt Swing. The fundamentals and underlying biomechanical claims are theirs. The sequencing, drill selection, and teaching voice in this primer are Ryan's interpretation, refined over years of teaching adult golfers at Mad River.

A few callouts are marked as Coach's verification notes to flag places where careful readers diverge on specific claims (S&T attributions to particular pros get argued, and different S&T instructors emphasize different mechanics). If something reads wrong against your own understanding, mention it — the primer improves the more it gets pressure-tested.

For the full treatment, Plummer & Bennett's The Stack & Tilt Swing is still in print. Their golf.com content and the Stack & Tilt instructor network are the best places to go deeper if this primer sparks a genuine interest.

Glossary

Stack
Staying centered over the ball. The body doesn't pivot backwards during the backswing; the center of mass stays above the stance.
Tilt
The spine angle tilting toward the target through impact, not away from it. Opposite of the conventional "stay behind the ball" instruction.
Reverse Pivot
A swing flaw where the upper body leans toward the target at the top of the backswing while the weight stays on the lead side. Looks superficially like S&T but is a compensation, not a pattern.
Ball-First Contact
Hitting the ball before the ground. The clubhead's low point is forward of the ball. Produces solid contact, penetrating flight, and repeatable distance control.
Low Point
The bottom of the swing arc — where the clubhead is closest to the ground. In S&T, the low point is forward of the ball by design.
In-to-Out Path
The clubhead traveling from inside the target line to outside it through impact. Produces a draw bias (for a right-handed player). Opposite of "over the top," which is out-to-in.
Early Extension
A swing flaw where the hips thrust toward the ball at impact (rather than toward the target). The body comes up out of its spine angle, the arms have to fold to compensate, and contact becomes inconsistent. The hip tuck is the fix.
Chicken Wing
The lead arm bending at the elbow through impact and into the finish. Usually a compensation for a swing that was out of position earlier — most often a weight-back backswing.
Post Up
The lead leg straightening through impact as the hips move forward and up. Provides vertical clearance and contributes to clubhead speed via ground-reaction force.
Kinematic Sequence
The order in which body segments accelerate and decelerate during the downswing. In an efficient swing, the hips fire first, then the torso, then the arms, then the club. S&T's transition move is designed to preserve this sequence.
The Wrench Drill
A Ryan-taught drill that ingrains the hip-tuck pattern. Ask Ryan in person.
The Bowler
A Ryan-taught drill that ingrains lower-body sequencing in transition. Ask Ryan in person.
90-90-90
A position check Ryan uses to calibrate three specific swing geometries. A drill, a reference, and a conversation-shortener.
