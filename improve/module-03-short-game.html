How I teach the scoring game — by skill, not by book. Built on the shoulders of Dave Pelz, Stan Utley, and James Sieckmann.

Ryan's Framework

This module is Ryan Rinneard's original teaching framework for the short game. It is organized by skill — putting, chipping, pitching, bunker, lob — rather than by book, because that is how the short game is actually taught and played. It draws on the published work of Dave Pelz, Stan Utley, and James Sieckmann. Every source referenced is in print; links to buy their books are provided in each skill page.

This is not an authorized publication of any named teacher or system. The Pelz Golf Institute, Stan Utley's teaching practice, and James Sieckmann's short-game school are separate entities from Ryan's coaching practice. Concepts below are described in Ryan's own words with the original work of each teacher cited as the inspiration.

From the coach

Half your shots happen inside 100 yards. Lower that number to inside 50 and you get closer to two-thirds. Most amateurs spend 90 percent of their range time on full swings and wonder why their scores won't move. If you want to get better, work on the five skills below in the proportions they actually show up in your round.

I could have written five separate book primers — one for Pelz's Short Game Bible, one for Utley's Art of the Short Game, one for Sieckmann's Your Short Game Solution. Instead I wrote this. The short game is five different motions, and every good teacher has something to say about each one. Some teachers are better on putting; some on wedges; some on bunker. You deserve the best thinking on each skill, not three competing takes on all of it.

Read the skill page that matches what's costing you strokes right now. Take one drill to the practice green this week. Come back for the next one when it starts to hold.

— Ryan

Putting

Primary: Utley · supporting: Pelz

The arc vs. straight-back-straight-through debate, and why it doesn't actually matter for most amateurs. Green reading as a discipline. Lag control as the thing that actually lowers handicap. Six-foot-and-in as the skill that makes everything else forgiveable.

Skill 02

Chipping

Primary: Sieckmann · supporting: Pelz

The low-running shot every amateur should own before they ever learn to flop. One club, one swing, lots of different landing spots. Sieckmann's ball-first strike pattern applied to short shots.

Skill 03

Pitching

Primary: Sieckmann · supporting: Pelz

The partial-wedge distance matrix — how to build three reliable numbers with each wedge in your bag. Why trying to "feel" a 40-yard shot doesn't work and what to do instead. Pelz's arm-clock system meets Sieckmann's face-control patterns.

Skill 04

Greenside Bunker

Primary: Pelz · supporting: Sieckmann

The shot most amateurs fear and most good players welcome. Pelz's splash technique, why the club hits the sand and not the ball, and the shocking truth that bunker shots are easier than short pitches off tight lies once you know the motion.

Skill 05

The Lob Shot

Primary: Pelz · supporting: Sieckmann

The shot you should hit last, not first. When a high-soft shot is the only answer — over a bunker to a tight pin, off a firm lie to an elevated green — and how to execute it without the fear that most amateurs carry. Lie reading and club selection first; technique second.

Sources & further reading

Every skill page below lists specific chapters and concepts by author. If any of these ideas land for you, buy the original work. Each author has spent a lifetime teaching what fits in my summary in a paragraph.

  • Dave Pelz — The Short Game Bible (1999), Putt Like the Pros (1989). Engineer-turned-teacher who applied research methodology to scoring shots. The short-game-is-most-of-scoring data is his.
  • Stan Utley — The Art of Putting (2006), The Art of the Short Game (2007). Tour putter and coach; taught the modern face-rotation approach to putting and a feel-based short game.
  • James Sieckmann — Your Short Game Solution (2016). Tour short-game coach; built the distance-control system that many top players now use for wedges.
