The Basics — Grip, Stance, Alignment, Posture

The four things you set up before you swing. Get these right and the swing mostly figures itself out.

Every shot in golf starts from a standstill. That gives you a massive advantage: you can fix most beginner problems by fixing your setup — before the club ever moves. Four things matter. Learn them in this order.

1. Grip — how you hold the club

For a right-handed golfer: your left hand goes on the club first, at the top of the grip. Rotate that hand slightly clockwise so that when you look down, you can see two or three knuckles. The “V” formed between your thumb and forefinger should point up toward your right shoulder — not your chin.

Then add the right hand below it. Your right pinky either overlaps your left index finger (Vardon grip), interlocks with it (interlocking grip — what Tiger and Jack use), or sits next to it (ten-finger grip). All three work. Pick whichever feels natural.

Pressure: think 4 or 5 out of 10. Too tight and your forearms tense up; too loose and the club wobbles at the top. A beginner's #1 grip mistake is gripping the life out of it — soften the hands.

2. Stance — where your feet go

Stand with your feet about shoulder-width apart for a mid-iron. For longer clubs (driver, 3-wood), widen slightly. For shorter clubs (wedges, short irons), narrow slightly. Weight starts 50/50 between your feet.

Ball position: for most mid-irons, the ball sits in the middle of your stance. For the driver, move the ball forward (off your lead heel) so you catch it on the way up. For wedges, move it one ball back of center for cleaner contact.

3. Alignment — where you're aiming

This is the one beginners skip and it's why their shots go everywhere. Before every swing, pick a target. Then pick an intermediate target — a blade of grass, a discolored spot, a small leaf — about three feet in front of your ball on the line to your target.

Walk in and set the clubface so it's square to that intermediate target. Then set your feet, knees, hips, and shoulders parallel to that line — like a railroad track. The target line is the outer rail (where the ball goes). Your body line is the inner rail. Parallel left of the target. Most beginners aim their body straight at the target, which actually points them right of where they want to go.

4. Posture — the athletic position

Start standing tall with the club out in front of you. Bend forward from your hips, not your waist — stick your rear end out slightly, let your arms hang naturally underneath your shoulders. Soften the knees just a touch. Keep your back straight, chin up off your chest.

Good posture looks athletic, like a shortstop in baseball or a tennis player waiting for a serve. Bad posture looks hunched, rounded, collapsed. Fix posture and half your bad shots disappear, because your body can now rotate properly.

DAILY REPS

At home, without a ball: 10 setups a day for a week. Grip, stance, alignment, posture. Then freeze. Then step away. It's boring. It's also how every tour player builds it into muscle memory.

Course Etiquette — The Unspoken Rules

Every golfer follows these. None are in a rulebook. Do them and you'll be welcomed anywhere in the world.

Golf has more unwritten rules than written ones. They exist because the game is played on shared ground and every stroke you play affects the next group and the course itself. Master these and no one will guess you're new.

Where to put your bag or cart

  • Never drive your cart onto the tee box or onto a green, full stop.
  • Leave your bag behind or to the side of the green, never in front (you'll be in the next group's line of fire when they hit in).
  • Park your cart on the side of the green toward the next tee, so you walk to the cart without crossing back over anyone's path.
  • On the fairway, stay on paths where required. Where you can drive on grass, follow “cart rules for the day” posted at the pro shop (often "90-degree rule" — drive on the path, then cut 90 degrees across to your ball).

Repair what you damage

  • Fairway divots: replace the sod if it came out clean. Tamp it down with your foot. If it's chopped up, use the sand / seed mix most carts carry.
  • Ball marks on greens: if your ball landed on the green hard enough to leave a dent, fix it. Use a ball-mark repair tool or a tee. Insert around the edge, push gently toward the center of the mark, then tap flat with your putter. Never lift straight up — it tears the roots.
  • Bunkers: enter from the low side, rake every footprint and every swing mark before you leave. Leave the rake inside the bunker, parallel to its edge, or wherever the course specifies.

On the green

  • Don't step in anyone's line. Your line is the path from your ball to the hole. Walk around everyone else's lines.
  • The player farthest from the hole putts first. If multiple players are on the green, mark your ball (with a coin or small marker) if it's in someone's line.
  • Don't stand behind someone while they putt — either their back line (behind the ball) or their target line (behind the hole). Stand off to the side.
  • Remove your hat when you shake hands on the 18th green. Small ritual, huge signal of respect.

Noise, phones, and people

  • When another player is setting up or swinging, stand still, stay quiet. Even a cough can derail a shot.
  • Phones on silent. If you must take a call, walk well away from the group.
  • Drinking and playing go together in most places, but match the tone of your group and the course. Upscale clubs are quieter; public muni courses are livelier.
The golden rule: leave the course better than you found it, and treat the group behind you the way you'd want the group ahead of you to treat you.

Pace of Play — Keeping It Moving

The single biggest complaint about beginner groups is slow play. It's also the easiest thing to fix.

A foursome should play 18 holes in roughly four hours. That's 13 to 14 minutes per hole. If you're tracking more than 15 minutes per hole, you're slow — and the groups behind you start piling up. Here's how to stay in rhythm without feeling rushed.

Ready golf — the modern default

Traditional golf makes the player farthest from the hole hit next. Ready golf says whoever's ready and can safely hit, hits. This is the default for most casual rounds now. Don't wait for your turn — if you're ready and it's safe, go.

Think before it's your turn

  • Walk to your ball while others are setting up.
  • Pick your club on the way — don't start the yardage math once everyone's waiting.
  • Take one practice swing, not four. Then hit.
  • On the green, read your putt while others putt (from a spot that doesn't block them).

When to pick up your ball

This is the one beginners feel weird about — but every experienced golfer does it when appropriate. If you've taken double-par strokes on a hole (8 on a par 4, 10 on a par 5) and you're still not on the green, pick up. Write "blow-up" on your scorecard and go to the next tee.

Why: the round is about the 17 other holes too, and about the groups behind you. One hole of hero-ball costs them 15+ minutes. Taking double-par and moving on keeps the day enjoyable for everyone — including you.

Letting faster groups play through

If a faster group is waiting on you for two holes in a row, wave them through. Here's how:

  1. After you hit your tee shot, stand aside near the cart path and wave them up.
  2. They'll tee off first. Wait until they're safely down the fairway.
  3. Play the hole as normal. You'll see them finish the next hole as you're playing yours.

This is not a failure. It's good etiquette. Every golfer has been on both sides of this.

POSITION, NOT SPEED

"Pace of play" isn't about rushing. It's about position — stay with the group in front of you. If they're a hole ahead, speed up. If there's no one behind you, take your time.

On-Course Safety — How Not To Get Hit (Or Hit Someone)

A golf ball at full speed is a deadly projectile. Most accidents come from simple attention lapses. Know the rules and you're 99% of the way there.

The cardinal rule

NEVER HIT THIS SHOT

Never, ever hit a golf ball when another person is in front of you and within range — even if they seem out of the way. If there's any chance you could reach them, wait. It's not worth it.

This is the single most important rule in golf. A ball off the face of a decent amateur driver moves at 120+ mph. Players have been hospitalized or killed by this. Wait thirty seconds. Hit your shot.

Calling "FORE"

If your ball looks like it might hit someone — even a group you thought was out of range, even a group on a neighboring hole — yell "FORE!" loudly and immediately. Don't mumble. Don't shrug it off. Scream it.

If you're on the receiving end and someone yells "fore," duck and cover. Drop to one knee. Cover the back of your head and neck with your hands and arms. Turn away from the direction the shout came from — you don't want a ball to the face.

Staying out of the line of fire

  • When someone's about to swing, stand to the side, not behind the ball (shanks fly sideways) and never in front of the ball.
  • Watch every shot your playing partners hit. If it looks bad, shout "fore" yourself — don't wait for the hitter to do it.
  • If a group ahead is still on the fairway, wait. Even if you think you can't reach them, conditions (wind, downhill, a chunked shot that rolls 300 yards) can surprise you.

Other common hazards

  • Lightning: if you hear thunder, get off the course. Don't wait for the rain. Head to the clubhouse or lowest indoor shelter. Avoid isolated trees, open fields, metal carts and flagsticks.
  • Backswing room: stand at least 8–10 feet behind a swinger. People have been concussed by follow-throughs.
  • Water hazards: walking on wet grass by a pond? Slippery. Stay on paths where you can.

Booking a Tee Time & Your First Lesson

The logistics nobody explains to first-timers: when to book, what to bring, what to wear, and how to set up your first lesson.

Booking a tee time

Most public courses let you book online. A few ways:

  • The course's own website — biggest selection, most accurate availability.
  • GolfNow (app or website) — aggregator with many courses; sometimes discounted "hot deals" for off-peak times.
  • Calling the pro shop — still works, especially for same-day or specific group requests.

Book at least a few days out for weekends. Popular courses in summer fill up a week+ ahead. Early mornings and twilight (after 4 pm) are usually cheaper and faster.

What to bring

  • Clubs (you can rent if you don't have a set yet)
  • A dozen balls — even decent players lose balls. Beginners: bring two dozen.
  • Tees — plastic or wood, a handful per round
  • A glove (one, for your lead hand)
  • Divot repair tool and a ball marker (or just a coin)
  • Water, sunscreen, a hat, sunglasses
  • Cash for tips (bag drop, beverage cart — $2–5 is normal)

What to wear

Most courses have a dress code. Safe default:

  • Tops: collared shirt (polo). Some higher-end places require tucked-in.
  • Bottoms: khaki or golf pants, golf shorts (to the knee). Jeans are forbidden at most clubs. No athletic shorts or cargo shorts at private clubs.
  • Shoes: golf shoes ideally, athletic sneakers acceptable at public courses, no sandals or street shoes with hard soles.
  • Hat: optional but recommended for sun and visibility. Remove on the 18th green handshake.

When in doubt, check the course's website or call the pro shop. A 30-second phone call saves you being turned away at the bag drop.

How to Actually Have Fun Out There

The secret every beginner needs to hear: golf gets way more fun once you stop trying to prove anything.

Golf is the hardest sport most people will ever take up. The ball doesn't move. The club is designed to hit a tiny area. The stakes are entirely self-imposed. You'll hit bad shots. Lots of them. So will everyone you play with, including the pros (who miss about 40% of their fairways and 35% of their greens, for perspective).

The people who love golf long-term aren't the people who hit it the best. They're the people who figured out how to enjoy it while getting better. Three ideas:

1. Aim to enjoy, not to impress

Nobody you play with cares what you shoot. Really. They care whether you were fun to spend four hours with. Be fun — friendly, curious, not apologetic about bad shots, not gloating about good ones. That's it. Nobody on the course has been evaluating your swing in their head the whole round — they've been thinking about their own swing.

2. Find the one shot you love

Every beginner eventually hits one shot in a round that feels perfect. Maybe it's a putt that drops, a wedge that checks up tight, a tee shot that splits the fairway. That's the shot you come back for. Remember it. Tell your playing partners about it. Text a friend a photo.

The bad shots will outnumber the good ones 50:1 for a year. Doesn't matter. The 1-in-50 is enough to keep you chasing it.

3. Play with people better than you

The fastest way to improve and enjoy the game is to play with people two or three strokes better than you. You learn by watching. They're usually happy to share what they know. You also realize that even better players hit bad shots — so yours stop feeling catastrophic.

Avoid the trap of playing only with people way worse than you (makes you impatient) or way better (makes you feel hopeless). The middle zone is where the fun lives.

4. Keep score — but keep it loose

For your first year, don't obsess over strokes. Count pars and bogeys. Track how many greens you hit. Write down one good shot from each hole. Scorecards become a diary of improvement rather than a report card.

The truest thing anyone will tell you about golf: every great round has at least five bad shots in it, and every bad round has at least one great shot. Find the great ones. They're what you came for.
