Build a Tour Pre-Shot Routine

The same routine, every shot, under every condition. The bedrock of consistent golf.

A pre-shot routine is not superstition. It's a repeatable physical and mental sequence that puts your body and mind in the same state for every swing. Pros do it because it produces consistency. Amateurs who build one see immediate improvement — not because the routine itself adds skill, but because it strips away the variability that tanks your average performance.

The anatomy of a pro routine

Watch Adam Scott, Justin Rose, or Rory McIlroy on any tee. The routine from "I'm going to hit this shot" to "club is moving" takes between 15 and 25 seconds. Inside that window, four phases happen in the same order, every time:

  1. Assess (3-5 sec) — yardage, wind, lie, pin, trouble. Pick the shot.
  2. Decide (2-3 sec) — one club, one target, one shot shape. Commit.
  3. Visualize (3-5 sec) — see the ball flight in your mind's eye. Feel it.
  4. Execute (5-8 sec) — stand behind the ball, walk in, set up, pull the trigger.

The behind-the-ball moment

The most important single component: a quiet, brief moment standing directly behind the ball looking at your target. Pros do this on every shot. They pick their exact intermediate target (a blade of grass, a discolored spot, a leaf) about 3 feet in front of the ball on the target line. Then they walk in and aim the clubface at that intermediate — it's much easier than aiming at a target 150 yards away.

The commitment trigger

Every pro has a physical trigger that starts the swing — a slight head swivel toward the target and back, a forward press, a waggle, a breath. The trigger is non-negotiable. Once the trigger fires, the shot happens. No second-guessing, no re-gripping, no stepping off. If you want to step off, do it before the trigger.

YOUR BASELINE ROUTINE

Pick yardage and club → stand behind ball → pick intermediate target → one practice swing with intent → walk in → align feet/face → one waggle → breath → swing. Practice this until it's automatic.

How to install it

Hit your next 50 range balls using the full routine on every single one. It'll feel slow and strange. That's the point — you're building a motor program. By ball 50 it starts to feel natural. Bring it to the course the next round and your scoring will immediately stabilize. The pros don't have a special routine; they just do theirs on every single shot, for decades.

Think Box · Play Box · Memory Box

Pia Nilsson and Lynn Marriott's Vision54 framework for separating analysis from execution — the clearest mental map in golf.

Pia Nilsson and Lynn Marriott, the Swedish instructors behind Vision54 (and coaches to Annika Sörenstam, Suzann Pettersen, and countless others), built a mental framework that changed how tour players think about each shot. Every shot has a future (planning), a present (execution), and a past (recovery). Treat them as separate "boxes" and the transitions between them become deliberate.

The Think Box

Behind the ball. Analytical. Here you process everything: yardage, wind, elevation, hazards, pin position, lie, comfort level. You calculate. You decide. You commit. All thinking happens here — and only here.

The Think Box ends when you step across an imaginary line toward the ball. That line is the doorway out of analytical mode.

The Play Box

Over the ball. Athletic. This is the state Pia and Lynn call your "play box" — a sensory, intuitive mode where you feel the shot, trust your body, and react. No more thinking. No more analyzing. You've made the decision; your job now is to execute.

Nilsson and Marriott's research shows elite players stay in the Play Box for about 4-7 seconds from set-up to impact. Any longer and doubt creeps in. Any shorter and you've rushed. Four to seven seconds is the sweet spot.

The transition that matters most. The step from Think Box to Play Box is where amateurs fail. They keep thinking over the ball — "don't hit it left," "keep your head down," "remember the wind." Those thoughts belong in the Think Box. Once you've crossed the line, you're an athlete reacting, not an engineer calculating.

The Memory Box

After the shot. Reflective. Now you take a moment — 5 to 10 seconds — to process what happened. A good shot gets savored: feel it, store the sensation, notice exactly what worked. A bad shot gets released: acknowledge it, learn one thing from it, and let it go before you reach the next ball.

This is the box most amateurs skip entirely — and it's why a single bad hole becomes a bad nine, which becomes a bad round. Without a deliberate Memory Box, negative shots pile up and poison your Think Box on every subsequent shot.

The walking rule

Vision54 teaches that the walk from one shot to the next is the Memory Box for the last shot and the Think Box prep for the next. Use that walk. Don't fill it with small talk or phone-checking or negative self-chatter. Feel the last shot, release it, then start gathering information about the next one.

ONE-SENTENCE SUMMARY

Think hard behind the ball. Don't think over it. Process it after. Repeat.

Further reading

Pia Nilsson and Lynn Marriott have written four books on this framework — Every Shot Must Have a Purpose, The Game Before the Game, Play Your Best Golf Now, and Be A Player. Every one is worth your time. The concept distills to three boxes, but the application is a lifetime of craft.

Stop Taking Your Playing Partner's Advice (and how to politely ask them to stop)

Unsolicited swing tips between shots are the silent score-killer of amateur golf.

Here's a scenario: you pull your drive. Your buddy, trying to help, says "you came over the top — try swinging more inside-out." You think about that for the next three swings. You now have a new swing thought your pro didn't install, overlaid on whatever thought you were actually working on. The rest of the round is a mechanical mess.

This happens to every amateur golfer constantly. And it's almost always well-intentioned — your partners want to help. The problem is the timing (mid-round is the worst time to install a new swing thought) and the expertise (they're usually wrong about the cause). Here's how to handle it without wrecking the friendship.

Why partner advice hurts your game

  • It's a diagnosis from the wrong angle. They see your ball flight; they don't see your impact. The cause they're naming is usually the symptom, not the actual issue.
  • It overrides your current swing thought. You came into the round with one thing to focus on. A new thought crowds it out.
  • It pulls you into the Think Box mid-Play Box. You're now analyzing between shots when you should be releasing and resetting.
  • It damages trust. If you start listening to every tip, your practice work loses authority in your own head.

The three scripts (polite)

Script 1 — Pre-emptive, at the first tee

"Hey — just a heads up, I'm working on a specific thing with my coach this season, so I'm going to be in my own head a bit. If I look frustrated, don't worry about me. And if you see something obvious in my swing, would you mind holding it until we get off the course? I'd love to hear it over a beer after, but mid-round I'll stay with what my pro has me on."

This is 95% effective. Most people respect it immediately.

Script 2 — In-the-moment, gentle

"Appreciate you — I've got a thing I'm working on with Ryan. I'll make a note and we can chat after the round."

Simple, warm, clear. No argument, no eye roll, no resentment. Move on.

Script 3 — For the persistent advisor

"You're a great friend but a terrible coach. Let's just golf."

Delivered with a smile. Works every time if the person has a sense of humor.

What to do with tips you DO receive

Don't argue with them. Say "thanks," nod, and physically write it down in your phone notes at the next green. That does two things: it gets the thought out of your head (you're not trying to remember it) and it commits to thinking about it later with your coach. Nine times out of ten, when you review the tip at home it's not worth acting on. The tenth time it might be a small insight worth exploring — bring it to your next lesson.

A note on your own mouth. If you're the one giving tips — stop. Even if you're right. The golfer you're helping didn't ask, and your tip is almost certainly landing during their Play Box. Be a great playing partner by being a quiet one.

Quiet the Mental Chatter — Stop Beating Yourself Up on the Course

Self-talk is the most modifiable factor in your mental game. Here's how to rebuild it.

The voice in your head after a bad shot — the one that says "you always do that," "you're terrible with this club," "there's no way you break 85 today" — is the voice that decides how the rest of the round goes. Sport psychologists call this negative self-talk, and it's the single most treatable mental-game problem in amateur golf.

What the research actually shows

Every major study on athletic performance and self-talk finds the same thing: golfers who use instructional or positive self-talk perform measurably better under pressure than golfers who don't. The effect is especially strong in closed-skill sports where you stand over the ball with time to think — which is almost every shot in golf.

You cannot silence the voice entirely. You can change what it says.

The three scripts to install

After a bad shot — the "reset phrase"

Pick one short phrase — 3 to 5 words — that you say to yourself the moment a bad shot happens. Something like "next shot's the one" or "release it, move on" or "that was information." Say it out loud if you're alone, silently if you're not. The phrase does two things: interrupts the negative spiral, and cues your body to physically relax.

Before a hard shot — the "permission phrase"

Over a tight lie, a dangerous pin, a must-make putt, the fear voice shows up: "don't hit it in the water." You need a counter. Something like "this is the one I practice for," or "I'm allowed to hit a great shot here," or simply "commit." Permission phrases turn fear into willingness.

Between holes — the "ledger check"

Walking to the next tee, ask yourself: "what's one good thing from the last hole?" Not "what went wrong" — good things. The reset isn't denial; you're just refusing to let bad outcomes accumulate unopposed. If nothing went well, your one good thing can be "I kept my rhythm" or "I didn't get angry."

THE 24-SECOND RULE

Give yourself exactly 24 seconds (the walk from ball to the next shot) to be frustrated about a bad one. Then it's gone. Professionals do this. You can too.

The story you're telling yourself

Over 18 holes you narrate your own golf round. "I'm grinding today." "I'm striping it." "I'm falling apart." "I'm a choker." The narration becomes the round. A player who tells themselves "I'm grinding" keeps grinding. A player who tells themselves "I'm falling apart" keeps falling apart. You get to pick the narration.

The anger cost

Every outburst (club slam, yell, mumbled curse) elevates your heart rate, tightens your grip pressure, and degrades your fine motor control for the next 5 to 10 minutes. Literally. The next shot after an emotional blow-up is, statistically, your worst shot of the round. This is measurable. The calmest players aren't less competitive — they're just protecting the next shot.

If this feels like you. The 24-second rule combined with one reset phrase is enough for 80% of players to break the spiral. If negative self-talk is deeper than golf — tied to self-worth or identity — consider working with a sport psychologist. It's worth it.
