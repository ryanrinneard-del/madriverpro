01 · Grip

How to hold the club

The club is your connection to the ball. If you hold it wrong, nothing else matters. This is the single biggest reason new golfers slice — and the easiest thing to fix.

Here's how, step by step:

  1. Put the club on the ground in front of you, face pointing at your target.
  2. Pick it up with your lead hand (left hand if you're right-handed). The grip rests in your fingers, not in your palm. Your thumb points down the shaft, slightly right of center.
  3. Add your trail hand (right hand). The life line of your right palm covers your left thumb. Your two hands feel like one unit.
  4. Grip pressure: like holding a tube of toothpaste with the cap off. Firm enough not to drop it. Soft enough not to squeeze any out.
Quick check
Look down at your hands. You should see 2 knuckles (maybe 2.5) on your lead hand. If you see 3 or 4, your grip is too strong. If you see 1 or 0, it's too weak. Two knuckles is the sweet spot.
Why this matters
About 90% of beginner slices come from a weak grip (too few knuckles visible). Fix the grip, half your bad shots fix themselves.
02 · Stance

How to stand to the ball

Your setup is half the shot. If you stand wrong, you can't swing right. This takes 30 seconds to learn and saves you years of guessing.

  • Feet: Shoulder-width apart for most clubs. A bit wider for the driver. A bit narrower for short wedges.
  • Knees: A small bend. Not a squat. You should feel athletic, not crouched.
  • Hips: Tilt forward from your hips (not your waist) until your arms hang naturally in front of you. Your chest is over the ball.
  • Ball position: For short irons (pitching wedge, 9-iron), the ball is in the middle of your stance. For mid-irons, slightly forward. For the driver, just inside your lead heel.
  • Shoulders: Parallel to your target line. Most amateurs aim their feet correctly but their shoulders point 20 yards right — that's the pulls and slices nobody can figure out.
Quick check
Hold a club across your shoulders like a bar. Look in a mirror from behind. If the club points at your target, you're square. If it points right of target, you're aimed right and don't know it.
03 · Pace of Play

How to move at the right pace

The single biggest complaint at every golf club in the world: slow play. Learn this now and you'll be welcome anywhere you go.

  • Be ready when it's your turn. Know your yardage. Pick your club. Walk to your ball while others are playing.
  • Watch your ball. Watch others' balls. If a shot goes into the woods, help find it — even if it's not yours.
  • Walk with purpose. If the cart is moving, move with it. Don't stand around waiting.
  • 4 hours is the target for 18 holes. 4.5 hours is the limit. Over 5 is a problem.
  • If you're lost on a hole, pick up. Beginner max score is double bogey. Write it down, go to the next tee.
  • Let faster groups play through. If the group behind is waiting more than once a hole, wave them up. It's not rude — it's polite.
Why this matters
Nobody remembers your score from your first round. Everyone remembers whether you held up the course. Be the player who moves.
04 · The First Tee

When you're nervous on the first tee

Everyone is nervous on the first tee. Tour pros. Tour winners. Your coach. It's normal. Here's what works.

  • Take one practice swing. Just one. Feel your balance. Feel the rhythm.
  • Pick a big target, not a small one. Aim at the middle of the fairway, not at a pin. Big targets forgive misses.
  • Breathe. Inhale for 4 counts, exhale for 6. Do it twice. It tells your nervous system you're fine.
  • Commit to the swing. Whatever happens, happens. A fully committed bad swing beats a half-committed good swing every time.
Important
Nobody on the first tee is watching you. They're all thinking about their own shot. The person who cares most about your first-tee shot is you. And you can let that go.
05 · Ball Flight

Why the ball curves

Golf balls don't fly perfectly straight. Ever. They always curve a little. Understanding why stops you from fighting the game.

Simple rule: the ball mostly goes where the clubface points at impact.

  • Face open (pointing right of target, for a right-handed player) = ball starts right, curves right. That's a slice.
  • Face closed (pointing left of target) = ball starts left, curves left. That's a hook.
  • Face matched to your swing path = ball goes roughly straight.
Fix a slice
Strengthen your grip. Rotate both hands slightly to the right on the club (for a right-handed player). You'll now see 2–3 knuckles on your lead hand instead of 1. This pre-sets a more closed face and the slice fades.
Fix a hook
Weaken your grip. Rotate hands slightly to the left. You'll now see 1–2 knuckles instead of 3. Hook disappears.
06 · Short Game

The short game

More strokes get saved from 50 yards and in than anywhere else on the course. Start practicing the short game early — it's where scores live.

Chipping — when you're just off the green

  • Use a pitching wedge or 9-iron. Don't reach for a sand wedge unless you have to carry a bunker.
  • Narrow stance. Feet close together.
  • Ball slightly back of center.
  • Most of your weight on your lead foot — keep it there the whole swing.
  • Small backswing, small follow-through. Let the club do the work.

Putting

  • Eyes directly over the ball.
  • Ball in the middle of your stance.
  • Shoulders swing the club. Your hands are passengers.
  • Speed, not line. A putt hit at the right speed that misses by 2 feet beats a putt on the perfect line that runs 10 feet past.
Why this matters
A beginner who can chip and putt shoots 10 strokes lower than a beginner who can't, without changing anything else. Short game is the fastest route to lower scores, period.
07 · Practice

How to practice so it actually sticks

The hard truth: most amateur practice doesn't produce learning. Here's how to make every range visit count.

  • 15 minutes beats 1 hour. Short sessions, spread across the week, work better than one big beat-the-ball session once a weekend.
  • Change clubs every shot. The course isn't block practice — you never hit two of the same club in a row. Practice the way you'll play.
  • Take breaks. Hit 5 balls. Pause. Hit 5 more. Your brain needs the space to absorb.
  • Pick ONE thing. Not three. Not five. One. Stick with it for a week before moving on.
  • Film yourself. What you feel and what you actually do are often wildly different. A phone on a tripod is the single best teaching tool in golf.
08 · Course Day

What to bring to the course

Going to a real course for the first time? Here's everything you'll want in your bag and your trunk.

  • Clubs. Any set works. You don't need the newest or most expensive.
  • Balls — at least a dozen. You will lose some. It's completely fine. Everyone does.
  • Tees. A pocketful. They disappear faster than you think.
  • Glove. One per round, on your lead hand.
  • Water and a snack. Four hours is a long time without food.
  • Hat and sunglasses. Yes, even in Canada, even when it's cloudy.
  • Sunscreen. You're outside for four hours. Please.
  • Golf shoes if you have them. Running shoes work in a pinch.
  • A collared shirt. Most courses require it. No denim, no t-shirts at most clubs.
  • A few dollars cash. For snacks at the turn, a small tip, or extra balls in the pro shop.
One more thing
Bring patience with yourself. Your first real round will feel overwhelming — there's a lot going on, people watching, rules to remember. That's normal. Just play, keep moving, and enjoy being outside. Everything else gets easier.
