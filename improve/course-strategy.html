CATEGORY 06 · COURSE STRATEGY

The game within the game.

Two golfers, same swing, same course — one shoots 82, the other shoots 89. The difference isn't talent. It's decisions. Scott Fawcett's DECADE Golf is the framework that separates those golfers, and it works better for a 38-handicap than for a tour pro.

DECADE
Distance· Eliminate· Club· Aim· Decide· Execute

A decision system developed by Scott Fawcett from PGA Tour ShotLink data. It doesn't ask you to hit better shots. It asks you to make better choices with the shots you already have. The result is usually 3–5 strokes a round without changing a thing about your swing.

You are not as accurate as you think — and that's the point.

Here's the hardest pill in golf: every shot you've ever hit is a dot in a cloud around your intended target. You don't have one swing. You have a shot pattern. A cloud of outcomes, some close, some wide, all statistically predictable once you have enough rounds logged.

The average 15-handicap's shot cloud from 150 yards isn't a tight circle around the flag. It's a scatter pattern 44 yards wide — roughly the width of a basketball court.

Once you accept your real shot pattern, three things happen:

  • You stop aiming at tucked pins.
  • You stop trying to carry hazards by a yard.
  • You stop compounding mistakes with hero recovery shots.

You start playing your game instead of a game that doesn't exist. DECADE is simply the discipline of doing that, shot by shot, all the way around a golf course.

"The best decision on a golf course is almost never the most exciting one. It's the one that eliminates the big number." — the core DECADE insight

How to use this page

Each letter of DECADE is a decision that happens in your head before the swing. Tap any letter below to expand the full breakdown — the common mistake, the DECADE way, and why it works. Then read the worked example to see all six letters applied to a single tee shot.

D
Distance
Know your real number — not your career-best number.
›

Every club in your bag has two distances: the one it goes when you strike it perfectly, and the one it averages. DECADE works only with the average.

Common mistake: "My 7-iron goes 150." What you mean is your best 7-iron goes 150. Your average 7-iron goes 140. You play for 150 and come up short 70% of the time.
The DECADE way: For each club, know (a) your average carry and (b) your rough spread. On course, you play to average. Anything better than that is a bonus. Anything worse is still in your expected range.

Why it works

Short of the pin is almost always worse than long of the pin on an average green. Playing to your real average means you're putting from behind the hole instead of chipping from short of it.

E
Eliminate one side
Look at the hole. Pick the side you cannot go.
›

Before you think about aim, you eliminate. Every hole has a death side — OB, water, a lost-ball-in-the-trees situation. Your first job is to identify it and make sure no version of this shot ends up there.

Common mistake: Trying to flirt with the trouble to get the best angle in. "If I just tickle it past the water I'll have a wedge." The odds of that perfect flirt are lower than you think. The cost of missing it is ruinous.
The DECADE way: You decide which miss is simply not an option. You then build the whole rest of your decision around making sure the death side is outside your shot pattern — not just outside your straight shot.

The phrase to use

Out loud, on the tee: "I cannot go right" (or left). Say it before you pick a club. Everything downstream flows from that one sentence.

C
Club selection
Pick the club whose whole shot pattern fits the safe zone.
›

This is the hinge of the whole system. You're not picking the club that could reach the pin. You're picking the club whose full shot cloud fits inside the fairway, the green, or the safe-miss area.

Common mistake: Taking driver on a tight par 4 because "I can carry the fairway bunker." Your best shot carries it. Your average shot catches the lip. Your worst shot is in the sand or through the fairway into trees.
The DECADE way: Mentally overlay your shot pattern for every club option onto the hole. The right club is the one whose wide end — your outlier miss — still lands somewhere playable.

A simple rule

If the wide end of your pattern with driver reaches trouble, step down one club. It's not weakness. It's math.

A
Aim at the center of your pattern
Never at the pin. The pin is a decoration.
›

Here's the line that changes everything: your aim point is not a target, it's the center of a cloud. You're placing the center of your dispersion on the map such that every point in the cloud is acceptable.

Common mistake: Aim at the flag. Worst miss ends up in the greenside bunker or over the green. You short-sided yourself before you even swung.
The DECADE way: If the pin is on the right with a bunker right, aim at the center-left of the green. Your best shot finishes near the pin. Your average shot is middle of the green. Your worst shot is the fat side fringe — a routine chip, not a buried-lie disaster.

The "fat side" rule

There's always a fat side of every green — the larger, safer half. Aim there every single time until a ball in the cloud is no longer at risk. Over 18 holes, this one change alone saves most amateurs 2–3 strokes.

D
Decide — fully.
The last thought before the swing is commitment, not math.
›

You've done the math. You know the club, you know the aim, you know why. The decision ends in the think box. When you step over the ball, the thinking is done.

Common mistake: Standing over the ball re-evaluating. "Maybe I should take one less." "Maybe I should aim a hair right." Second-guessing at address pulls the swing off-plane as reliably as any technique flaw.
The DECADE way: If you feel doubt over the ball, step off. Walk back behind the ball, redo the decision, and come in again clean. Then commit. You cannot execute what you haven't committed to.

The simplest test

Can you say the shot out loud in one sentence? "7-iron, fat-side left, full swing." If you can, you're ready. If you can't, you're not decided yet.

E
Execute — trust the pattern.
You chose the pattern for a reason. Now go.
›

Execute means make your normal swing at your chosen target and accept whatever part of the pattern today's shot lands in.

Common mistake: Trying to steer the ball at the last second — a defensive swing that pulls or blocks because you're no longer swinging freely.
The DECADE way: The decision was built to handle the bad ends of your pattern. If today's shot is one of your outliers, that outlier was already accounted for. You picked this line because even the worst miss is acceptable. Swing through the target with zero steering.

What success looks like

A round of DECADE golf has a lot of "meh" shots that finish in perfectly fine positions. Boring is the goal. Boring wins.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Par 4 · 390 yards · Pond up the right off the tee

One drive, six letters. Here's the thinking the DECADE golfer does in the 15 seconds before pulling a club.
D
Distance. Your driver carries 240 on average, 260 if you flush it. Your 3-wood carries 220, much tighter spread.
E
Eliminate. The pond sits 260 yards out on the right. Out loud: "I cannot go right."
C
Club. Driver's wide-right end extends well into the pond's airspace at that carry. 3-wood's wide-right end stops short of it. Club: 3-wood.
A
Aim. Fairway is 40 yards wide. Aim down the left-center. Worst left-miss is light rough — playable. Worst right-miss stays short of the pond.
D
Decide. Said out loud: "3-wood, left-center, full swing." Step into the shot.
E
Execute. Normal swing. Trust the pattern.
Outcome: You're 170 yards out from the fairway. Clean wedge to 20 feet, two-putt par. Compare that to the aggressive driver: 1-in-5 you make par, 4-in-5 it's a double-bogey fishing expedition. Over a season, that's the difference between an 85 handicap and a 90.

How to start playing DECADE on your next round

You don't need a Fawcett subscription or a PhD in statistics. You need four habits:

  1. Know your average distance for every club. Map My Bag is there for this. Update it every 2-3 weeks as your game changes.
  2. Say the death side out loud before every shot. "I cannot go right." It sounds silly. It works.
  3. Aim at the center of the fat side — not the flag. For one entire round, don't aim at a single pin. Aim at the middle of every green. Count your score at the end. The number will surprise you.
  4. Review big numbers after the round. For every double or worse, ask: was that a bad swing or a bad decision? The honest answer is usually the decision.
"Smart golfers don't play scared. They play clear. Massive difference — and you can feel it in the way they stand over the ball." — Ryan Rinneard, on-course coaching notes

DECADE isn't conservative. It's confident. Once you've done the math, there's nothing left to be nervous about. You picked this shot because even the bad version of it is fine. Step up, make your swing, and watch your score drop without working on a single technique thing.

This is the game within the game. Once you see it, you can't unsee it.
