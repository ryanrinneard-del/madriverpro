Five Lessons: The Fundamentals That Repeat.

Grip, stance, plane, and the two-part swing — the book that said any golfer could build a repeating swing.
Inspired by Ben Hogan's Five Lessons: The Modern Fundamentals of Golf
Independent Interpretation

This primer is Ryan Rinneard's independent educational summary of the fundamentals Ben Hogan laid out (with Herbert Warren Wind) in the 1957 book Five Lessons: The Modern Fundamentals of Golf. It is not an official Hogan publication, nor authorized by his estate. Concepts below are described in Ryan's own words; the original work is attributed to Hogan throughout.

From the coach

In the Practical Golf primer I said that if you ask any serious golf teacher to name the most important instruction book ever written, you get one of two answers: Jacobs, or Hogan. This is the Hogan.

Five Lessons is a different kind of book from Practical Golf. Jacobs taught you how to diagnose — read the ball, find the cause. Hogan taught you what to build — a specific, repeatable swing assembled from four fundamentals he insisted any golfer could learn. Jacobs is the doctor. Hogan is the architect. You need both.

What makes Five Lessons remarkable is the claim Hogan opened with: that the average golfer, applying these fundamentals, was “entirely capable” of breaking 80. Coming from the most famously obsessive ball-striker who ever lived — a man who reportedly “dug his swing out of the dirt” over years of relentless practice — that was a generous, almost radical thing to say. He believed the fundamentals were teachable. Not magic. Not talent. Learnable positions, learnable in order.

This primer is for the recreational player who owns the book, or has heard about the famous “pane of glass” image, and wants the four fundamentals in plain modern English before sitting with Hogan's own words. Eight chapters: why the book still matters, the grip (Hogan's obsession — and ours), stance and posture, the plane, the two parts of the swing, the much-misunderstood idea of supination, and where Hogan sits in modern teaching.

One honest note up front: a few of Hogan's specific feels — especially around the hands and the idea of “pronation” and “supination” — have been refined or reinterpreted in the seventy years since. I'll flag those clearly. The fundamentals, though — grip, plane, sequence — are as true today as they were in 1957.

— Ryan

Chapter One

Why Five Lessons still matters

Hogan's claim: the fundamentals are teachable, and any golfer can build a repeating swing.
›

Ben Hogan is famous for two things: being one of the greatest ball-strikers in the history of the game, and being famously private about how he did it. There was a long-running idea that Hogan had a “secret” he never fully shared. Whether or not that's true, Five Lessons is the closest thing to Hogan opening the hood — and what he chose to share wasn't a secret at all. It was four fundamentals, taught in order, that he believed anyone could learn.

The key idea. Hogan's central claim was almost shocking coming from him: the average golfer, applying these fundamentals, is “entirely capable of building a repeating swing and breaking 80.” He didn't think great ball-striking required rare talent. He thought it required correct fundamentals, built in the right sequence, and practiced with understanding.

What “modern fundamentals” meant in 1957

The full title is Five Lessons: The Modern Fundamentals of Golf. The word “modern” mattered. Hogan was arguing against an older school of teaching that treated the swing as a mysterious, individual, feel-based thing — something you either had or you didn't. He wanted to replace mystery with structure: here are the positions, here is the order, here is what each one does.

The book is built around four fundamentals, which the title counts as “five lessons” because the swing itself is split into two parts:

  1. The Grip
  2. Stance and Posture
  3. The First Part of the Swing (the waggle and backswing)
  4. The Second Part of the Swing (the downswing)

And running underneath all four is the concept Hogan made famous — the plane, the imaginary pane of glass that the swing travels on. We give it its own chapter because it's the idea most people remember from the book, and the one most people half-understand.

Why the order matters

Hogan was deliberate about sequence. He started with the grip because nothing downstream can be correct if the grip is wrong. Then stance and posture, because the swing has to start from a sound platform. Then the backswing, then the downswing — because you can't practice the second half of a motion until the first half is repeatable.

This is the same logic behind how we teach at the academy, and the same logic behind why the Friday Morning Series starts with the grip. Hogan would have approved entirely.

“Good golf begins with a good grip.”

That's not a throwaway line. It's the organizing principle of the entire book. Hogan spent more pages on the grip than on any other single topic, and the next chapter is going to do the same.

Chapter Two

The Grip

Hogan's obsession. The most detailed grip instruction ever written — and why he started here.
›

No one in the history of golf instruction has written about the grip with the obsessive precision Hogan brought to it. He described where each finger sits, the pressure points, the angles, the way the two hands must work as one connected unit. To a casual reader it can feel like overkill. It isn't. Hogan understood that the grip is the only thing connecting the golfer to the club, and that everything the swing produces is filtered through it.

The key idea. The two hands must function as a single unit, not two independent hands fighting each other. A correct grip makes the clubface behave through impact without conscious manipulation. A poor grip forces the rest of the swing to spend its entire effort compensating for a clubface that won't square up on its own.

The lead hand (left hand, for a right-handed player)

Hogan placed the club in the lead hand so it ran from the base of the little finger across to the middle of the index finger — more in the fingers than the palm. When the hand closes over the top, the club is held mostly by the last three fingers. The thumb sits on top, slightly to the trail side of center. Looking down, you should see roughly two knuckles.

Hogan was emphatic that the lead hand's last three fingers provide the primary grip pressure. The pad at the heel of the hand sits on top of the grip and acts almost like a lid. This combination — fingers holding, heel pad on top — is what lets the wrist hinge correctly and keeps the club secure at the top of the backswing.

The trail hand (right hand)

The trail hand goes on so that its lifeline nestles over the lead thumb — the two hands lock together. Hogan favored the overlapping (Vardon) grip, with the trail-hand little finger riding on top of, or interlocked with, the lead-hand index finger. The trail hand's pressure comes mostly from the middle two fingers. The thumb and forefinger stay relatively light — Hogan worried that an active trail thumb and forefinger would take over the swing and throw the club from the top.

The Vs, and grip “strength”

The V formed by thumb and forefinger on each hand should point roughly toward the trail shoulder/chin area. Hogan's grip is often described as slightly “weaker” (more rotated toward the target) than what many players use today — partly because Hogan, early in his career, fought a hook, and his grip was one of the tools he used to tame it.

This is one place to take Hogan as principle, not gospel. The exact rotation of the grip that's right for you depends on what your clubface does. A chronic slicer and a chronic hooker should not have identical grips. What's universal in Hogan's teaching is the structure — fingers not palm, two hands as one unit, light trail thumb and forefinger. The precise rotation is the dial you adjust to your own ball flight (which is exactly the Jacobs diagnostic from Volume 01).

The grip mistakes Hogan was fighting
  • Club in the palm of the lead hand. Kills wrist hinge, opens the face at the top, makes release impossible.
  • The two hands working against each other. A lead hand set one way and a trail hand set another — they fight through impact instead of cooperating.
  • An overactive trail hand. Thumb and forefinger gripping hard, taking over the downswing and throwing the clubhead early.
  • Grip pressure too tight everywhere. Hogan wanted firm pressure in specific fingers, not a white-knuckle clench across both hands.
“The hands swing the clubhead. The grip is how you give them something honest to swing.”
Drill — Build It in the Mirror, Not on the Range

Hogan's grip chapter is best practiced indoors, slowly, with no ball and no swing. Stand at a mirror. Place the lead hand — check the fingers, the heel pad, the two knuckles. Add the trail hand — lifeline over lead thumb, light thumb and forefinger. Take the hands off. Do it again. Ten times a night for two weeks and the correct grip stops feeling foreign. This is the single highest-return fifteen minutes in golf, and it costs nothing.

If it feels wrong, that's expected
A corrected grip always feels strange at first — that's not a sign it's wrong, it's a sign your old grip was wrong long enough to feel normal. Give a new grip two weeks of honest reps before you judge it. This is exactly why our Friday Morning Series spends a full session on it: eyes-on coaching gets you past the “this feels weird” wall that makes most players quit and revert.

Chapter Three

Stance and Posture

The address position Hogan built everything on. Width, ball position, the bend from the hips.
›

Hogan treated the address position as part of the swing, not a preamble to it. The way you stand to the ball pre-loads what the swing can do. Get it right and the motion has a chance; get it wrong and the motion spends itself compensating.

The key idea. A sound address position is athletic, balanced, and built to turn. Hogan wanted a player who could rotate fully both ways from a stable base — not reach for the ball, not crowd it, not lock up.

Stance width

Hogan tied stance width to the club. For the driver, the insides of the heels were roughly shoulder-width apart. For the shorter irons, the stance narrowed progressively. The logic: longer clubs need a wider base for a bigger turn and more speed; shorter clubs need a narrower base for control and a steeper, more precise strike.

Posture — the bend from the hips

Hogan's posture was a bend forward from the hip joints, not a slump of the spine. The back stays relatively straight. The knees are flexed slightly — enough to be athletic, not so much that you're sitting. The arms hang down naturally from the shoulders. The chin is up off the chest so the lead shoulder has room to turn under it.

A useful Hogan image: you should feel ready — the same athletic readiness as a shortstop waiting for the ball, or a player about to return a serve. Weight balanced, not on the toes, not on the heels.

Ball position and alignment

Hogan played the ball forward in the stance for longer clubs and progressively back toward center for shorter clubs — the same principle Jacobs taught for controlling where the club's low point meets the ball. For alignment, the body lines — feet, knees, hips, shoulders — should run parallel to the target line, not aimed directly at the target. A player aimed with the body at the target is actually aimed well right (for a right-hander), and the swing has to come across the line to rescue it.

Common posture faults Hogan was correcting
  • Slumping from the lower back instead of bending from the hips. Restricts the turn and pushes the swing toward the heel.
  • Chin buried on the chest — the lead shoulder has nowhere to turn.
  • Reaching for the ball — arms not hanging naturally, which flattens the swing and ruins balance.
  • Too much knee flex — “sitting down” to the ball kills rotation and power.
Drill — The Two-Photo Posture Check

Have someone photograph you at address from two angles: down-the-line (behind you, along the target line) and face-on. You're checking: spine bent forward from the hips with a relatively straight back, slight knee flex, arms hanging straight down under the shoulders, chin up, body lines parallel to the target. Compare to Hogan's own address photos — they're widely available and still the gold standard.

Chapter Four

The Plane

The pane of glass. The single most famous teaching image in the history of golf.
›

If you know one thing about Five Lessons, you know the pane of glass. Hogan asked you to imagine a sheet of glass resting on the golfer's shoulders and sloping down to the ball, with the player's head poking through a hole in it. The backswing, he said, should travel just below the underside of that glass — on the plane — without ever bumping or breaking it.

The key idea. The swing plane is the angle the club travels on, set at address by the shaft's lean and the player's posture. A swing that stays “on plane” delivers the club to the ball consistently and repeatably. A swing that breaks the plane — goes too steep or too flat — has to make a compensating move on the way down to rescue it.

Why the image is so powerful

Before Hogan, “plane” was a vague idea. Hogan gave it a shape you could hold in your head. The glass image does three things at once:

  • It defines a reference — there's now a surface the swing relates to.
  • It defines a fault — “breaking the glass” (too steep) and “dropping under it” (too flat) become things you can picture and feel.
  • It connects setup to motion — the glass is set by your posture and the shaft angle at address, which is why a bad setup makes an on-plane swing nearly impossible.

The honest modern footnote

Modern analysis has refined the picture. We now understand the golf swing actually operates on more than one plane — the backswing plane and downswing plane are not identical for most good players, and the club “shifts planes” in transition in a way Hogan's single-glass image simplifies. Some elite players are visibly more “up and down” than the pure single-plane picture.

But the teaching value of the image survives all of that. For a recreational player, the single most common, most damaging plane fault is being far too steep coming down — over the top, swinging across the ball. Hogan's pane of glass is still the cleanest mental tool ever invented for feeling that fault and getting the club to deliver from a shallower, more inside path. Use it as a feel, not as physics.

“The plane isn't a rule. It's a picture — and the picture fixes more swings than the rule ever could.”
Drill — Trace the Glass

In slow motion, with no ball: take the club back and feel the shaft stay just under the imaginary glass — not lifting up through it, not dropping under it. Pause at the top. Then feel the downswing return under the glass, shallower rather than steeper. Ten slow reps. You're not building the full swing here — you're building the feel of the reference surface so your fast swing has something to relate to.

Chapter Five

The First Part of the Swing

The waggle, the one-piece takeaway, and the backswing that stays on plane.
›

Hogan split the swing into two parts — backswing and downswing — and treated them as separate lessons because they have separate jobs. The first part has one purpose: to get the club to the top of the backswing in a position from which a correct downswing is possible. Nothing more glamorous than that. The backswing doesn't hit the ball. It just sets the table.

The key idea. The backswing is a coordinated turn, not a lift. The body, arms, and club move away together — “one piece” — staying under the plane, winding the big muscles, and arriving at a top position that's balanced and on-plane. A good backswing makes a good downswing easy. A bad one makes it impossible.

The waggle

Hogan devoted real attention to the waggle — the small back-and-forth motion of the clubhead before the swing starts. To most amateurs the waggle looks like a nervous tic. To Hogan it was a rehearsal and a tension-release: a miniature preview of the takeaway path, a way to keep the hands and forearms free rather than frozen, and a trigger that flowed the player into the swing instead of starting it from a dead stop.

The practical takeaway: don't stand frozen over the ball. A small, repeatable waggle keeps you athletic and gives the swing a running start.

The one-piece takeaway

Hogan wanted the club, hands, arms, and shoulders to start back together. The big muscles — the shoulders and the torso — control the early move. The hands don't snatch the club inside; the arms don't lift it straight up. Everything moves as a connected unit for the first stretch of the backswing, which keeps the club on plane from the very start.

The turn, and the top

From there, the backswing is a turn — the shoulders rotate, the torso winds against the resistance of the lower body, the lead arm stays comfortably extended, the wrists hinge naturally because the grip allows them to. At the top, the club is on plane, the player is balanced, and the big muscles are coiled. Hogan was not chasing a huge, loose, over-rotated backswing. He wanted a controlled wind-up that could be delivered.

Backswing faults this chapter targets
  • Snatching the club inside with the hands on the takeaway — off plane immediately.
  • Lifting the club with the arms instead of turning the body — too steep, no coil.
  • Over-swinging — a backswing so long and loose the player can't control the delivery.
  • Standing frozen with no waggle, then starting the swing from total tension.

Chapter Six

The Second Part of the Swing

The downswing sequence. Hips first — the move that separates good ball-strikers from everyone else.
›

This is the chapter that mattered most to Hogan, and the one most amateurs get most wrong. If the backswing sets the table, the downswing is the meal — and Hogan was insistent about the order in which it unfolds.

The key idea. The downswing starts from the ground up. The hips lead — they begin to unwind toward the target first — followed by the torso, then the shoulders, then the arms, then the hands and clubhead last. This sequence is what stores and then releases speed at the bottom. Reverse the order — start with the hands and shoulders — and you've thrown the club away from the top.

Why “hips first” is the whole ballgame

When the hips start the downswing, two good things happen at once. First, the club is dropped into a shallower, more-from-the-inside delivery path — back under Hogan's pane of glass. Second, the body creates a stretch between the fast-moving lower half and the still-coiled upper half, and that stretch is the engine. The arms and club get pulled through by the body's rotation rather than pushed by the hands.

The amateur disaster is the opposite: from the top, the shoulders and hands fire first. The club is thrown outward and steep — over the top — and the path cuts across the ball. That single sequencing error produces the most common ball flight in recreational golf: the pull-slice. (Cross- reference the nine ball flights in the Jacobs primer — the pull-slice is an over-the-top swing wearing a disguise.)

The feeling Hogan was after

Hogan described the downswing as turning the hips back to the left (for a right-hander) as the very first move down — a feeling of clearing the lead hip out of the way so the arms and club have room to swing past. The hands stay passive longer than feels natural. The clubhead is the last thing to arrive, which is exactly why it's moving fastest when it does.

“From the top, the first move is the hips. If your hands go first, the swing is already over.”
Drill — Pump the Transition

Swing to the top and stop. From there, make three slow “pumps” of the transition: feel the hips start, feel the arms drop while the hands stay quiet, feel the club shallow under the plane. Stop. Reset. After the third pump, let the swing go through to a balanced finish. You're training the order of the downswing at a speed slow enough to actually feel it.

How to know you've got it
Two checks. First: a balanced finish — full weight on the lead foot, chest facing the target, held for three seconds. If you can't hold the finish, the sequence was off. Second: ball flight — if your pull-slice starts becoming a straighter, slightly-drawing flight, the club is finally coming from the inside because the hips finally went first.

Chapter Seven

Supination — the misunderstood chapter

What Hogan actually meant about the hands through impact, and what modern teaching says now.
›

Five Lessons contains a famous passage about the lead wrist “supinating” through impact — and it is probably the single most argued-over paragraph in golf instruction. It's worth understanding both what Hogan meant and why modern teaching treats it carefully.

The key idea. Hogan used “supination” to describe the lead forearm and wrist rotating so that, through impact, the back of the lead hand faces the target and the wrist is firm — not flipping, not breaking down, not scooping. The intent behind the word is sound. The word itself has caused decades of confusion.

What Hogan was trying to prevent

Strip away the anatomical term and Hogan's concern is one every teacher still shares: the lead wrist breaking down through impact. The classic amateur fault is the lead wrist cupping or flipping at the ball — the hands trying to “help” the ball into the air. It adds loft, throws away speed, and makes contact a lottery. Hogan wanted the lead wrist firm and slightly bowed through impact, the hands leading the clubhead, loft delivered as designed.

Modern data — from launch monitors and 3D motion capture — confirms the principle completely. The best ball- strikers have a flat-to-bowed lead wrist at impact and the hands ahead of the ball. That part of Hogan was exactly right.

Where the confusion came from

The trouble is that “supination” describes a rotation, and a lot of readers took it as an instruction to actively roll or twist the lead forearm hard through impact. Done as a conscious, aggressive manipulation, that's a great way to produce a snap hook or wildly inconsistent contact.

The reconciliation: the firm, slightly-bowed lead wrist at impact is the result of a correct grip and a correct downswing sequence — not a thing you manufacture with an independent hand action. If your grip is sound (Chapter 2) and your hips lead the downswing (Chapter 6), the lead wrist tends to arrive in the right place on its own. Hogan, who had the most grooved hands in the history of the game, may have underestimated how literally a recreational player would try to copy a feel that, for him, was automatic.

Reading this chapter safely
  • Don't try to actively twist or roll your lead forearm at the ball. That's not the lesson.
  • Do understand the goal: a firm, flat-to-bowed lead wrist at impact, hands ahead of the clubhead.
  • Get there through the grip and the sequence, not through an independent hand manipulation.
  • If you want this checked properly, it's a great thing to put in front of a coach — it's hard to self-diagnose.
“Hogan wasn't wrong about the destination. He just had a car most of us don't.”

Chapter Eight

Where Hogan fits — and how to use both books

Hogan and Jacobs together: build with one, diagnose with the other.
›

Five Lessons and Practical Golf are the two books every teacher names — and they're not competitors. They're two halves of the same job.

The key idea. Jacobs gave you a way to diagnose — read the ball, find the cause at impact, trace it back to the swing. Hogan gave you a way to build — a specific, sequenced set of fundamentals to construct a repeating swing. Diagnosis without a build leaves you knowing what's wrong but not what to do. A build without diagnosis leaves you grinding on positions that may not be your actual problem. You need both.

What Hogan got exactly right

  • The grip is the foundation. Start there. Spend real time there.
  • Address position is part of the swing, not a preamble.
  • The swing has a plane, and a clear mental picture of it fixes more amateur swings than any rule.
  • The downswing sequences from the ground up — hips lead. This is still the single biggest separator between good and poor ball-strikers.
  • The fundamentals are teachable. You don't need rare talent. You need correct positions, in order, practiced with understanding.

What modern teaching has refined

  • The single plane — the swing actually uses more than one plane; the glass image is best used as a feel, not as literal physics.
  • Supination — the destination (firm, bowed lead wrist) is right; the idea of actively rolling the forearm to get there is a trap for recreational players.
  • Grip “strength” — Hogan's slightly weaker grip suited a man fighting a hook. Yours should be dialed to your ball flight (back to the Jacobs diagnostic).

How to use the shelf

Here's the order I'd actually recommend:

  1. Read the Jacobs primer first. Learn to diagnose. Find your pattern. Know what your ball is telling you.
  2. Read this Hogan primer second. Now you have the build — the fundamentals to construct the swing that produces better impact.
  3. Then the others — McLean, Kelley, Plummer & Bennett — are different, more specific organizations of the same material. Useful once the foundation from Jacobs and Hogan is in place.

Diagnose with Jacobs. Build with Hogan. That's the whole shelf in one sentence.

“Hogan said the average golfer could break 80. He meant it. The fundamentals are the path — and they're learnable.”

Putting Hogan to work this week

Three concrete actions, in order:

  1. Build the grip in the mirror. Ten minutes a night, no club swing — just placing the hands correctly until the new grip stops feeling foreign.
  2. Photograph your address. Down-the-line and face-on. Check posture against Hogan's own address photos.
  3. Pump the transition. Swing to the top, then three slow pumps feeling the hips lead and the club shallow, before letting one go to a held, balanced finish.

If you want to do this with eyes-on coaching — especially the grip, which is the hardest one to install solo — our Ladies' Friday Morning Series starts with exactly this fundamental. Hogan built his whole book on the grip; so does our curriculum.

About the original

Five Lessons: The Modern Fundamentals of Golf by Ben Hogan, with Herbert Warren Wind, illustrated by Anthony Ravielli. First serialized in Sports Illustrated and published as a book in 1957. Alongside John Jacobs' Practical Golf, it is considered one of the two most important instruction books ever written. Hogan (1912–1997) won nine major championships and is regarded as one of the finest ball-strikers in the history of the game.

Buy the original. It's a short, beautifully illustrated book, and Ravielli's drawings communicate things no summary can. It belongs on the shelf next to Jacobs.

Glossary

The plane / pane of glass
Hogan's image of an imaginary sheet of glass sloping from the shoulders down to the ball, defining the angle the swing should travel on.
One-piece takeaway
Starting the backswing with the club, hands, arms, and shoulders moving away together as a connected unit, controlled by the big muscles.
The waggle
A small rehearsal motion of the clubhead before the swing starts. Releases tension and previews the takeaway path.
Downswing sequence
The ground-up order of the downswing: hips, then torso, then shoulders, then arms, then hands and clubhead last.
Supination
Hogan's term for the lead forearm/wrist rotation through impact. The intended result — a firm, flat-to-bowed lead wrist with the hands ahead of the clubhead — is correct; the idea of actively rolling the forearm to get there is a common misreading.
Pronation
The opposite forearm rotation; in Hogan's framework, the rotation occurring on the backswing as the club is taken back.
Address position
The setup: grip, stance width, posture, ball position, and alignment. Hogan treated it as part of the swing, not a preamble.
