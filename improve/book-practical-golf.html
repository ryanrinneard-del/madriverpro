Practical Golf: The Book That Built Modern Teaching.

Ball flight tells the truth. Everything else is theory.
Inspired by John Jacobs' Practical Golf
Independent Interpretation

This primer is Ryan Rinneard's independent educational summary of the principles John Jacobs laid down in his 1972 book Practical Golf. It is not an official Jacobs publication, nor authorized by his estate. Concepts below are described in Ryan's own words; the original work is attributed to Jacobs throughout.

If you only ever buy one golf book, buy this one. The full text covers nuance and detail this primer can't reproduce, and Jacobs' own writing has a clarity that nothing has matched in the fifty years since. Find it on Amazon →

From the coach

If you ask any serious golf teacher in the world to name the most important book ever written on instruction, you'll get one of two answers: Five Lessons by Ben Hogan, or Practical Golf by John Jacobs. Hogan's book is about how he played. Jacobs' book is about how you can be helped — and that distinction is why Jacobs sits, for me, at the very top of the shelf.

Jacobs was a Ryder Cup player and captain, but he became famous as a diagnostician. He could stand on the back of the range, watch a golfer hit three balls, and tell them exactly what their clubface and path were doing at impact without ever seeing the swing in slow motion. He did this because he understood, before anyone else did, that the ball is the truth. Whatever the ball does is the consequence of what the club did. And whatever the club did is a consequence of how the swing was set up to deliver it.

Everything modern teaching is built on — ball flight laws, D-Plane, TrackMan numbers, the “new” understanding that face dominates direction — Jacobs was teaching, with no technology and no high-speed cameras, in 1972.

This primer is for the recreational player who's heard Jacobs' name in passing, or seen the book referenced in a lesson, and wants the core ideas before sitting down with the original. Eight chapters: his big idea, the diagnostic system he invented, the impact conditions he insisted matter, the setup he refused to compromise on, the grip he said was non-negotiable, and how to diagnose your own game from the only piece of evidence that doesn't lie — what the ball just did.

Read it straight through. Then go to the range with one shot per ball and watch where it goes. Jacobs would tell you that's where the lesson actually starts.

— Ryan

Chapter One

Why Practical Golf still matters

The book that gave teachers a language. And why every modern system traces back here.
›

Before Jacobs, golf instruction was mostly anecdote and method. A great player would write a book about how they swung, and recreational golfers would try, with varying success, to copy it. There was no shared vocabulary. There was no diagnostic framework. If your shot was slicing, the cure was whatever the last teacher you saw happened to believe.

The key idea. Jacobs separated method from function. He didn't care how you got there — he cared what the club was doing at the moment it met the ball. That single move — teaching the function instead of the form — is what turned golf instruction into something you could repeat, scale, and trust.

What was new in 1972

When Practical Golf came out, Jacobs put three things on paper that no one had organized into a single system before:

  • Ball flight laws — the relationship between clubface, club path, and what the ball does. He framed it as cause and effect, in a way any player could understand.
  • The impact essentials — the small set of geometric facts that must be true when the club meets the ball. If you've ever heard a teacher say “impact is everything,” this is where that line came from.
  • The setup hierarchy — the idea that faults in setup multiply through the swing, and that fixing setup first solves most problems before they need a swing change.

All three ideas are now considered obvious. They weren't in 1972. And the reason they feel obvious today is that every subsequent teaching system — Leadbetter, Harmon, Pelz, McLean, Plummer & Bennett, Vision54, TrackMan University, modern TPI — is built on the foundation Jacobs laid.

Why “practical” was the right word

Jacobs called the book Practical Golf because that's what it is. It's not a theory book. It's not a swing-method book. It's a working teacher's manual. Every page asks: what does this look like on the course? What does the recreational player actually need to know? What's the smallest change that fixes the largest number of shots?

“There is no swing that is right for everybody, but there is impact that is right for everybody.”

That's the line. It's the entire book in fifteen words. Whatever your swing looks like, whatever your handicap, whatever you do with the rest of your life — if you can deliver the club to the ball with the face square to your target, on a path that travels toward the target, with the low point of your swing just ahead of the ball, with center-face contact at appropriate speed, you will hit good golf shots. The job of the swing is to produce that impact. The job of teaching is to find the shortest path from where you are to that impact.

Chapter Two

The ball is the truth

Why Jacobs taught what came out, not what went in. The diagnostic mindset.
›

Jacobs would stand behind a player, watch a few balls fly, and start the lesson by describing exactly what their club had done at the moment of impact. The face was a fraction open. The path was traveling slightly to the left. The angle of attack was steeper than ideal. The strike was towards the heel. He could tell you these things without seeing the swing, because the ball flight he'd watched had already told him.

The key idea. The golf ball doesn't care about your backswing, your weight shift, your “feel,” or your mental model. It only responds to what the clubhead was doing for the half-thousandth of a second it was in contact with it. Read the ball, and you read the truth.

Why this was revolutionary

Pre-Jacobs, most teaching looked at the swing and tried to decide if it was “correct.” If your backswing was flat, you were told to make it more upright. If your hips slid, you were told to turn. The teacher had a model of the perfect swing, and your job was to copy it.

Jacobs flipped the order. Instead of starting with the swing, he started with the result. The ball did something. What did the club have to be doing at impact to make the ball do that? And what does the swing need to look like to produce that impact condition?

This is reverse-engineering instead of pattern matching. It's the difference between a doctor who treats your symptoms and a doctor who diagnoses the cause. Jacobs was the first golf teacher to systematically diagnose causes, and once you've learned to think this way, you can never go back.

What this means for your range time

If you take only one idea from this primer to your next practice session, take this one: watch where every ball goes. Not in a general “that was fine” way. Specifically. Did it start left, center, or right of your target line? Did it curve left, right, or stay straight? Each of those answers tells you something concrete about what the club did, and what the club did is something you can change.

Drill — One Ball, One Note

Hit ten balls. For each one, write a quick note: where it started (L/C/R) and how it curved (left/straight/right). At the end you'll have a clear pattern — eight pull-cuts, seven push-draws, whatever it is — and that pattern is the conversation you need to have with your swing. Most amateurs think their misses are random. They're almost never random. They're a pattern you haven't been looking at.

The trap to avoid
  • Caring about what the swing felt like instead of what the ball did. Feel is unreliable. Ball flight is data.
  • Hitting twenty balls in a row to “groove a feeling” without watching where any of them went. You can groove the wrong impact for an hour and your handicap won't move.
  • Assuming that because one good shot felt like X, all your good shots will feel like X. Different days, different bodies, different feels. Same impact conditions.

Chapter Three

The nine ball flights

Three start directions, three curves. Every shot you'll ever hit lives in this grid.
›

Jacobs catalogued every shot you can hit into nine patterns. He didn't invent the patterns — players had been hitting them forever — but he was the first to organize them into a teaching grid and explain what caused each one.

The key idea. A golf ball does two things in the air: it starts somewhere, and it curves somewhere. The combinations of starting direction (left, center, right) and curve (left, straight, right) give you exactly nine possible flights. Every golf shot ever hit lives in this 3 × 3 grid.

The three starting directions

Where the ball starts is, for an iron shot, determined almost entirely by where the clubface is pointing at impact — relative to the target. Square-to-target, the ball starts at the target. Open, it starts right. Closed, it starts left.

Modern launch monitor data has refined this slightly: the start direction is roughly 85% face, 15% path for irons, and slightly less face-dominant for driver. But Jacobs' principle was right in 1972 and is still right today. The face starts the ball.

The three curves

Where the ball curves is determined by the relationship between face and path. If the face is pointing where the path is traveling, the ball goes straight. If the face is closed relative to the path, it curves left. If it's open relative to the path, it curves right.

This is the key insight every recreational golfer misses: curve is not about face direction in space. It's about face direction compared to path direction. You can have an open face that draws (if the path is even more out-to-in), or a closed face that fades (if the path is even more in-to-out). Slices and hooks live in the gap between face and path, not in either one alone.

The nine combinations

Walk through them yourself:

  • Push-slice: starts right, curves further right. Face open to target, path even more right (in-to-out), face open to path.
  • Push: starts right, stays straight. Face and path both pointing right of target, face square to path.
  • Push-hook: starts right, curves back to or past target. Face open to target but closed to path.
  • Slice: starts square, curves right. Face square to target, path left of target, face open to path.
  • Straight: starts square, stays square. Face, path, and target all aligned.
  • Hook: starts square, curves left. Face square, path right of target, face closed to path.
  • Pull-slice: starts left, curves back to or past target. Face closed to target but open to path (the classic mid-handicap pattern).
  • Pull: starts left, stays left. Face and path both left, face square to path.
  • Pull-hook: starts left, curves further left. Both face and path left, face closed to path.
Verify
Stand behind the line on your next range session. Hit three balls and put each one into one of the nine boxes above. By the third ball you'll have your pattern. That pattern is what your lesson should be about. Trying to fix a slice with a hook drill, or a pull-cut with a closed-face drill, is the difference between making progress and going in circles.

Chapter Four

The six things that must be true at impact

Face, path, angle, low point, sweet spot, speed. Everything else is decoration.
›

Jacobs argued that the entire golf swing exists to produce one half-thousandth of a second — the moment the clubhead touches the ball. Everything before that moment is just the delivery mechanism. The swing is the means; impact is the end.

The key idea. There are six geometric facts that determine what a golf shot does. They are not opinions. They are not stylistic. They are physics. Get them right, and the ball goes where you want. Get any one of them wrong, and the ball doesn't.

The six essentials

Jacobs listed them in different orders in different writings, but the canonical six are:

  1. Clubface direction at impact, relative to the target.
  2. Club path at impact, relative to the target (and the face).
  3. Angle of attack — whether the club is descending, level, or ascending at impact.
  4. Low point of the swing arc — where the club bottoms out relative to the ball.
  5. Sweet spot contact — whether the ball is struck on the center of the face, the heel, the toe, or off the grooves.
  6. Clubhead speed at impact.

What each one does

Face direction mostly determines starting line. It's responsible for about 85% of where the ball starts for irons. If your shots are starting in the wrong direction, your face is pointing in the wrong direction at impact.

Path mostly determines curve (in combination with face). It also affects starting line, but secondarily. The relationship between face and path is what produces draw, fade, or straight flight.

Angle of attack affects launch and spin. With irons, a slightly descending blow compresses the ball and produces predictable distance and trajectory. With driver, a slightly ascending blow maximizes distance. Get the angle wrong and you'll either thin it, fat it, or hit it on the wrong launch window.

Low point is where the club reaches the bottom of its swing arc. For irons hit off the ground, the low point must be after the ball (closer to the target side). If your low point is behind the ball, you'll hit it fat. If it's well past, you'll thin or top it. This is the single most underrated impact factor in amateur golf.

Sweet spot contact is roughly a 5-yard difference per inch of off-center contact on a 7-iron. Heel strikes lose distance and add slice spin. Toe strikes lose distance and add hook spin. Center-face contact is the most boring, most repeatable thing in golf and it's worth more strokes than almost any swing change.

Speed sets the ceiling on distance. You can improve everything else and still be capped if you swing too slowly. Conversely, more speed without the other five is just a more violent miss.

“The good player produces the impact conditions. The recreational player hopes for them.”

How to think about this on the course

You don't think about all six at once. Jacobs argued that for most amateurs, two of them matter more than the others most of the time: face direction and low point. Face controls where the ball goes. Low point controls whether you actually make decent contact. If you can get those two right for any given shot, the others tend to fall into a workable range.

Which is why, when Jacobs gave a lesson, he checked the grip first (which controls face) and the setup ball position (which influences low point). Two checks, ninety seconds, and most of the diagnostic was done before the player had hit a ball.

Chapter Five

Setup is half the swing

Grip, stance, posture, alignment, ball position. Get these and the swing has a chance.
›

Jacobs argued that more amateur golf problems are caused by setup than by swing motion. The reasoning was simple: if the way you stand to the ball pre-determines a certain kind of swing, no amount of in-motion correction can override that geometry. The swing has to compensate for what the setup made inevitable.

The key idea. Five setup elements — grip, stance, posture, alignment, ball position — determine a huge percentage of what your swing can possibly do. Get them right and the swing has a fighting chance. Get them wrong and the swing has to perform miracles to produce decent impact.

The five elements, in priority order

Grip is first, and it's first by a long way. The grip controls the clubface, and the clubface controls starting direction and most of the curve. We're giving the grip its own chapter next; this one covers the other four.

Stance is the width and angle of your feet. A stance roughly shoulder-width is the default for irons; slightly wider for the driver. Toes turned out a touch helps the hips turn through. Stance too narrow and you have no base; too wide and you can't rotate. Get the width right once and you almost never have to think about it again.

Posture is the way the spine angles toward the ball. Jacobs taught a tall posture from the hips, bowed forward, arms hanging naturally from the shoulders. Not slumped, not straight up, not bent over from the lower back. The arms should fall directly below the shoulder line. If you have to reach for the ball or you feel crowded by it, your posture is wrong before the swing has started.

Alignment is where your feet, hips, and shoulders point. The classic Jacobs alignment was parallel left of the target (for right-handed players) — meaning your feet line is parallel to the target line, not pointing at the target. Most amateurs are aimed right of where they think, which forces them to swing across the line to bring the ball back. The pull-slice pattern that dominates club golf is almost entirely an alignment problem.

Ball position is where the ball sits relative to your feet. The general Jacobs principle: longer clubs slightly more forward, shorter clubs slightly more centered. With a 7-iron the ball sits roughly in the middle. With a driver, just inside the lead heel. Ball position controls where in your arc the club meets the ball, which controls low point, which controls the quality of strike.

How to check each one

Drill — The Setup Audit

Two clubs on the ground. One pointing at the target. The other parallel to the first, on the ground in front of your toes. That's your alignment check.

Have a friend take a photo from down-the-line (behind you, along your target line) and from face-on (perpendicular to the target line, at hip height). Compare to a tour player you like. You're looking for: shoulders, hips, and feet all parallel to the target line; spine tilted forward from the hips; arms hanging straight down; ball position correct for the club.

Two photos, ninety seconds, and you'll catch problems your swing has been compensating for since you started playing.

The most common setup mistakes
  • Aimed right. Players line up at the target with their shoulders, which puts their feet aimed well right. Hugely common, hugely consequential.
  • Ball too far back. Players who once played with longer-shafted clubs often kept the ball position from years ago. Today's clubs need the ball slightly forward of old positions.
  • Slumped posture. Bending from the lower back instead of the hips. Restricts turn and shifts the swing toward the heel.
  • Arms tucked in. Arms locked against the chest, no clearance for the swing. Forces a flat, inside path.

Chapter Six

The grip — Jacobs' non-negotiable

Why every diagnostic conversation ended the same way: “Let me see your hands on the club.”
›

If you walked onto Jacobs' range with a problem, he checked your grip first. Always. It didn't matter what the problem was. Slice? Grip. Hook? Grip. Distance? Grip. Inconsistent strike? Grip. The reason was simple: the grip controls the clubface, and the clubface controls 85% of what the ball does. Almost every persistent fault in club golf comes back to hands that aren't on the club the right way.

The key idea. The grip is the one and only physical connection between you and the club. Every other body part influences the swing through the grip. Get the grip right, and the clubface returns to square at impact by default, without conscious effort. Get the grip wrong, and your swing spends its entire motion trying to compensate for a face that won't behave.

What a sound grip actually looks like

For a right-handed player, the lead hand (left hand) goes on first. The club sits diagonally across the base of the fingers — not in the palm. When you close the hand, the thumb rests on the top of the grip, slightly right of center. You should be able to see two or three knuckles when you look down (Jacobs called this “the V” formed by thumb and forefinger pointing toward your right ear).

The trail hand (right hand) goes on second. The lifeline of the right palm covers the left thumb. The right hand fingers wrap around. The two Vs — left thumb-forefinger and right thumb-forefinger — should be parallel and both pointing to the same place (roughly your right shoulder/ear).

That's it. Two hands, fingers not palm, both Vs pointing the same direction.

Why most amateurs get it wrong

The most common amateur fault is the lead hand sitting in the palm instead of the fingers. This single error does three bad things at once:

  • Reduces wrist hinge, which costs distance.
  • Forces the face open at the top of the backswing.
  • Makes it nearly impossible to release the club through impact.

The second most common fault is a grip that's too “weak” — the hands rotated too far counter-clockwise (for a right-hander), with both Vs pointing toward the chin instead of the trail shoulder. This is the slice grip. Almost every chronic slicer in club golf has this grip.

“Show me your hands on the club and I'll tell you what your bad shot is.”

The fix is one Friday morning

A bad grip feels weird when you correct it. That's the whole problem. Players try a proper grip, hit two bad shots, decide it doesn't work, and go back to what they had. The fix isn't complicated; it's just that nobody walks them through it slowly enough for the new grip to stop feeling foreign.

That's why our Friday Morning Ladies' Series starts with the grip. Spend one morning getting your hands on the club correctly, with eyes-on coaching, in a low-pressure setting, and you'll save years of trying to fix the shot that the bad grip was producing.

Drill — The Grip Check, Every Time

Before every shot in practice, look at your hands. See the two Vs. Confirm they're parallel and pointing to your trail shoulder. Five seconds, every ball. After two weeks you'll never have to think about it again. The grip is muscle memory that responds to attention, and attention is just looking.

Chapter Seven

Diagnose yourself

How to read your own ball flight and know what your club did. The Jacobs method, step by step.
›

Jacobs' real gift wasn't that he could diagnose you. It's that his framework was simple enough you could diagnose yourself. Once you understand the nine ball flights and the six impact essentials, you can take a bucket of balls to the range, observe your own pattern, and work backwards to the cause.

The key idea. Diagnosis isn't a guess. It's a series of questions you can answer from the ball alone. Where did it start? What did it curve? Where did it finish? Those three answers give you the face and path at impact, and the face and path tell you what your grip and swing are doing wrong.

The self-diagnostic, step by step

  1. Hit ten balls with a 7-iron, same target. Don't grind. Don't change anything between shots. Just hit ten shots and watch where they go.
  2. For each ball, classify the start. Did it start left of the target, at the target, or right of the target?
  3. For each ball, classify the curve. Did it curve left, stay straight, or curve right while in flight?
  4. Find the pattern. If 8 of 10 are pull-cuts (start left, curve right), that's your pattern. If 7 of 10 are pushes (start right, stay straight), that's your pattern.
  5. Translate the pattern to face/path. Use chapter three's grid. Pull-cut means face slightly closed to target at impact, path well left, face open to path. Push-draw means face slightly open to target, path further right, face closed to path.
  6. Translate face/path to setup/grip. Face persistently closed to target usually means a grip that's too strong (Vs pointing past trail shoulder), or alignment aimed left. Path persistently out-to-in usually means shoulders aimed left at address, or an over-the-top swing motion.

The two most common patterns in club golf

The pull-cut — starts left, curves right, finishes near target. The single most common pattern in recreational golf. The cause is almost always: shoulders aimed left at address, over-the-top swing path, face that gets back to square late. You're hitting a slice that gets a head start. Fix the alignment first; fix the grip second; the path tends to follow.

The push-draw — starts right, curves left, finishes near or past target. The pattern most low handicaps eventually settle into. Cause is generally: a slightly in-to-out path (good), with a face that's open to target but closed to path. This is a workable miss; you can control it. Don't “fix” this if it's consistent.

Diagnose, don't catastrophize
  • Don't decide your swing is broken because you hit two bad shots. Hit ten. Look for the pattern.
  • Don't try to fix three things at once. Pick the one most likely to fix the pattern — usually grip or alignment — and work on it alone.
  • Don't change anything without verifying with ten more balls. If the new ball flight isn't an improvement, you changed the wrong thing.
When to ask for help
If you've run two diagnostic sessions and you can't identify a clear pattern, or you can identify the pattern but can't move it, that's the moment to book a lesson. A good teacher will see in ten minutes what you've been puzzling over for ten range sessions. That's not a failure of self-diagnosis; it's the right time to use a coach.

Chapter Eight

Where this fits in modern golf

Jacobs, TrackMan, D-Plane, and why the technology proved him right.
›

For three decades after Practical Golf, Jacobs' ball flight laws were the consensus understanding of how golf shots worked. Then in the mid-2000s, launch monitor data started flooding in — TrackMan, FlightScope, GCQuad — and something interesting happened. The new technology refined some of Jacobs' specifics. But the framework was right.

The key idea. Jacobs taught with his eyes what TrackMan measures with radar. The technology has refined the percentages — we now know the face is about 85% responsible for starting direction with irons, not 100% — but every modern teaching system is still operating inside the framework Jacobs laid down: ball flight is the evidence, impact is the cause, and the swing is the delivery mechanism.

What modern technology added to Jacobs

The 85/15 face-path split. Pre-launch-monitor, most teachers thought path dominated start direction. The radar data revealed it's the opposite: face dominates, by a lot, especially with irons. This is a refinement of Jacobs, not a contradiction. He always said the face controlled start direction; the technology just put a precise number on it.

D-Plane. A more accurate model of how the three-dimensional clubhead behaves through impact. Updates Jacobs' 2D model of face and path, but doesn't replace it. Useful for teachers; rarely useful for recreational players.

Spin axis. Modern monitors measure the actual axis of ball rotation, which is a cleaner way to think about curve than “face open to path.” Same physics, just measurable now.

What Jacobs got exactly right

Everything that matters:

  • The ball is the evidence. Start there, work backwards.
  • Face dominates start direction. Path dominates curve (in combination with face).
  • Impact is the goal. The swing is the means.
  • Setup determines most of what the swing can do.
  • The grip is the single highest-leverage setup element.
  • Teaching is diagnosis first, prescription second.

Where Jacobs fits with the other primers on this shelf

Jacobs is the foundation. Read this primer first. The other primers on this shelf are different angles on the same problem:

  • The 8-Step Swing (McLean) — gives you a position-by-position map of the swing motion that delivers the impact Jacobs is asking for. Mechanical.
  • The Golfing Machine (Kelley) — an engineer's catalogue of every component that can be in a swing. Useful for understanding the variations between tour players. Dense.
  • Stack & Tilt (Plummer & Bennett) — one specific way of organizing the impact essentials into a teachable system. Excellent for ball-first contact.

All three of those are houses built on the foundation Jacobs laid. If you're going to read one book on the shelf, read Practical Golf. If you're going to read four, read this one first.

About the original

Practical Golf by John Jacobs (with Ken Bowden), first published 1972. Considered by most working golf teachers to be the most important instructional book ever written. Jacobs (1925–2017) was a Ryder Cup player, captain, and the founder of the modern golf-teaching profession. He taught using ball flight and cause-and-effect diagnosis decades before launch monitors proved him right.

If you only buy one golf book in your life, buy this one. Used copies are widely available. The full text covers technique, strategy, the short game, and putting in a way no primer can reproduce.

Glossary

Ball flight law
The relationship between clubface, club path, angle of attack, and the resulting flight of the ball. The foundation of modern golf instruction.
Clubface
The direction the front of the clubhead points at impact. Dominates starting direction (~85% for irons).
Club path
The direction the clubhead is traveling through impact. In combination with face, determines curve.
Angle of attack
The vertical angle of the club's approach to the ball at impact. Descending (irons), ascending (driver), level (long clubs off the tee).
Low point
The lowest point of the club's swing arc. For irons, must be in front of the ball (target-side) for clean contact.
Sweet spot
The center of percussion on the clubface; the point of maximum energy transfer and minimum twist on off-center contact.
D-Plane
A modern 3D model of clubhead behavior through impact, refining Jacobs' 2D face/path framework. Mostly a tool for teachers, not for players.
The Two Vs
The Vs formed by the thumb and forefinger of each hand on the grip. Jacobs' shortcut for checking grip neutrality: both Vs should be parallel and point to the same place (roughly the trail shoulder).
