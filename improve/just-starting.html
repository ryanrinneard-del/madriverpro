Just Starting.

The part of the portal where we strip out the jargon. No handicap required. No fairway percentages. Just the basics, in order, with room to grow.

From the coach

Most of the people who start golf never stick with it. Not because they lack the talent — because the game is set up to make them feel stupid in their first year. You show up to the range and everyone around you seems to know what they're doing. You show up to the course and you're not sure which tees to play, how fast to walk, or whether it's your shot. You hit twelve shots on one hole and wonder if you'll ever hit a good one.

Here's the truth nobody tells you: every good golfer was terrible once. Ben Hogan shot in the 100s. Tiger Woods had to be taught to swing. The difference between the people who stick with golf and the people who don't isn't talent — it's whether anyone gave them the patience and the road map to keep going.

This section is that road map. Three phases. Week one, first month, first season. No stat dashboards. No benchmarks telling you what you're doing wrong. Just the stuff that matters, in order, with a coach's voice telling you you're doing fine.

— Ryan
Week One · The absolute basics

Learn to hold the club, stand to the ball, and survive a round.

Before you worry about how far you hit it or whether your swing plane is correct, learn these four things. They're not the sexy parts of golf — they're the ones that make you welcome on any course.

1
The grip
How you hold the club. The single biggest source of bad shots for beginners — and the easiest fix.
2
Stance, posture, and alignment
How to stand to the ball. If your feet and shoulders aren't pointing where you think they are, you'll fight your swing forever.
3
Pace of play and etiquette
The social rules of golf. Play ready, keep up, know when to pick up — and never worry about bothering the group behind you again.
4
How this portal works
Your home for everything we work on together. Log rounds, set goals, track what matters — and skip the rest until you're ready.
First Month · Comfortable on the course

The unspoken rules, the rules that are actually spoken, and the basics of how a ball moves.

Now we layer in the stuff that makes golf make sense. How a round actually flows. Why balls curve. What "first-tee ready" means. By the end of this phase, you walk to the first tee without nerves, and you know what to do with your body for the next four hours.

1
First-Tee Ready
The quiet code of golf — every rhythm, ritual, and unspoken rule that separates a confident first round from an anxious one.
2
Why the ball does what it does
The laws of ball flight, in plain English. Why you slice. How to hit it higher. The one thing that separates a solid strike from a thin one.
3
The short game is where scores live
You'll shave more strokes from 50 yards and in than you will from the tee. Chip, pitch, and putt with intent.
4
Log your first few rounds
Use the Simple Log — score, how it felt, one thing to remember. That's it. Every round moves a milestone.

A reminder.

You're going to hit bad shots this season. You're going to hit them in front of people. You're going to hit them on holes where you'd have sworn you finally had it figured out. That's the game.

The players who end up good — and the players who end up happy — are the ones who didn't let the bad shots define them. Take the next one, commit to it, and keep going. Every hour on the course is a bet on the next hour being better than this one. Keep making the bet.

First Season · Building actual skills

Now we start sharpening. Pick one thing at a time, work on it, and move on.

After you've played a dozen rounds and you feel like you belong on the course, we start layering in real skill work. Not a lot — one thing at a time. The goal for your first full season is simple: break a score you didn't think you could. 120, 115, 110, 100 — whatever your ceiling is, break it.

1
How to practice so it actually sticks
Most amateur practice doesn't produce learning. Here's the version that does. Short sessions. Use video. Keep it simple.
2
The mental game for new players
A pre-shot routine. What to do when you hit a bad one. How to stay in your own round when the group next door is crushing it.
3
Playing smarter, not harder
Why a 7-iron to the middle of the green beats a 56° wedge at the pin. Course management in plain English.
4
When you break 100, come back to your profile
Flip your skill tier from Developing to Player. The stats-and-handicap view of the portal becomes useful. Until then, keep doing what's working.

When you're ready to graduate.

The portal has a whole other layer for players who want to track fairways, greens, putts, scrambling percentages, and move handicap indexes. It's great — and it's waiting for you.

You don't need any of that until you're consistently breaking 100. Before then, the stats are noisy and the benchmarks are discouraging. After — they're exactly what you want. When the time comes, switch your skill tier to Player in your profile, and the dashboard rearranges itself.
