Ball Flight Laws — what actually controls your shot

The single most important concept to understand before you ever take another lesson.

For decades, golf instruction taught the wrong thing. The old rule said the club's swing path determined where the ball started, and the face angle determined the curve. Sound familiar? It should — it's still being taught in countless lesson bays around the world. The problem is, it's wrong.

Modern launch monitors — TrackMan, GCQuad, FlightScope — proved the opposite is true. Roughly 85% of where the ball starts is determined by the club face angle at impact. The path determines how the ball curves relative to that starting line. This is sometimes called the "new" ball flight laws (though they're not new — physics has always worked this way; we just couldn't measure it until the technology existed).

THE LAW

Face controls start direction. Path controls curve. Period.

The four-shot map

Every full shot you hit fits into one of four buckets, defined by the relationship between face and path at impact:

Face vs. TargetPath vs. FaceBall Flight
Face open to targetPath is left of facePush-slice (starts right, curves more right)
Face open to targetPath is right of facePush-draw (starts right, curves back to target)
Face closed to targetPath is right of facePull-hook (starts left, curves more left)
Face closed to targetPath is left of facePull-fade (starts left, curves back to target)
Face squarePath matches faceStraight (rare in real golf)

Why this rewires your practice

Knowing the laws changes what you fix. A slice isn't caused by an over-the-top path — it's caused by a face that's open to the path at impact. The over-the-top motion is often a compensation for an open face. Cure the face first, and the path tends to fix itself.

Same logic applies to your pulls, pushes, hooks, and that mystery shot that goes nowhere near where you aimed. Stop guessing. Identify where the ball started (face), where it curved (path-to-face), and you've diagnosed the swing in two seconds.

Lesson tee shorthand. If a shot starts left of target, the face was pointing left at impact — full stop. Where it curves from there tells you what your path did relative to that face. Train your eye for this and you'll never need a launch monitor to know what your swing produced.

Why the Club Face Is King

85% of where the ball starts. 100% of why your scorecard looks the way it does.

Take any tour pro and any 20-handicap, and the single biggest difference between them isn't club path, swing speed, or even strike location. It's face control at impact. The pros put the face within ±2° of where they intended; amateurs miss by ±10° regularly. That single variable — face angle — is the source of most of your miss-hit pain.

What controls the face

Three things, in this order of impact:

  1. Grip. The number-one face controller. A grip that's too weak (hands rotated toward the target) opens the face at impact. Too strong (hands rotated away) closes it.
  2. Lead wrist position at the top. A cupped lead wrist (extended) opens the face. A flat or bowed lead wrist (flexed) closes it. Look at Dustin Johnson and Jon Rahm — their lead wrists are bowed at the top, which is why they don't hit big slices.
  3. Forearm rotation through impact. The forearms have to rotate around the body for the face to square. Many slicers stop their rotation at impact ("holding off" the release), which leaves the face open.

How to test your face control

Put a piece of impact tape (or foot powder spray) on your driver face. Hit ten balls at a target. If the strike marks are scattered all over the face, your strike location is the issue. If the marks are tight but the ball doesn't go where you aimed, your face angle is the issue. The fix is different for each.

PRACTICE CUE

You can hit a great shot with a bad path. You can't hit a great shot with a bad face.

Drill: the gate drill

Set two tees in the ground about 3 feet ahead of your ball, just wider than your driver head. Hit balls trying to send them through the gate. If the ball misses left or right of the gate, the face was off — you'll feel it instantly. Five minutes of this rewires face awareness faster than any verbal cue I can give.

Why You Slice the Ball — every reason, in order

Six causes, ranked from most-likely to least-likely. Diagnose top-down and stop the bleeding.

A slice is a ball that curves hard right (for a right-handed player). It's the most common miss in amateur golf because almost every cause produces the same symptom: the face is open relative to the path at impact. Here's the full list of culprits, in the order I look for them on the lesson tee.

1. Weak grip (most common, by a mile)

Set up to your driver and look at your lead hand (left for righties). You should see at least 2 to 3 knuckles. If you see only 1 — or worse, your "V" between thumb and forefinger points at your chin instead of your trail shoulder — your grip is too weak. The face will come back to address position at impact, which is open relative to your path. Fix: rotate your lead hand clockwise on the grip until you see 2-3 knuckles.

2. Open face at the top of the backswing

If your lead wrist cups at the top (back of the lead hand bends backward), the face is pointing skyward relative to where it should be. Square that wrist or even bow it slightly — DJ-style — and your face arrives more square at impact.

3. Heel strikes

This is sneaky. A ball struck off the heel of the driver imparts left-to-right sidespin even with a square face — it's called gear effect (more in the Science section). A heel strike combined with a fade swing turns into a banana slice. The fix is strike location: check your driver face for heel marks (toe-side wear is fine; heel-side wear is destroying you). Stand a quarter-inch farther from the ball, ball off the lead heel, and feel the toe of the club exit toward the target.

4. Over-the-top swing path

Coming from outside-in cuts across the ball, sending it left of where the face points then curving right. Notice this is #4, not #1 — most over-the-top motions are compensations for an open face. Fix the face first, and the path naturally moves more in-to-out.

5. Body open at impact

Hips and shoulders that race ahead of the arms leave the face open. Drill: hit half-swing shots feeling like your chest faces the ball at impact, not the target. You'll square the face dramatically faster.

6. Aim left to "compensate"

The classic slicer's death spiral: you slice, so you aim left. Aiming left makes your path more out-to-in, which adds more slice. Always aim square or slightly right of target while you're fixing the swing. Trust the work.

Diagnosis order matters. Don't skip to #4 (path) before checking #1-3 (face controllers). Eight out of ten slicers have a grip issue. Fix that and the slice often disappears in one bucket of balls.

How to Hit the Ball Higher

It's almost never about loft. It's about ball speed, spin, and angle of attack.

Trajectory is a function of three things at impact: launch angle, ball speed, and spin rate. Most amateurs think launch angle is everything — "I need to swing under it more." That's almost always wrong. Here's what actually moves your apex up.

Ball speed is the biggest lever

A ball traveling 130 mph reaches an apex 25-30 yards higher than the same ball at 100 mph, even with identical launch and spin numbers. The faster the ball, the longer it hangs in the air and the higher it climbs. If you want to hit it higher, you need to hit it harder — there's no getting around the physics.

Launch angle, properly understood

Launch angle is determined by your dynamic loft at impact (the actual loft on the face when it meets the ball, not the loft stamped on the sole) and your angle of attack (whether you're hitting up or down on the ball).

  • For a driver, you want a positive (upward) angle of attack — about +3° to +5° for most players. Tee the ball higher, ball forward in stance, and feel like your sternum stays behind the ball through impact. Hitting up on a driver adds ball speed AND launch — it's a free upgrade.
  • For irons, a slightly negative (downward) angle of attack is correct — you want to compress the ball. Trying to "lift" it will scoop it and rob you of compression and speed.

Spin: the secret variable

Two balls launched at the same angle and speed will fly different heights if their spin rates differ. Higher spin = higher peak height (it's lift, like an airplane wing). Driver spin should be 2,200-2,600 rpm for most players; mid-irons 6,000-7,000 rpm. Get on a launch monitor once and find your numbers — you can't optimize what you don't measure.

THE FORMULA

Higher trajectory = more speed + correct attack angle + appropriate spin. Strike it cleanly first; "lifting" never works.

What to actually do tomorrow

  1. Tee your driver 1.5 inches off the ground. Move the ball to your lead heel.
  2. Set your trail shoulder lower than your lead shoulder at address (slight tilt back).
  3. Feel like you "stay behind" the ball — sternum behind the ball at impact.
  4. Make a full, committed swing. The launch and apex will increase noticeably.

How to Stop Hitting Fat & Thin Shots

It's all the same problem: low-point control. Master that and both shots disappear.

A fat shot is a swing whose lowest point happens behind the ball. A thin shot is a swing whose lowest point happens past the ball. They look like opposite problems. They're actually the same problem — your swing's bottom is in the wrong place. Fix that one variable and both shots vanish.

What controls low point

The bottom of the swing arc is determined by where your lead shoulder (left for righties) is at impact. If your lead shoulder is over or ahead of the ball, the low point is at or past the ball — clean strike. If your lead shoulder is behind the ball (you've "hung back"), the low point is behind the ball — fat or thin (depending on how badly you've hung back).

The thing that moves your lead shoulder forward is weight pressure transferring to your lead foot in the downswing. Tour pros are at 80-90% lead-foot pressure at impact. Amateur fat-hitters are often at 50% or less.

The pressure plate drill

Take your address. Have a friend stand behind you. Take a slow practice swing and say out loud when your weight feels like it's on your lead foot. If you can't say it before impact, that's your bug.

Hit half-swing 9-iron shots feeling like your trail heel comes off the ground at impact. That's a wild exaggeration of forward shift, and it'll feel uncomfortable. Good. Hit ten balls. Now hit ten with normal feel — the ground contact will feel different. That's what a clean strike feels like.

Posture matters too

A round, slumped back at address rounds further at impact, dropping your lead shoulder down and behind the ball — fat city. Set up tall, hinge from your hips (not your waist), and feel your sternum point at the ball. Maintain that posture through impact.

Ball position is a quick win. If you're hitting it fat with irons, your ball is probably too far forward in your stance. Move it back to center (or one ball back of center for short irons). For most players, this single change cleans up half their fat shots.

Thin shots specifically

A thin shot (low, no spin, "skulled") is almost always a player who is trying not to hit it fat. They quit on the downswing, lift the head and arms, and catch the equator of the ball. The fix is the same: commit to the forward weight shift, trust the compression, and let the divot happen past the ball.

How to Build a Tour-Pro Tempo

3:1 — the same ratio whether you're Ernie Els or Justin Thomas. The number that matters.

Researchers at TaylorMade studied 100+ tour pros' swing tempos with high-speed video. The result: every single one had a 3:1 ratio between backswing time and downswing time. Whether the player swung quickly (Justin Thomas, ~21 frames at 60fps) or slowly (Ernie Els, ~28 frames), the ratio held. Three units of time going back, one unit coming down. Tempo isn't about speed — it's about ratio.

Why ratio matters more than speed

The 3:1 ratio is what allows the kinematic sequence (see Science section) to work properly. The downswing fires in the right order — hips, then torso, then arms, then club — only when the transition is patient. Rush the backswing-to-downswing transition and the sequence collapses. Result: hands and shoulders fire together, club gets stuck, and you throw it from the top.

How to install tempo

The simplest fix is the "1-2-3, 1" count:

  • Count 1-2-3 as you swing back, slowly
  • Count 1 on the downswing through impact
  • The first 1-2-3 takes about ¾ of a second; the final "1" takes about ¼ second

Practice this on the range with a wedge first — 30 balls counting out loud. Then move to mid-irons. Don't try it with driver until the 1-2-3, 1 feels automatic with shorter clubs.

The metronome alternative

Apps like Tour Tempo or any free metronome do the work for you. Set it to ~75 BPM (the average tour pro setting). The first beat is the start of your backswing, the next beat is the top, and the third beat is impact. If your swing is too fast or slow, adjust by ±5 BPM until it feels natural.

DAILY TOUCH

Two minutes of metronome swings before you hit balls beats 50 swings without it. Tempo is the foundation everything else gets built on.

What you'll feel

Players who fix their tempo report better contact within the first session. The reason is simple: tempo gives the body time to sequence properly. The arms drop into the slot naturally, the hands get to the right place, and the face squares without manipulation. Tempo isn't a swing thought — it's the framework around all your swing thoughts.
