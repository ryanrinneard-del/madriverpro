A Component-Based Model of the Golf Swing.

The most misunderstood golf book ever written — translated.

From the coach

I've picked up The Golfing Machine three times and put it down three times. It reads like the operating manual for a piece of industrial equipment — which, in a way, is exactly what Homer Kelley intended. Kelley was an engineer, not a golf teacher, and the book he published in 1969 isn't a swing method. It's a catalog of every legal component of a golf swing, organized like a periodic table.

That's the first thing to understand, and nobody tells you up front: there is no "Kelley swing." The book is a reference for instructors to build a swing that fits a specific player. Ben Doyle used it to coach Bobby Clampett to world amateur number one. Mac O'Grady swore by it. Bryson DeChambeau built a career around one of its patterns. But none of those three swing the same way — they all just assembled valid choices from Kelley's catalog.

This primer is for recreational players who tried to read the book and bounced off. Six chapters. I've translated the five or six concepts worth stealing from TGM into plain English, and I've flagged the places where careful readers disagree so you know where to double-check. Read it and you'll understand what Kelley was actually arguing — more than most people who own the book.

The concepts that matter: hitters versus swingers, the three imperatives, the four pressure points, and plane lines without the geometry textbook. At the end, a practical takeaway about how to use TGM without becoming a TGM nerd.

— Ryan

Chapter One

What Kelley was actually arguing

Why TGM isn't a swing method — and what it actually is.
›

Here's the thing nobody tells you before you open The Golfing Machine for the first time: Homer Kelley wasn't writing a swing method. He was writing a catalog. If you read it as a method — like McLean's 8-Step Swing or Leadbetter's The Golf Swing — you'll spend 400 pages looking for "the answer" and come out frustrated, because Kelley never gives you one. He gives you every answer, laid out like parts in a machinist's catalog, and leaves you to assemble the ones that fit you.

The key idea. Kelley was an engineer who catalogued every legal component of a golf swing and showed how they fit together. There is no "Kelley swing." Every valid swing is an assembly of choices from his catalog — and infinitely many valid swings exist.

What Kelley actually said

The book organizes the golf swing into 10 basic components (grip, stance, plane, position, stroke, power, motion, etc.), each of which contains sub-components. By the time you reach the last chapter there are hundreds of individual variations catalogued. Each variation is a valid choice. Any combination of valid choices, assembled coherently, produces a swing that works.

“A golf swing is whatever a golfer does to move the clubhead from address to impact. The Golfing Machine categorizes the valid ways to do that. It does not prescribe one.”

This is why the book reads the way it does. Kelley wrote in the voice of an engineer because he was writing a reference manual, not a teaching text. His prose is dense because it has to be unambiguous — an instructor reading the book needs to know exactly which component is which, with no interpretation. It's closer to an IKEA assembly manual than a golf instruction book.

Why it's hard to read

Kelley invented precise vocabulary for components that previously had no names — or had a dozen different names across different teachers. A typical sentence might read: "The stroke pattern is the geometric manifestation of the chosen power package, plane, and hand-action trace path." That's not bad writing. That's an engineer being precise. But if you don't know what "power package" or "trace path" means — and you don't, because Kelley invented the terms — it's incomprehensible.

The dense vocabulary is also why TGM attracted a cult following. Once you learn Kelley's terms, you can talk about golf with a precision nobody else has. That precision is intoxicating for a certain kind of student. It's also why most people quit — because learning the vocabulary is a project unto itself, separate from actually improving your golf swing.

Common misunderstanding

"TGM is a swing method. If I read it carefully, I'll learn The Kelley Swing."

No such thing. Kelley never taught a swing. He documented the swing, as a system, the way a biologist documents an ecosystem. You can't copy his swing because he didn't have one to copy.

Practical takeaway

Read TGM as a menu, not a manual. When you see a concept that resonates — a pressure point, a plane, a motion type — circle it and try it at the range. When you hit a chapter that feels like engineering homework, skip it. The book becomes useful the moment you stop trying to read it cover-to-cover.

The biggest fork in Kelley's menu is whether you're a Hitter or a Swinger. That single choice determines most of what comes after.

Chapter Two

Hitters vs. swingers

Kelley's most useful idea. Two valid ways to deliver the club. Pick one.
›

If you take away one thing from The Golfing Machine, take this. Kelley identified two fundamentally different ways the human body can deliver a golf club to a ball. Both work. Neither is better. But they are not the same motion, and most amateurs are stuck somewhere between the two — which is why their swings feel inconsistent.

The key idea. A Swinger uses centrifugal force — the body pulls the arms, the arms pull the hands, the clubhead trails behind and whips through at the bottom. A Hitter uses muscular thrust — the trail arm actively pushes the club into the ball, like throwing a punch. Both deliver the clubhead. They do it through completely different mechanisms.

What each one looks like

Swingers look effortless. The action flows. The clubhead appears to be trailing the body all the way down, then catches up at impact through pure momentum. The finish is long, balanced, held — you can picture the tour-pro pose. Rory McIlroy, Bobby Jones, Fred Couples, Sam Snead are often described this way.

Hitters look muscular. There's a visible thrust from the trail arm through impact. The action happens at the ball, not through it — the peak effort is right at the moment of contact. The finish is often abbreviated or less balanced, because the work is done once the ball is gone. Mac O'Grady (a famous TGM instructor) and, in some readings, Bryson DeChambeau, fit the hitter pattern.

Coach's verification note
Applying "hitter" or "swinger" labels to specific tour players is a favorite TGM-adherent pastime and the labels get argued over constantly. The point here isn't that any specific pro is definitively one or the other — it's that the two patterns exist and are visibly different. Film both and you'll see it.

How to tell which one you are

You're probably already one or the other by nature. A few indicators:

  • If you try to "swing easier" and lose significant distance, you're likely a hitter — your body generates speed through muscular effort, and removing effort removes speed.
  • If you try to "hit it harder" and the shots get wild and off-line, you're likely a swinger — your body generates speed through rhythm and flow, and adding effort breaks the rhythm.
  • Background matters: people who come from throwing sports (football, baseball, shot put) or lifting tend toward hitter. People from rotation sports (tennis, hockey, dance) tend toward swinger.
  • Grip pressure preference: hitters usually want a firmer grip, swingers a softer grip. Not always — but often.
“Most amateurs are stuck because they're half-hitter, half-swinger. They swing easy because the magazines tell them to — but their body wants to hit. So they get neither the flow of a swinger nor the power of a hitter.”
Common misunderstanding

"Hitters hit the ball. Swingers swing at the ball. The vocabulary is the same thing."

Both strike the ball. The difference is the source of the speed. Swingers use centrifugal force (the trail hand is soft and passive, trailing behind). Hitters use muscular push (the trail arm is active, thrusting the club). You can't do both at the same time — the hand actions contradict each other.

Practical takeaway

Pick one. Commit. Spend a full range session experimenting. Hit 20 balls as a pure swinger (soft trail hand, rhythm, long finish). Hit 20 as a pure hitter (firmer trail hand, thrust through impact, abbreviated finish). One will feel obvious. That's your pattern. Stop trying to be the other one.

Whichever you pick, Kelley said all valid swings share three non-negotiable truths at impact. Those are the Three Imperatives.
3
Chapter Three

The three imperatives

The three things every valid golf swing must do at impact. No exceptions.
›

This is Kelley's single most-cited contribution to golf instruction. Regardless of whether you're a hitter or a swinger, regardless of whether your swing is upright or flat, short or long, fast or slow — three things must be true at impact, or your shots will be inconsistent. These aren't style preferences. They're physics.

The three imperatives at impact.

1. A flat lead wrist. The back of your lead wrist (left wrist for a right-handed player) is flat or slightly bowed — not cupped up. This is what squares the clubface.

2. Clubhead lag pressure point maintained. Specifically, the trail hand's index finger keeps pressure on the grip all the way through impact. This is the lag that stores and delivers power.

3. A straight plane line. For a window around impact, the clubhead travels along a line parallel to the target. Not swinging across it, not wandering.

Why these three, specifically

Kelley didn't pick these three arbitrarily. Each one controls a specific variable that determines where the ball goes:

  • Flat lead wrist controls clubface. If the lead wrist is cupped at impact, the clubface is open and the ball goes right (for a right-handed player). Bowed, and it closes down the face.
  • PP3 pressure controls clubhead lag. Maintain the pressure from the trail index finger, and the clubhead trails the hands through impact — meaning the shaft leans forward, the loft is de-lofted, and the energy stored in the downswing is delivered to the ball. Lose the pressure, and the clubhead throws past the hands early: "casting." Fat shots, thin shots, loss of distance.
  • Straight plane line controls direction. If the clubhead is traveling parallel to the target when it hits the ball, the ball goes toward the target. Swing across that line and you get pulls, pushes, slices, hooks.
“Every valid golf swing ever hit — Nelson, Hogan, Jones, Woods, McIlroy, Sorenstam, every pro in every era — has these three things true at impact. Styles differ. Impact doesn't.”

What each one looks like when an amateur gets it wrong

Most recreational players fail on imperative #1 (flat lead wrist). The wrist breaks down — flips upward — at or just before impact. The result is the classic "scoop": the clubface opens, the loft adds, and the ball either goes weak-right or gets topped / hit thin. Watch any amateur pull a phone out at the range and ask them to video a 7-iron from face-on. Seven out of ten will show cupping at impact.

Imperative #2 failures show up as casting: the wrists unhinge too early in the downswing, the clubhead races past the hands, and by impact the player has no lag left to deliver. The shaft points backward (lean is gone), loft is added, ball balloons.

Imperative #3 failures show up as direction inconsistency: pulls, pushes, pull-hooks, push-fades. The clubface might be square, but the clubhead is traveling across the target line at impact.

Common misunderstanding

"The three imperatives = three positions I need to hold throughout my swing."

No. They're truths about impact only. Everything before impact (grip, takeaway, top, transition) and everything after (release, follow-through, finish) can vary enormously between valid swings. Kelley's point is that the imperatives are the destination — the impact condition. How you get there is the component choice.

Practical takeaway

If you fix nothing else in your golf swing for the rest of your life, fix the flat lead wrist at impact. It is the single biggest miss in amateur golf. Film yourself from face-on. Freeze at impact. If your lead wrist is cupped, that's your project. Everything else can wait.

To feel imperative #2 (lag pressure), you need to know what Kelley meant by "pressure points." Four of them, and PP3 is the one that changes your swing the most.

Chapter Four

The four pressure points

The four contact points that tell you what the club is doing in real time.
›

Kelley's most physical, most feel-based concept. The pressure points are the specific places where your hands contact the club — and where your chest contacts your lead upper arm. They're how you feel what the clubhead is doing without having to think about it. Once you feel the right pressure points, you can diagnose your own swing in real time.

The four pressure points.

PP1 — The heel pad of your trail hand, pressing against the side of the grip. The "aim" pressure. When PP1 is firm, the clubhead is pointing where you've aimed it.

PP2 — The base of your lead hand's index finger, pressing against the grip. Often called "the last finger on the club." Critical for swingers especially.

PP3 — The trail hand's index finger, pressing against the grip. THIS IS THE BIG ONE. Kelley's "clubhead lag pressure point."

PP4 — Your chest / sternum, pressing against your lead upper arm. The "pivot pressure" — your body and lead arm are connected as one unit.

What to feel, in plain English

Pick up a club and grip it normally. Now focus on four specific sensations:

  • PP1 — push the heel pad of your trail hand down into the top-side of the grip. You'll feel the grip pressing back.
  • PP2 — curl the base of your lead-hand index finger around and up under the grip, so the finger presses into the handle. You'll feel your lead hand "hooked" onto the club.
  • PP3 — feel the side of your trail-hand index finger pressing into the grip (not just hooked around it, but actively pressing). This is the critical one.
  • PP4 — feel your lead arm resting against the side of your chest. Your body can't turn without your arm going with it.
“If you only feel one thing, feel PP3. Maintain that trail-index-finger pressure from the top of your backswing all the way to just after impact. Casting disappears. Lag appears. Distance goes up.”

What PP3 does for your swing

Of the four, PP3 is the one worth obsessing over. The pressure from your trail-hand index finger against the grip is the direct physical evidence of clubhead lag. If that pressure is there, the clubhead is trailing your hands (good). If you lose that pressure — if it goes slack — the clubhead has passed your hands and you're casting (bad).

The reason PP3 matters so much is that it translates Kelley's abstract "imperative" into a sensation you can actually feel. You don't have to think about shaft lean or wrist angles. You just feel whether that one finger is still pressing. That's it.

Coach's verification note
Different TGM sources describe PP1/PP2 with slightly different emphasis — some instructors focus on PP1 as the aim-of-the-face, others as the pure lag-support pressure for hitters. The descriptions above are a consolidated version of the most common framings. PP3 and PP4, by contrast, are described consistently across most TGM sources.
Common misunderstanding

"Pressure points are just another way of saying grip pressure."

They're not the same thing. Grip pressure is how hard you squeeze the whole club. Pressure points are specific contact points that give you feedback about what the club is doing. You can have a light overall grip pressure and still feel strong pressure-point feedback. In fact, that's the goal.

Practical takeaway

Hit 20 range balls focusing only on maintaining PP3 pressure from the top of your backswing through impact. Don't worry about anything else. Most amateurs will feel the pressure go slack at the transition from backswing to downswing — that's casting happening in real time. Once you feel PP3 stay firm from the top all the way to the ball, you've felt what Kelley was pointing at the whole time.

Now that you can feel the club, let's talk about where you're swinging it — Kelley's famously dense concept of "the plane."

Chapter Five

Plane lines in plain English

Kelley's obsession. The tilted circle your clubhead draws — without the geometry textbook.
›

Kelley wrote hundreds of pages on plane. It's the densest part of TGM and the place most readers quit. But the concept itself — stripped of the geometry-textbook language — is simpler than it sounds.

The key idea. A "plane" is just the tilted circle the clubhead traces as it swings around you. Imagine a door on hinges: the door swings through a specific tilted angle. That angle is the plane. Kelley's point is this: pick a plane, stay on it, don't wander between planes. When you wander, the clubface direction at impact becomes random.

The three planes Kelley referenced most

There are a lot of planes in TGM, but for a primer the three most-cited are:

  • Turned shoulder plane. Draw a line across your shoulders at address. Extend it out over the ball. That tilted line, extended, traces the highest of the three planes. Often used by tall, flexible players on the backswing.
  • Elbow plane. Defined by your trail elbow. When the trail elbow folds during the backswing, it points down toward a specific spot. The clubshaft aligning with that trail-elbow angle traces the elbow plane. A common delivery plane — meaning many players are on the elbow plane during the downswing even if they went up on a different plane.
  • Hands plane. Traced by the butt of the club (your hands) as they move around your body. Flatter than the shoulder plane for most players.

You don't need to memorize these three. The concept you need is: a plane is a tilted circle, and you're on some plane at every moment of the swing. The question is whether you stay on it — or drift onto a different one — during the most important half-second, impact.

“Kelley allowed multi-plane swings — you can go up on one plane and come down on another. But he insisted the transition has to be deliberate. Most amateurs wander between planes unconsciously, and that's why their direction is random.”

What "plane line" actually means

A plane is a tilted circle. The "plane line" is the straight line on the ground where that tilted circle would intersect the ground if you extended it. For most shots, where the plane is tilted over the target line, the plane line = the target line (or a line parallel to it).

Kelley's imperative #3 — "straight plane line" — just means this: for the window around impact (a few feet before and after), the clubhead should travel parallel to the target line. Not swinging across it (that's over-the-top or in-to-out). Not wandering up or down (that's lifting or chopping).

Coach's verification note
Plane terminology is where even long-time TGM readers diverge. Some modern instructors (Leadbetter, Hardy in The Plane Truth, etc.) took Kelley's multi-plane idea and simplified it into the "one-plane vs. two-plane" framework — which is not the same as Kelley's original model, though it draws on it. If you've read The Plane Truth and are comparing, the vocabulary overlaps but the frameworks are distinct.

How to check your own plane

You don't need a launch monitor. You need a phone:

  • Film yourself from down-the-line — behind you, looking toward the target.
  • Freeze the video at halfway back — when the club is parallel to the ground in the backswing.
  • Draw an imaginary line from the butt of the club through your trail elbow, back toward you.
  • Where does it point? At your trail hip = on a workable plane. At the sky = too upright (off-plane high). At the ground = too flat (off-plane low).

This isn't a full Kelley plane analysis — it's the amateur's version. But it'll tell you within one video whether your swing is on a reasonable plane or whether that's a piece of your game worth working on.

Common misunderstanding

"On-plane means the club stays on one shaft-plane the whole swing — the way Moe Norman or modern one-plane swingers do it."

That's the one-plane model popularized by Jim Hardy and others, not Kelley's model. Kelley allowed multi-plane swings — you can transition from one plane to another during the swing. What he required is that the transition be deliberate and that the clubhead be on a straight plane line through the impact window.

Practical takeaway

Film one swing a month from down-the-line and run the halfway-back check. That's 90% of the plane awareness you need. If the club is pointing somewhere reasonable (trail hip, trail elbow), you're on a workable plane. If it's pointing at the sky or the ground, that's your project for the month.

Chapter Six

Using TGM without becoming a TGM nerd

Steal the concepts. Skip the cult. Five things worth keeping from all 400 pages.
›

There's a particular type of golfer who reads TGM three times, tattoos "PP3" on their forearm, and spends the next eighteen months obsessing over plane geometry and pressure-point taxonomy instead of playing golf. Don't be that golfer. The book is useful. The cult is not. The whole point of this primer is to let you take what's valuable from Kelley and leave the rest alone.

The rule. Steal the concepts. Skip the vocabulary.

The five TGM concepts worth stealing

  1. Flat lead wrist at impact. The single highest-leverage fix in amateur golf. If you do nothing else from this book, film your impact and work on eliminating the cup.
  2. Hitter or Swinger — pick one. Stop trying to swing like the tour player who's wired the opposite way from you. Your body has a preferred pattern. Find it and commit.
  3. PP3 pressure. Feel the trail-index finger pressing into the grip. Maintain that pressure from the top of the backswing to just past impact. Casting disappears the moment you can actually feel this.
  4. Plane awareness. Film yourself down-the-line once a month. Check whether the club is on a workable plane at halfway-back. That's it. You don't need to become a geometry student.
  5. The assembly mindset. Kelley's deepest insight: your swing is an assembly of valid choices, not a single "correct" pattern. Stop looking for the swing. Look for your swing, built from valid components that fit your body, your history, and your goals.
“The best TGM students I've met aren't the ones who can recite all 216 components. They're the ones who know which five components apply to them — and practice those five.”

When to read the actual book

Pick up Kelley's book if:

  • You teach golf seriously and want a precise shared vocabulary with other instructors.
  • You're a low single-digit handicap (4 or better) and plateaued — meaning you need swing diagnosis at a resolution most golfers never reach.
  • You're an engineer or physicist who genuinely enjoys dense technical writing for its own sake. (Plenty of those in the TGM community — no judgment.)

Skip it if:

  • You're a recreational player trying to break 90, 80, or 75.
  • You already have a coach who knows what they're doing — trust the coach over the book.
  • You want to improve at golf rather than know golf. Those sound like the same thing but they are not. Most TGM readers end up knowing more than they improve.
Common misunderstanding

"TGM is outdated because modern biomechanics and TrackMan have moved past it."

Partly true, partly not. Kelley's specific geometry and mechanics have been refined by modern launch-monitor data — we know more now about impact dynamics than he did in 1969. But his framework — the idea that the swing is a catalog of components, that impact has non-negotiable truths, that pressure points give real-time feedback — still stands. Most modern instruction, knowingly or not, borrows from it.

The final takeaway

Kelley gave you a language for talking about your own swing — a language more precise than most players ever develop. Use the language when it helps. Put it down when it doesn't. Most importantly:

Go hit some golf balls.

That's the primer. Six chapters. Five concepts worth stealing. One book you don't have to read cover-to-cover to benefit from.

What to do with this.

The concepts here are worth more when you try them than when you read them. In order of highest-leverage to lowest:

  1. This week: Film your impact from face-on and check your lead-wrist position. If it's cupped, that's your project for the next month.
  2. Next range session: Hit 20 balls focusing only on PP3 — the trail-hand index-finger pressure, maintained from the top to past impact.
  3. Next week: Spend a session experimenting with hitter vs. swinger. 20 balls each, then pick the pattern that felt more natural and commit.
  4. Once a month: Film yourself down-the-line and run the halfway-back plane check.
  5. Only if you're still curious: Pick up the actual book. But only then.

About this primer

This is a primer — a plain-English translation of the highest-leverage concepts from Homer Kelley's The Golfing Machine (1969). It is not a replacement for Kelley's book, and it does not attempt to cover the full ~216-component catalog Kelley documented. The goal is to give a recreational player enough of the framework to recognize what Kelley was pointing at — and, if they want more, to read the original with a chance of actually finishing it.

Several callouts are marked with a Coach's verification note — flagging places where careful TGM readers disagree on specific emphasis, or where TGM's framework diverges from later systems (like the one-plane / two-plane model from The Plane Truth) that borrow from Kelley's work. If you notice something that reads wrong against your own understanding, mention it — the primer improves the more it's pressure-tested.

For the fuller treatment, Homer Kelley's The Golfing Machine is still in print. Ben Doyle's teaching archive, Mac O'Grady's work, and the community around the Authorized Instructor program are the best places to go deeper if the primer sparks a genuine curiosity.

Glossary of TGM terms

Hitter
A player who delivers the clubhead through muscular thrust from the trail arm. Active trail hand, often a firmer grip.
Swinger
A player who delivers the clubhead through centrifugal force. Passive trail hand, soft trailing action, long held finish.
The Three Imperatives
Kelley's three non-negotiables at impact: a flat lead wrist, maintained clubhead lag pressure point (PP3), and a straight plane line.
Flat Lead Wrist
At impact, the back of the lead wrist (left for a right-handed player) is flat or slightly bowed — not cupped upward.
Lag
The clubhead trailing behind the hands in the downswing. Evidence that energy is still stored for delivery. Opposite of "casting."
Casting
The clubhead passes the hands too early in the downswing. Lag is lost. Contact becomes thin/fat, loft is added, distance drops.
PP1 / PP2 / PP3 / PP4
The four pressure points. PP1 = trail-hand heel pad against grip. PP2 = base of lead-hand index finger against grip. PP3 = trail-hand index finger against grip (the lag pressure point). PP4 = chest/sternum against lead upper arm (pivot pressure).
Pressure Point (generally)
A specific point of contact that provides sensory feedback about what the clubhead is doing. Different from overall grip pressure.
Plane
The tilted circle the clubhead traces as it swings around the body. Think of a door on hinges.
Plane Line
The straight line on the ground where the swing's plane would intersect the ground if extended. For most shots, parallel to the target line.
Turned Shoulder Plane / Elbow Plane / Hands Plane
Three commonly-cited reference planes in Kelley's system, defined respectively by the line of the shoulders, the trail elbow, and the path of the hands.
Component / Basic Component
Kelley's name for an individual element of a golf swing. Ten "basic components" (grip, stance, plane, etc.) each contain multiple sub-components. Over 200 total. Any valid swing is an assembly of valid component choices.
Power Package
Kelley's term for the specific combination of body motion and hand/arm action a player uses to generate clubhead speed. Different power packages fit different bodies.
Authorized Instructor
An instructor trained in the TGM system and certified to teach it. Ben Doyle was the first; Mac O'Grady the most famous.
The Book
Shorthand among TGM adherents for Kelley's original 1969 text. Affectionate, slightly reverent, and occasionally insufferable.
