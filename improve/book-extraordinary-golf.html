From the coach

If Gallwey told you Self 1 was loud, Fred Shoemaker told you what to do about it on the practice tee. Extraordinary Golf is the book about practice that I've recommended more times than any other.

Shoemaker started something called the School for Extraordinary Golf in 1991, and the first thing he asked students to do was throw a club. Underhand. Toward a target. Every one of them did it gracefully — no instruction, no swing thoughts, just an athletic motion toward something they wanted to hit. Then he'd ask them to swing the club at a ball. And the same body, the same target, would turn into a mechanical, judgmental, anxious event. That gap — between the natural athletic motion and the “golf swing” — is the entire premise of the book.

The central distinction is this: most golfers are playing golf swing, not playing golf. They're trying to execute a series of mechanical checkpoints. Shoemaker's claim is that golf is a target sport, and target sports get easier when the brain orients to the target instead of to the motion. The body already knows how to move; it just needs the right job.

This primer is for the player who's frustrated by practice — who hits hundreds of range balls and isn't sure what they're getting out of it. Eight chapters: the toss-the-club story, what changes when the ball stops being a problem to solve, possibility vs prediction, how to practice with awareness instead of repetition, and what a “committed swing” actually means.

Shoemaker doesn't disagree with the technical books. He just points out that they're describing the destination, not the way there. The way there, he says, is awareness, possibility, and commitment — practiced in that order, every range session.

— Ryan

Chapter One

Why Extraordinary Golf still matters

The difference between playing golf and playing “golf swing.”
›

Walk down a driving range. Watch what golfers are actually doing. Most of them aren't aiming at anything. They're hitting balls. They're working on a swing thought. They're trying to feel something that someone told them to feel. The target is incidental — sometimes a flag, sometimes just “out there.”

The key idea. Shoemaker says this is the core problem: we've separated the act of swinging from the act of playing golf. Golf is a target sport. The brain is wired beautifully for target sports. But when you make the swing itself the target — positions to hit, feels to achieve — you shut off the very system that would make you good at the actual sport.

Playing golf vs playing golf-swing

A player who's playing golf is oriented to the target. They see the shot they want, they commit to a swing that fits it, they pull the trigger, they walk after the ball. A player who's playing golf-swing is oriented to the motion. They're thinking about positions before, during, and after the shot — the target is just where the ball happens to go when the swing was “right.”

“Most golfers are practicing the wrong sport.”

That's not Shoemaker quoting Shoemaker. That's how he tends to describe it. The whole book is about how to practice the right one.

Chapter Two

Toss the club

Shoemaker's most famous exercise. What it shows about the swing you already have.
›

Pick up a club. Hold it by the grip. Now toss it, underhand, out toward a target ten yards away. Try to land it near the target.

Almost every golfer does this gracefully on the first attempt. The body finds the rhythm, the weight transfer, the release. No instruction. No swing thoughts. No checkpoints. Just an athletic motion toward something you wanted to hit.

The key idea. The swing you produce when you toss a club is the swing your body has actually learned. It is target-oriented, weight-shifted, rhythmic, and committed. Then someone puts a ball in front of you, and that swing disappears — replaced by a careful, controlled, anxious version that's trying not to fail. The toss is the proof: the good swing is in there.

Why this exercise matters

Shoemaker isn't suggesting you start tossing clubs on the range (though you can, and should, occasionally). The exercise is a reference point. When your swing starts to feel mechanical, when you've lost rhythm, the toss is the closest thing to a reset button golf has. It tells your body: remember this. It's the same motion. Trust it.

How to use it at home

  • Pick a soft target ten yards away in the yard or driveway.
  • Toss a club underhand at it. Land it close.
  • Now pick the club up and make a normal swing in the air with the same intention — toward the same target, with the same feel.
  • Repeat ten times. That's the warm-up your body actually wants.
“The ball isn't the problem. The story you tell yourself about the ball is the problem.”

Chapter Three

The ball is not the problem

What happens when you stop trying to hit it and start trying to play with it.
›

Notice the language we use. We say we're going to “hit some balls.” Watch where your eyes go before a shot: straight to the ball, sitting there, daring you. The ball is the object of attention — and frequently the object of frustration.

The key idea. Shoemaker says the ball isn't the problem. The ball is a beautiful, neutral, perfectly still piece of equipment that does exactly what the clubface tells it to do. Treating it as an adversary — something you must defeat by hitting it — is what creates the tension and the over-control. Treat it as a partner: a stationary marker that lets you see whether your swing went to the target.

The mental shift

Try this on the next shot: don't think about hitting the ball. Think about swinging the club at the target. The ball happens to be in the way of where the club has to go. That's all. The ball will go to the target because the club went to the target. You don't have to make it happen.

This sounds like word games. It isn't. Your body responds completely differently to “hit it” than it does to “swing to the target.” One contracts. The other extends.

“The ball is a target indicator, not a target.”

Chapter Four

Possibility vs prediction

Playing for what could happen versus avoiding what might. The orientation that changes everything.
›

Stand on a tee with water down the right side. Two thoughts are available. Don't go in the water. Or: I want to start the ball over the left edge of the fairway and let it draw back to the middle.

The key idea. Shoemaker calls the first one prediction — you're predicting what might go wrong and trying to avoid it. The second one is possibility — you're describing what you want. Both are accurate descriptions of the situation. Only one helps you swing.

Why your body believes the possibility

Self 2 doesn't process negatives well. “Don't slice into the water” gets received as “slice into the water” with a confusing label on it. “Draw over the bunker” is a clean, executable instruction. Shoemaker's practice is: spend the pre-shot routine constructing the possibility, in detail — ball flight, landing area, rollout — and let prediction die on the vine.

The mid-shot tell

If you find yourself thinking the word “don't” at any point in your pre-shot routine, you're predicting. Back off. Re-construct the shot as a possibility. Then commit.

“You can't aim at ‘not in the water.’”

Chapter Five

Practice with awareness

Replace “drill until perfect” with “drill until informed.”
›

Mindless reps build callouses, not skill. Shoemaker's whole alternative is awareness-based practice: every ball is an experiment, and the goal isn't to repeat a motion but to gather information.

The key idea. A range session has three good questions: what am I noticing, what is the ball telling me, and what do I want to change for the next shot? When those three questions drive the session, fifty balls is plenty. When they don't, three hundred is wasted.

The Shoemaker range session

  • Warm up with the toss exercise or a few slow rhythmic swings — reconnect to the natural motion.
  • Pick an awareness target. One thing to notice for the next ten balls: tempo, grip pressure, balance, where the divot points.
  • Hit ten balls. Report what you noticed after each one. Don't try to fix anything.
  • Decide whether to keep that awareness target or switch. Switch deliberately.
  • End with five “play” balls. Random clubs, random targets, full routine, full commitment. No awareness target. Just play.

That's a complete session. Forty-five balls. More information than two hundred mindless ones.

“A range ball is a piece of information, or it's nothing.”

Chapter Six

The committed swing

A swing made on purpose, not in hope. The one decision before every shot.
›

Watch an amateur over a shot they're not sure about. Two waggles. Three looks. A small backswing pause. A hopeful decelerating hit. That swing was never decided. It was a guess executed slowly.

The key idea. A committed swing is a swing whose decision is fully made before it starts. The shot shape, the target, the club, the tempo — all settled. The only thing left is the doing. Commitment is what makes the body free; uncertainty is what makes the body tight.

How to commit

Shoemaker's rule is simple: don't begin the swing until you've fully described the shot to yourself. If you can't describe it — what shape, what flight, what landing spot — you don't get to swing yet. Back off, re-pick, re-describe.

The cost of half-committing

A half-committed swing is the worst swing in golf. It has the tension of trying and the timing of not-quite-going. You'd rather commit to the wrong shot than half-commit to the right one. The wrong shot, fully committed, often comes out fine. The right shot, half-committed, almost never does.

“Decide the shot, then swing the swing.”

Chapter Seven

Coaching yourself on the course

What to do when you can't take a lesson mid-round and the wheels are coming off.
›

You're playing a round. Somewhere around hole 5, the swing quietly walks off. Now what? Most players try to mid-round fix it — a new swing thought, a different grip pressure, a shorter takeaway. By hole 12 they've tried five things and none of them stuck.

The key idea. On the course, you can't change technique. You can only change awareness. Shoemaker's coaching rule for mid-round: stop trying to fix the swing. Pick one neutral thing to notice. Tempo, balance, grip pressure, the target. Let Self 2 sort it out while Self 1 has a job.

The three-step recovery

  1. Notice you're spiraling. Tightness in the hands, “don't” thoughts, fast pre-shot routines. Name it without judging it.
  2. Pick one awareness. Tempo is usually the best one in distress, because tempo is the first thing to go and the easiest to feel.
  3. Commit to a target. Conservative if needed. Possibility, not prediction. Swing.

You won't fix the round. You'll stabilize it. And that's a skill that pays off for thirty years.

“The way out of a bad stretch is awareness, not fixes.”

Chapter Eight

Where Extraordinary Golf fits

With Gallwey for the mind, Jacobs and Hogan for the swing — here's the seat at the table.
›

Think of this shelf as a small library of complementary voices. Each one teaches a different piece of what it actually takes to play.

  • Jacobs (Vol. 01) — diagnose the shot from the ball flight.
  • Hogan (Vol. 02) — build the fundamentals in order.
  • Gallwey (Vol. 06) — quiet Self 1 so Self 2 can deliver.
  • Shoemaker (Vol. 07) — practice in a way that builds real skill instead of grooved tension.
The key idea. Where Gallwey gave you the concepts (Self 1, Self 2, awareness), Shoemaker gave you the protocols — how to actually structure a range session, how to commit to a shot, how to coach yourself when the round turns. Gallwey teaches you to see. Shoemaker teaches you to practice.

How to use this book

Read it once cover to cover, then keep it near the practice bag. The chapter on awareness-based practice is one to revisit every spring as you reset your range routine for the season.

If you'd like to build a practice plan that uses both Shoemaker's awareness model and the technical fundamentals, that's exactly what a private lesson is for.

“Practice on purpose. Play on purpose. The rest is golf.”
Where to go from here

Three things to take to the range this week.

  1. Toss the club, three times. Before you hit a single ball, toss a club underhand at a target. Three tosses. Then make three swings with the same intention. Now you can start the session.
  2. Pick one awareness target, ten balls. Tempo, grip pressure, balance, or where the divot points. One. For ten balls. Don't try to fix anything. Just notice.
  3. End with five “play” balls. Random clubs, random targets, full routine, full commitment. No awareness target. Just play. This is the bridge from range to course.

Glossary

Playing golf vs playing golf-swing
Shoemaker's central distinction. Target-oriented play (“swing the club at the target”) vs motion-oriented play (“hit the positions”).
Toss the club
Shoemaker's signature warm-up. Tossing a club underhand at a target reveals the natural, target-oriented athletic motion every golfer already has.
The committed swing
A swing whose decision — shape, target, club, tempo — is fully made before motion starts. The opposite of a half-committed, hopeful swing.
Possibility vs prediction
Possibility describes what you want to happen (“draw to the centre”). Prediction describes what you're avoiding (“don't go in the water”). Possibility plays; prediction tightens.
Awareness-based practice
Practice driven by what you're noticing (tempo, grip pressure, balance) rather than by repetition of a fixed motion. Information per ball, not balls per hour.
Range session structure
Shoemaker's pattern: warm-up (toss/rhythmic), awareness block (ten balls with one focus), play block (five real shots with full commitment).
Self 2 (carried from Gallwey)
The athletic, non-verbal part of you that already knows how to swing. Shoemaker's exercises are designed to give Self 2 room to work.
